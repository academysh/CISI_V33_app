INTRODUCTION

Why a special regime is needed for retail clients

Running through the entire UK regulatory system is one consistent idea: retail clients need stronger protection than professional clients and ECPs. They are, ultimately, consumers — ordinary people bringing their savings into the financial system, often without understanding all the nuances. When a financial firm fails or a product turns out to be unsuitable, it is retail clients who suffer most.

Before 2023, retail client protection was built around two Principles for Businesses:

  • Principle 6 (Customers' interests): a firm must pay due regard to the interests of its customers and treat them fairly.
  • Principle 7 (Communications with clients): a firm must pay due regard to the information needs of its clients and communicate fairly/clearly/not misleadingly.

Plus an operational tool — the FTOC (Fair Treatment of Customers) initiative with 6 outcomes. But after the financial crisis and a series of scandals (PPI, London Capital & Finance, mini-bonds), it became clear this was not enough. Firms were formally complying with the rules, yet retail clients were still receiving poor outcomes.

That is why the FCA launched Consumer Duty in 2023 — a new, higher standard expressed in Principle 12. For firms within the scope of Consumer Duty, Principles 6 and 7 are disapplied — replaced by the much tougher P12.

Structure of the topic — two parallel regimes
In scope of Consumer Duty (firms dealing directly with retail): Principle 12 + 3 cross-cutting rules + 4 outcomes.
Out of scope of Consumer Duty (wholesale firms, exemptions): FTOC + Principles 6 and 7 + client's best interests rule + 6 outcomes.
6.1

Consumer Duty — general overview

Consumer Duty is an FCA initiative that sets higher and clearer standards of consumer protection in financial services and requires firms to put customers' needs first.

▸Structure of Consumer Duty

Consumer Duty consists of four components:

  1. Principle 12 — high-level principle: "A firm must act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers."
  2. Three cross-cutting rules — general rules applying to all activities.
  3. Four outcomes — specific areas where the FCA wants to see results.
  4. Formal guidance — FCA guidance on how to apply all of this.

▸Implementation dates

The Consumer Duty rules came into effect in stages:

Date What takes effect
2023 For new and existing products / services
31 July 2024 For closed products and services — products only available to existing customers where no new investments can be made, or services that are being wound down
Exam — don't confuse the dates
2021 — consultation was published.
2022 — final rules published but not yet in effect.
2023 — rules in force for new/existing products.
31 July 2024 — rules extended to closed products.
The exam most often asks the year of taking effect — the correct answer is 2023 (for open products).
What "act to deliver good outcomes" means
Crucially important: the standard is formulated in terms of outcomes, not actions. Simply "following the rules" is not enough — a firm must actually deliver good outcomes and understand whether they are being delivered. Firms need to consider the characteristics and objectives of their customers, and also understand and provide evidence that those outcomes are being met.
6.2

Scope — to whom Consumer Duty applies

▸Basic rule

Consumer Duty applies to retail customers — including prospective customers.

Applicability of Consumer Duty
✓ IN scope:
• Retail customers (existing)
• Prospective retail customers
• Any retail customer in any sourcebook
  (COBS, BCOBS, ICOBS, MCOB, etc.)

✗ OUT of scope:
• ECPs
• Professional clients (generally)
• Wholesale activities
• Specific exemptions
The "retail customer" definition varies
This is a key nuance: the definition of retail customer depends on the specific sourcebook. One criterion applies in COBS, another in BCOBS (banking conduct), yet another in ICOBS (insurance), a fourth in MCOB (mortgages). For each product context, look up the retail customer definition in the relevant sourcebook.

▸Wholesale exclusions

Some activities are excluded from Consumer Duty as "wholesale". One key number:

  • Activities in non-retail financial instruments with a minimum denomination / investment of £50,000 — are excluded from Consumer Duty.
Exam number
£50,000 minimum denomination for non-retail financial instruments = exclusion from Consumer Duty. You may be tempted to pick £10K / £25K / £100K — all are wrong.

▸Agent as client

Firms acting under the "agent as client" rules (see Section 3.2) must ascertain whether retail customers are at the end of the distribution chain, and whether they (the firm) can determine or materially influence the outcomes for those retail customers. If yes — Consumer Duty applies proportionately.

▸Misclassification prohibition

The FCA has made clear: deliberate misclassification of clients (for example, classifying a retail client as professional to avoid Consumer Duty) is not permitted. Both manufacturers and distributors in the distribution chain carry their share of the responsibility.

6.3

The three Cross-Cutting Rules

The FCA cross-cutting rules are basic requirements applying to everything the firm does for retail clients. There are three:

▸Rule 1: Act in good faith towards retail customers

  • A standard of conduct characterised by honesty, fair and open dealings.
  • The firm's actions must be consistent with the reasonable expectations of retail customers.
  • Firms should consider this rule at the design stage of a product or service — not just at the point of sale.

▸Rule 2: Avoid foreseeable harm to retail customers

  • Firms must ensure that retail customers understand and accept the inherent risk of products.
  • Firms do NOT have responsibility to protect customers from ALL foreseeable harm — an important clarification.
  • Firms must be clear and transparent.
Common misconception
Some think that Consumer Duty obliges firms to fully protect clients from any possible losses. This is NOT the case. The client still bears the inherent risk of investments — and can lose money. The firm is responsible for avoiding foreseeable harm (for example, designing a product that is clearly unsuitable for the target market), but not for guaranteeing positive returns.

▸Rule 3: Enable and support retail customers to pursue their financial objectives

  • The conclusion firms reach depends on the products/services requested or bought by retail customers.
  • Firms are NOT responsible for customers' decisions — clients make their own choices. But the firm must be clear and transparent when providing material and information.

▸General applicability of cross-cutting rules

For each cross-cutting rule, the firm must assess and take into account the financial understanding (knowledge and awareness) of the target market and provide clear and transparent support where necessary.

6.4

The four Consumer Duty Outcomes — the key construct

This is the most exam-heavy part of the section. You need to know all 4 outcomes, what each one means, and how they apply.

OUTCOME 1
Products and Services
Products and services are designed for the needs of an identified target market and do not cause foreseeable harm
OUTCOME 2
Price and Value
Price is proportionate to the benefit received by the client — fair value
OUTCOME 3
Consumer Understanding
Communications are clear and allow clients to make informed decisions
OUTCOME 4
Consumer Support
Appropriate service throughout the relationship — for the full life of the relationship

▸Outcome 1: Products and Services

The FCA expects that all products and services for customers are designed to meet the needs and objectives of the target group.

Consumer Duty interacts with the requirements in PROD (Product Intervention and Product Governance Sourcebook) regarding manufacturers and distributors.

Role Responsibilities
Product providers (manufacturers) • Ensure design of the product/service for the specific needs of an identified target market
• Carry out regular reviews of product performance
Product distributors • Distribute/market the product focused on the identified target market
• Take reasonable steps to prevent the product being offered to customers outside the intended target market

Where multiple firms are involved in the distribution chain, each firm takes responsibility proportionately according to its ability to influence product design, operation, and distribution.

▸Outcome 2: Price and Value

The FCA does not regulate pricing directly. But it expects firms to offer products/services that represent fair value.

  • Fair value does NOT mean all retail consumers will achieve the same outcomes. Those who shop around gain confidence — because products will match their needs and represent better value than an existing provider.
  • Firms are not required to price the same for new and existing retail customers. But they must be able to demonstrate that any discounts for new customers are clearly communicated to both groups so that both can receive fair value.
  • The price of products must represent a "fair" value for customers in the target market / group.
  • Value is not just the price paid by the customer for a product/service: the firm must ensure there is a reasonable relationship between price paid and benefit received by the customer.
Key exam mistake
"Fair value" does NOT = cheapest price. Firms may charge premium prices if justified by the benefit to the customer. Also — the FCA does NOT pre-approve pricing. The notion that the firm must "set the lowest price in the market" is wrong.
Example: premium wealth management
A large firm charges a 2% AMC for its discretionary portfolio — above the market average. This is not automatically a breach. If the firm provides individualised service, high-quality portfolio managers, detailed reporting, and additional services worth the difference, fair value can be demonstrated. The key is the relationship between price and benefit.

▸Outcome 3: Consumer Understanding

Firms must communicate with customers in a consistent manner so they can make informed decisions about purchasing financial products or services.

The FCA expects firms to provide the required information at the right time and presented clearly and easily understood, enabling customers to understand and assess:

  • The features of products and services
  • The likely costs, associated risks and benefits from specific products/services
  • Whether products/services meet their financial needs and objectives

Firms need to consider who the target market audience is and use that to design marketing material and information:

  • Firms should put themselves in the shoes of retail customers and sense-check that the information is pitched at the right level of detail and understanding.

▸Outcome 4: Consumer Support

Firms must provide an appropriate level of service that meets customers' needs throughout their relationship.

  • Communications must be fair, clear and not misleading, so clients can make informed decisions.
  • The firm must present information in a format understood by the customers — the firm needs to understand the "financial awareness" of the target market audience.
Example of breaching multiple outcomes
A firm designs an investment product with high fees and complex features that is difficult for most retail customers to understand. Which outcomes might be breached?

Potentially several at once:
• Products and Services — is the product designed for the target market's needs? Who is the target market?
• Price and Value — do the high fees represent fair value?
• Consumer Understanding — can customers actually understand these complex features?
A good example of how a breach often spans multiple outcomes.
6.5

Accountability — the new Conduct Rule and senior managers

▸New Conduct Rule 6 in SM&CR

As part of Consumer Duty, the FCA introduced a new Conduct Rule (under SM&CR) for firms in scope:

Conduct Rule 6
"You must act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers"

Where a firm is NOT in scope of Consumer Duty, Conduct Rule 6 does not apply.

▸COCON rules — the details

The FCA in COCON (Code of Conduct) rules has identified:

  • Acting in good faith — a standard of conduct characterised by honesty, fair and open dealing, and consistent with the reasonable expectations of retail customers.
  • Firms and their individuals must avoid foreseeable harm to retail customers.
  • Firms and their individuals must enable and support retail customers to pursue their financial objective.

▸Senior managers accountable

The FCA will hold senior managers accountable for implementation and ongoing compliance with their regulatory obligations under Consumer Duty — ensuring that it is embedded in the firm via the SM&CR.

Firms must therefore make sure that senior managers are clear about what they are accountable and responsible for, and how they ensure the firm complies with the Consumer Duty on an ongoing basis.

6.6

FTOC — Fair Treatment of Customers for out-of-scope firms

What about firms that are not in scope of Consumer Duty? This happens in two cases:

  • The firm does not deal directly with retail customers and is not able to materially impact good outcomes for them (for example, pure B2B investment banks).
  • The firm's products/services fall under one of the exemptions from Consumer Duty.

Such firms continue to be subject to the FTOC (Fair Treatment of Customers) initiative, underpinned by Principles 6 and 7.

▸The concept of fair treatment

The concept of fair treatment sits at the heart of the regulatory system. It is expressed in Principle 6 and in an important conduct of business rule:

The client's best interests rule
"A firm must act honestly, fairly and professionally in accordance with the best interests of its client."

▸Supervision and management information

An important part of the FCA's supervision of firms is assessing how they demonstrate fair treatment of clients. The firm's management culture must have the client's best interests at the heart of the firm.

Firms must gather sufficient management information (MI) to:

  • Monitor how well they adhere to these obligations
  • Identify where they may be failing
What is required to demonstrate FTOC compliance
Management information (MI) demonstrating fair treatment at all stages of the client relationship. Not a formal certificate from the FCA, not annual audit reports, not a policy on the website — it is specifically MI.

▸Principle 6 for out-of-scope firms

All firms regulated by the FCA and not in scope of Consumer Duty must adhere to Principle 6: firms must pay due regard to the interests of its customers and treat them fairly.

▸What the FTOC initiative raises

The FTOC initiative raises standards in how firms carry on their business. Specifically, FTOC:

  • Helps customers to fully understand the features, benefits, risks and costs of the financial products they buy.
  • Minimises the sale of unsuitable products by encouraging best practice before, during and after a sale.

▸The six FTOC Outcomes

The FTOC initiative defines six consumer outcomes:

  1. Consumers can be confident they are dealing with firms where fair treatment of customers is central to the corporate culture.
  2. Products and services marketed and sold in the retail market are designed to meet the needs of identified consumer groups and targeted accordingly.
  3. Consumers are provided with clear information and kept appropriately informed before, during and after the sale.
  4. Where consumers receive advice, the advice is suitable and takes account of their circumstances.
  5. Consumers are provided with products that perform as firms have led them to expect, and the associated service is of an acceptable standard.
  6. Consumers do not face unreasonable post-sale barriers imposed by firms to change product, switch provider, submit a claim or make a complaint.
Don't confuse: 4 vs 6 outcomes
Consumer Duty = 4 outcomes (Products+Services / Price+Value / Understanding / Support)
FTOC = 6 outcomes (culture / design / info / suitable advice / performance / no post-sale barriers)
This is a favourite exam trap — you must know both numbers and both structures.
6.7

Mixed business — when a firm serves both retail and non-retail

What about a firm whose activities and business model mean it engages with clients who are both in scope and out of scope of Consumer Duty?

Such firms must comply with the relevant requirements for the services and products they provide to the different types of customers/clients.

+ client's best interests rule

This presents operational challenges for firms that provide products and services to both retail and non-retail customers (i.e. professional clients).

▸Firm's discretion on extending Consumer Duty

Firms decide themselves whether to apply Consumer Duty standards to their business activities that are out of scope. The decision is based on:

  • Differences in operating standards
  • Procedures
  • Policies for the products and services they provide to all customers

In practice, many large firms opt to apply Consumer Duty principles across the whole business — this simplifies operational consistency and reduces the risk of inadvertent breaches.

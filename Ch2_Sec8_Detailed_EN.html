Chapter 2, Section 8 — Detailed Deep Dive
Product Disclosure and the Right to Cancel · Syllabus 2.12 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 8 governs two connected questions. First, product disclosure — what documents the firm must give the client so they understand what they are buying (KIID for UCITS, KFD for packaged products, PRIIPs KID for insurance-based and other packaged retail investments). Second, right to cancel — the client's right to change their mind and cancel the contract within a cooling-off period (14 or 30 days depending on the product). On the exam — 3-5 questions focused on specific numbers and tables.

8.1

Purpose and Nature of Disclosure Rules — why product disclosure exists

The main purpose of product disclosure rules is to ensure clients are provided with sufficient and relevant information to make a decision on whether to undertake an investment. This applies both to recommended investments and to those the client selects independently.

The logic of the rules
Suitability and Appropriateness (Section 7) provide procedural protection — ensuring the firm assesses suitability. Product disclosure is informational protection: the client receives a document setting out what the product actually is. Both layers work together.

Section 8.1 describes three types of key documents, each for a different product class:

KFD

8.1.1

KIID — Key Investor Information Document

Authorised Fund Managers (AFMs) managing UCITS schemes must produce a KIID.

The KIID must be fair, clear and not misleading and consistent with the relevant parts of the scheme's prospectus. It must provide investors with information about the essential characteristics of the UCITS scheme, enabling them to reasonably understand the nature and risks of the product and make informed investment decisions.

▸5 mandatory elements of the KIID

  1. (a) Identification of the scheme
  2. (b) Short description of investment objectives and investment policy
  3. (c) Presentation of past performance, or (where relevant) performance scenarios
  4. (d) Costs and associated charges
  5. (e) Risk and reward profile, including appropriate guidance and warnings about risks

▸3 exceptions — when a KIID is not required

  • Where another firm has agreed to prepare such a document
  • For certain types of schemes where information is already provided in sufficient detail in another document
  • For reinsurance and pure protection insurance contracts — special rules apply
Exam mnemonic
KIID = produced by AFMs for UCITS. Fair/clear/not misleading + consistent with prospectus. 5 elements: ID / objectives+policy / past performance / costs / risk+reward. 3 exceptions: another firm agrees / already in another document / reinsurance + pure protection.
Past performance vs performance scenarios example
An established UCITS fund with a 10-year history shows past performance — a bar chart of annual returns over the last 5-10 years. A newly launched fund with no history shows performance scenarios — moderate / favourable / unfavourable / stress scenarios of future returns.
8.1.2

KFD — Key Features Document

Product disclosure rules require the firm to prepare a disclosure document (KFD) in good time before it needs to, in respect of:

  • Each packaged product
  • Cash deposit ISA
  • Cash deposit Child Trust Fund (CTF)
  • Key features illustration for each packaged product the firm produces

Firms must provide particular information about the nature and risks of designated investments to clients for whom they conduct MiFID business. They must also do so for retail clients in relation to certain other business.

What is a packaged product
Not one specific product, but a category: life policies, personal pensions, unit trusts, investment trust savings schemes, ISAs, CTFs — anything that "packages" inner investments into a single product for retail. It is for these products that a KFD is required.
8.1.3

PRIIPs KID — Key Information Document

The regulation on Key Information Documents (KIDs) for Packaged Retail and Insurance-based Investment Products (PRIIPs) introduces a KID covering not only CIS but also other "packaged" investment products offered by banks or insurance companies.

A KID is a short document giving key facts to investors in a clear and understandable manner.

▸Aim of the PRIIPs legislation

To help investors better understand and compare the key features, risks, rewards and costs of different PRIIPs through a short, consumer-friendly KID.

▸UCITS and PRIIPs — exemption until 31 December 2026

UCITS already has its own KIID and KFD, as noted in 8.1.1 and 8.1.2. UCITS is subject to a 5-year exemption period following the implementation of PRIIPs. The extension period expires on 31 December 2026. After that date, UCITS will also move to the PRIIPs KID.

▸4 categories of PRIIPs

The scope of PRIIPs is defined by broadly comparable functions for retail investors. They typically:

  • Provide exposure to multiple underlying assets
  • Deliver capital accumulation over a medium- to long-term period
  • Entail a degree of investment risk (though some may provide capital guarantees)
  • Are normally marketed to retail investors

4 PRIIPs groups:

  1. Investment funds
  2. Insurance-based investment products (e.g. unit-linked insurance bonds)
  3. Retail-structured securities
  4. Structured term deposits

▸Who is affected

The regulation applies to firms that:

  • Manufacture PRIIPs
  • Advise on / sell PRIIPs

▸Format and structure of the KID

The PRIIPs KID must be:

  • A stand-alone document
  • Standardised format
  • Maximum 3 A4 pages in length
  • 7-section pre-determined sequence (strict sequencing of 7 sections)

▸7 sections of the PRIIPs KID

  1. What is this product?
  2. What are the risks and what could I get in return?
  3. What happens if [manufacturer name] is unable to pay out?
  4. What are the costs?
  5. How long should I hold it and can I take my money out early?
  6. How can I complain?
  7. Other relevant information

▸Timing and RTSs

The KID must be provided in good time before the transaction. Detailed rules on content, calculation, review and timing are contained in the Regulatory Technical Standards (RTSs):

  • Content and presentation
  • How to calculate certain information
  • Review, revision and republication
  • Timing of delivery
"Retail client" under PRIIPs — a broader definition
For PRIIPs, retail client = (1) retail clients as defined in MiFID, OR (2) customers as referred to in the Insurance Distribution Directive (IDD) who would not qualify as professional clients under MiFID. So the scope is wider than just MiFID retail.
Example of product classification
An insurance company manufactures a unit-linked insurance bond — an investment product where the client's premium is invested in inner funds, and the policy covers lives. This is an insurance-based investment product → one of the 4 PRIIPs categories → a PRIIPs KID is required. A KIID is not needed here — a KIID is only for UCITS.
8.2

Right to Cancel — general structure

A "cooling-off period" is a period after contract conclusion during which the client can change their mind and cancel the contract without giving a reason. A fundamental retail consumer protection: giving them time to reflect after coming under sales pressure.

▸To whom it applies

Cancellation rights must be provided to retail customers. The rules apply to:

  • Firms providing retail financial products based on designated investments or deposits
  • Firms entering into distance contracts with consumers relating to deposits or designated investments
Who is NOT covered
Professional clients and ECPs — NO cancellation rights. This is a retail protection.
8.2.1

Cancellation Periods — 14 or 30 days

Product typeCancellation period
Life assurance or pension (incl. life endowment policies, personal pension schemes, stakeholder pensions) 30 calendar days
Cash deposit ISA 14 calendar days
Other non-life/non-pension (e.g. investment products, distance contracts for deposits, etc.) 14 calendar days

▸When the period starts

The period begins on the later of two dates:

  • Date of contract conclusion, OR
  • Date of receipt by the client of contractual information (when the client received all the terms)
Timing logic
The client must have real time after learning the terms to decide. So if the firm delayed contractual info — the cooling-off period is extended. A firm cannot "quickly conclude and run out the 14 days without info".

▸The logic of different periods

Why 30 days for life/pension and 14 for the rest? Life and pension are long-term, complex products with serious consequences (sometimes over decades). A mistake in such a choice is a catastrophe. Hence the double cooling-off period.

Timing example
A client signs a life endowment policy on 1 May. But the full contractual info only arrives by post on 5 May. The 30-day period starts from 5 May (later of), so the client can cancel until 3 June.
8.2.2

Required Disclosures — what the firm must tell the client

The firm must disclose to the client 5 elements about cancellation rights:

  1. Existence of the right to cancel
  2. Duration of the period
  3. Conditions (including any costs associated with exercising the right)
  4. Consequences if the right is not exercised
  5. How to exercise the right (practical mechanics)

▸Timing and medium of disclosure

This information must be provided in a durable medium, before the client is bound by the contract. If this is not possible — immediately after binding.

8.2.3

Exercising the Right — how the client cancels

To exercise the right to cancel, the client must notify the firm. The notification is considered timely if dispatched before the deadline expires — i.e. the date of dispatch counts, not receipt by the firm.

▸Key features

  • The client is not required to give a reason for cancellation — cooling-off without questions.
  • On cancellation the client may not get the full amount back — market movements apply. If the fund has fallen 5% over 14 days, the client gets back less than they paid in.
Important exam nuance
Many think cancellation means full refund. It does NOT. For investment products the client bears market risk from the moment of conclusion to cancellation. What is returned is the current value of the investment.
Market movement example
A client invested £10,000 in an investment product on 1 April. On 10 April they decide to cancel. The market fell 3% in that time. The client will get back roughly £9,700, not £10,000. This is not a penalty — it is simply the current value of the investment.
8.2.4

Record-Keeping — retention periods for cancellation records

TypeRetention period
Pension transfers, pension opt-outs, FSAVCs INDEFINITELY
Life policies, pension contracts, personal pension schemes, stakeholder pension schemes At least 5 years
All other cases At least 3 years
Link to Section 4 — same rules
The same retention periods apply to client agreements and other records (see Section 4). Pension transfers — always indefinite. Life/pension — 5 years. Other — 3 or 5 years depending on the record type.

What is this section about?
Section 7 covers the Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act (ECCTA), which significantly expanded the UK's anti-economic-crime framework. The key development: a new corporate offence of "failure to prevent fraud", in force from 1 September 2025. The structure mirrors Bribery Act s.7 — strict liability + a defence of reasonable procedures. It applies only to large organisations (2 of 3 criteria: turnover >£36m / balance sheet >£18m / >250 employees). Base fraud offences — the 3 limbs of Section 1 Fraud Act 2006 (false representation, failing to disclose, abuse of position) + others. Also — Companies House reforms, cryptoasset seizure, SLAPPs provisions. 1-2 exam questions.

7.1

ECCTA Overview

The Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act (ECCTA) aims to deliver reforms tackling economic crime + improve transparency over corporate entities.

▸What ECCTA introduced

  • A new regulatory objective in the Legal Services Act 2007 — tackling economic crime
  • Reforms to Companies House — preventing creation of + shutting down fraudulent companies
  • Reforms to prevent abuse of limited partnerships
  • Additional powers to seize + recover suspected criminal cryptoassets
  • New failure to prevent fraud offence
  • Measures to address SLAPPs (Strategic Lawsuits Against Public Participation) — used to silence whistleblowers / journalists
7.2

Failure to Prevent Fraud — new corporate offence

ECCTA failure to prevent fraud
ECCTA introduced the corporate criminal offence of failure to prevent fraud — came into effect 1 September 2025.

Home Office guidance: "An organisation may be criminally liable where an employee, agent, subsidiary, or other 'associated person' commits a fraud intending to benefit the organisation, and the organisation did not have reasonable fraud prevention procedures in place".

▸Scope — only large organisations

The offence applies to:

  • All large incorporated bodies, subsidiaries, and partnerships
  • Large not-for-profit organisations (incorporated charities)
  • Incorporated public bodies
"Large" — 2 of 3 criteria
The organisation must meet at least 2 of 3 criteria:
  • Turnover > £36 million
  • Balance sheet total > £18 million
  • > 250 employees
SMEs are exempt — unlike Bribery Act s.7, which applies to all.

▸Associated person

The base fraud offence must be committed by a person associated with the relevant body:

  • Employee
  • Agent
  • Subsidiary of the relevant body
  • Person providing services for / on behalf of the relevant body, while providing those services

Aiding, abetting, counselling, or procuring the commission of the listed offences also qualifies.

▸Intent to benefit organisation

Benefit requirement
The benefit may be financial or non-financial. The organisation does not need to actually receive the benefit — the offence is complete before any gain is received. It is enough that the organisation was intended to be the beneficiary.

▸Defence

The only defence is that the "organisation had reasonable procedures in place to prevent fraud". Mirrors the Bribery Act s.7 structure.

7.2.1

Base Fraud Offences — Schedule 13 ECCTA

The offence applies to the "base fraud" offences listed in Schedule 13 of ECCTA:

▸Fraud Act 2006, Section 1 — 3 ways of fraud

1
Fraud by false representation — dishonestly making a false representation with intent to make gain / cause loss.
2
Fraud by failing to disclose information — dishonestly failing to disclose information where there is a legal duty to disclose, with intent to make gain / cause loss.
3
Fraud by abuse of position — dishonestly abusing a position in which the person is expected to safeguard the financial interests of another person.

▸Other base fraud offences

  • Participation in a fraudulent business
  • Obtaining services dishonestly
  • Cheating the public revenue (common law)
  • False accounting
  • False statements by company directors
  • Fraudulent trading — carrying on business with intent to defraud creditors
ML — NOT a fraud offence
Money laundering is NOT an offence of fraud. ML has its own regime under POCA (Sec 4). ECCTA failure to prevent fraud is separate.
7.3

6 Home Office Fraud Prevention Principles

The Home Office provided guidance — 6 fraud prevention principles that should inform an organisation's framework (very similar to the MoJ Bribery Act 6 principles):

1
Top-level commitment — senior management actively demonstrates zero tolerance of fraud
2
Risk assessment — identifying where the organisation is at risk of fraud
3
Proportionate risk-based prevention procedures
4
Due diligence — understanding who the organisation works with — including employees, agents, third parties that could commit fraud on its behalf
5
Communication (including training) — staff / partners aware of policies + training
6
Monitoring and review — risks change; procedures must be kept up to date
ECCTA vs Bribery Act — parallel structure
ECCTA failure to prevent fraud closely mirrors the Bribery Act s.7 failure to prevent bribery:
  • Both — strict corporate liability
  • Both — defence of "reasonable procedures"
  • Both — 6 guidance principles
Difference: the Bribery Act applies to ALL organisations; ECCTA only to LARGE.

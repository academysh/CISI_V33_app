Chapter 2, Section 4 — Detailed Deep Dive
Client Agreements and Information Provision · Syllabus 2.4 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 4 answers two practical questions: "What must be in the written agreement with the client" and "What information must the firm provide to the client before services begin". This is a foundational part of COBS: it describes the basic hygiene of the firm↔client relationship — client agreement, firm information, disclosure of risks, costs, and compensation schemes. On the exam — expect 3-5 questions, with many details about timing, mediums, and exceptions.

INTRODUCTION

The philosophy: protecting the client through transparency

Across all financial services regulation, there is a fundamental problem — information asymmetry. The firm knows everything about the product, risks, costs, and contract terms; the client knows very little. Without intervention, the client has no real chance of making an informed decision.

Section 4 is a concrete tool to address this asymmetry. It forces the firm to:

  • Fix rights and obligations in a written agreement (client agreement).
  • Proactively disclose information about itself, products, risks, and costs.
  • Keep the client updated about changes.
  • Do all of this through a durable medium — so the client can return and check.
The underlying principle: "in good time before"
Almost every disclosure in this section must be made "in good time before" the client is bound by the agreement or before services begin. This is one of the most repeated principles — and a classic exam trap (answer options often include "after", "at the time", "within 14 days" — all wrong; the correct answer is "in good time before").

"Durable medium" is another pervasive term. It is any medium that allows the client to store information personally addressed to them, access it for an adequate period, and reproduce it unchanged. Durable media include: paper, email, PDF, attachments to electronic communications, and (under conditions) websites. A plain SMS or a verbal conversation does not qualify.

4.1

Requirement to Provide a Client Agreement

▸When a client agreement is required

The requirement to enter into a written client agreement applies in two cases:

Business type Client category Agreement needed?
Designated investment business (broadly) Retail client ✓ Yes
MiFID business and ancillary services (or equivalent third-country) Retail OR Professional ✓ Yes
Designated investment business (non-MiFID) Professional client ✗ No
Any business ECP (Eligible Counterparty) ✗ No (COBS 8A disapplied)
Insurance firms issuing life policies as principal Any ✗ No (special exclusion)
Exam mnemonic
Retail: agreement is always required for designated investment business.
Professional: agreement is required only for MiFID / ancillary / equivalent third-country.
ECP: never required (COBS 8A disapplied).
Exclusion: insurance firms issuing life policies as principal — outside the scope of this rule.

▸Form of the agreement

The firm must enter into a written basic agreement with the client, setting out the essential rights and obligations of the firm and the client. The agreement must be on paper or another durable medium.

What is covered by "essential rights and obligations"
Typical content of such an agreement:
• Description of services the firm will provide
• Fees and firm remuneration
• Reporting to the client
• Limitations of liability (within permitted bounds)
• Complaints procedure
• Termination conditions
• Jurisdiction and applicable law
The FCA does not mandate a specific template — the firm decides how to structure it, as long as essential rights and obligations are covered.

▸When the agreement and related information must be provided

For a retail client: the firm must provide the client (in good time) before the client is bound by the agreement, OR before investment services commence (whichever is earlier):

  • The terms of any such agreement
  • Information about the firm and its services relating to that agreement or those services as required by COBS 6.1.4 and COBS 6.1ZA.2 — including authorised communications, conflicts of interest, and the firm's authorised status
Timeline for a retail client
────────── TIME ──────────────►
▼
BEFORE signing / before services
the firm must:
① deliver the agreement terms
② deliver firm information
▼
Client reviews and analyses
▼
Client signs / consents

Implicit: the client had reasonable time to review.

▸Exception: distance communications + voice telephone

One important exception. If (a) the client themselves requested to use distance communications (e.g. concluding everything over the phone or through a web form), and (b) the firm complies with the voice telephone communications rules, the firm may provide the agreement and information immediately after the client is bound.

Practical example of the exception
A client phones a broker and says: "I have an urgent trade, I want to buy 1,000 Tesla shares right now, I cannot wait for the paperwork to arrive." If the client themselves requested the phone transaction, and the firm complies with all voice-telephone-communications rules (call recording, disclosure of recording, party identification, etc.), then the full agreement and firm information can be sent after the transaction. But this is only at the client's initiative — not the firm's.

▸Changes to agreement terms

The firm must notify the client of any material change to the terms of the client agreement:

  • In good time BEFORE the change takes effect (not after!)
  • In a durable medium
Common mistake
Some people think material changes can be communicated "within a reasonable time after they take effect". Wrong: BEFORE. The logic is obvious — if the terms are changing, the client must have time to decide whether to continue the relationship or walk away.

▸Retention periods for client agreement records

Client agreement records must be retained for the longer of the three periods:

Type Retention
Baseline minimum 5 years
If the client relationship runs longer than 5 years Whole duration of the relationship
Pension transfer, pension opt-out, FSAVC (freestanding additional voluntary contributions) INDEFINITELY
Example: client transferred pension 8 years ago
A client transferred their pension through the firm 8 years ago. The firm has no active relationship with them now. Must it retain the records?

Answer: Yes, indefinitely. Pension transfer is a special category where records must be retained indefinitely. The 5-year minimum is not applicable here.
Additional requirements
When preparing client agreements, firms must also consider other COBS rules:
  • Fair, clear and not misleading
  • Disclosure of information
  • Distance communications
4.2

Information Requirements — nature and risks

The client agreement is about the relationship. Section 4.2 is about the product and service. What exactly the client is buying, what risks are involved, what it costs.

4.2.1

Information about the Nature and Risks of Investments

COBS 2.2 and COBS 2.2A set out high-level disclosure requirements for firms carrying on MiFID business or equivalent third-country business.

▸What must be disclosed (4 categories)

The firm must provide clients — retail AND professional (including potential retail and potential professional clients) — in a comprehensible form with the following information:

  1. The firm and its services
  2. Designated investments and proposed investment strategies, including guidance and warnings about associated risks
  3. Execution venues (trading venues)
  4. Costs and associated charges

Purpose: to enable the client to reasonably understand the nature and risks of the service and of the specific designated investments, and therefore to take investment decisions on an informed basis.

MiFID vs non-MiFID: who receives disclosures?
For MiFID business: disclosures are needed for retail AND professional clients (including potential clients).
For non-MiFID business: disclosures are needed only for retail clients. BUT for non-MiFID business with a retail client in relation to derivatives, warrants or stock lending — disclosure is also required (as under MiFID).

These high-level rules are expanded in COBS 6.1 (firm information) and COBS 14.2 (product information).

4.2.2

Information about the Firm

The firm must provide retail, professional and ECP clients (including potential clients) with the following general information, where relevant:

Item Applicability
Name and address of the firm, including contact details enabling effective communication All clients
Languages in which the client may communicate and receive documents MiFID / equivalent third-country only
Methods of communication between firm and client, including sending/receiving orders All clients
Statement that the firm is authorised, name and contact details of the competent authority (FCA) All clients
If the firm acts through an AR or tied agent — a statement of this fact All clients
Nature, frequency and timing of reports on the performance of the firm's service — per COBS 16 reporting rules All clients
Information about the firm's conflict of interest policy, summary of that policy, including how the firm ensures fair treatment + statement that further details are available on request All clients
Example: failure to disclose AR status
A retail client complains that the firm did not disclose it was acting through an appointed representative. This is a breach of the firm information disclosure requirement. The client has a formal right to know who they are dealing with: directly with the principal or through its AR (this affects the chain of responsibility, regulation, and complaint routes).
4.2.3

Information Requirements Relating to Managing Investments

If the firm manages investments for a client (discretionary or non-discretionary), additional requirements arise.

▸Method of evaluation and comparison

The firm must establish an appropriate method of evaluation and comparison — for example, a meaningful benchmark — based on the investment objectives of the client and the types of investment in the portfolio. The aim: to enable the client to assess the firm's performance on an objective basis.

Practical benchmark example
A firm manages a UK-equity portfolio for the client. A sensible benchmark would be the FTSE All-Share or FTSE 100. Saying "We returned +7% this year" is not enough — maybe the market rose +15% and that's a poor result. Saying "We returned +7% while the FTSE All-Share returned +5%" is correct — now the client has context.

▸Additional disclosures for retail + managed accounts

For retail clients whose accounts are managed by the firm, additional disclosures are required. Among them:

  • Details of any delegation of discretionary management of all or part of the portfolio to a third party
  • Valuation policy
  • Benchmark
  • Timing and nature of reporting
4.2.4

Safeguarding Investments and Money

When the firm holds client investments or money, this is the most sensitive situation: the client has handed their assets to a third party. The rules demand very clear disclosure of how this works.

▸To whom it applies

Safeguarding information must be provided to all client categories (retail, professional, ECP), including potential clients — where the UK MiFID custody / client money rules apply.

This is unique!
Note: this is one of the few areas where disclosure obligations extend even to ECPs — unlike most disclosure requirements, where ECPs are "disapplied". The logic: asset safety is too important to rely on the "assumed sophistication" of ECPs.

▸What must be disclosed (8 elements)

  1. Possible holding by a third party on the firm's behalf; firm's responsibility for acts/omissions of the third party; what happens if the third party becomes insolvent.
  2. Lack of separate designation: if in the country of holding, assets cannot be separately designated from the custodian's own assets — explain what this means for the client and what the risks are.
  3. Non-UK jurisdiction: if assets are subject to non-UK jurisdiction laws — disclose the fact and what it means for the client's rights.
  4. Security interest, lien or right of set-off: if assets or money are subject to security interest, lien or set-off right — full disclosure of the fact and terms of such arrangement.
  5. Securities lending: full and clear information about the firm's obligations and associated risks must be in a durable medium in good time before entering into securities lending.
  6. Summary of protection measures the firm takes for the client's money / investments, including details of applicable investor compensation or deposit guarantee schemes.
  7. Nature and scope of the firm's powers in respect of the assets.
  8. Arrangements for returning assets to the client.
Right of set-off example
The firm holds £50,000 of client money in a clients' money account. The client agreement contains a clause: if the client fails to pay fees owed to the firm, the firm may "set off" that debt against the client's money. This must be explicitly disclosed: the fact that such a right exists + the terms on which it applies. If this clause is buried in small print and was not explained to the client — that's a breach.
Non-UK jurisdiction example
A client buys US-listed shares through a UK broker. The shares are held in the US via a sub-custodian, subject to US law. The firm must disclose: (a) the fact that the shares are under US law; (b) what this means — e.g. in the event of sub-custodian insolvency, the client's rights are determined by US insolvency law, which may differ from UK segregation rules.
4.3

Disclosure of Costs and Associated Charges

▸To whom mandatory cost disclosure applies

Mandatory cost disclosure is required for retail clients. Professional clients and ECPs may request this information, but there is no regulatory obligation to provide it — it is a commercial decision for the firm.

▸What must be disclosed (content of cost disclosure)

The firm must provide retail clients with information on all costs and charges applicable to investment services and financial instruments, including ancillary services. In particular:

  • Total price payable — including all related fees, costs, charges, expenses and taxes payable via the firm.
  • If the total price cannot be stated at the time of disclosure — the basis on which it will be calculated, so the client can verify and clarify later.
  • Costs charged by the firm itself must be itemised separately in every case.
  • If a foreign currency is involved — specify the currency, conversion rate and related costs.
  • If other costs and taxes may apply (not charged/imposed by the firm):
    • The fact that such costs/charges/taxes may be payable
    • How they will be paid / levied
    • Information about compensation schemes
Key rule
The firm's own costs must ALWAYS be itemised separately. Even if not all third-party costs can be precisely stated, the firm is obliged to break out its own charges. This detail is non-negotiable.
Example of a cost disclosure structure
A client is planning to buy fund units. A correct disclosure:
Our (firm) charges:
• Initial charge: 3.0%
• Annual management fee: 1.2%
Third-party costs (approx):
• Custodian fee: ~0.15% p.a.
• Transaction costs in the fund: ~0.35% p.a.
Taxes:
• Stamp duty on UK shares in the fund: 0.5%
• Returns taxed on the client's side (CGT/income tax)
Compensation: The fund is covered by FSCS up to £85,000.
4.3.1

Timing and Medium of Disclosure

▸When to disclose

Default: client information (including cost information) must be provided in good time before the provision of the firm's services.

▸Exception: distance communication

The firm may provide the information immediately after the provision of services if:

  • (a) at the client's request, the agreement was concluded using distance communication which prevented the firm providing the information beforehand, AND
  • (b) the firm complies with the voice telephone communications rules

Both conditions must be met simultaneously.

▸In what medium

Disclosures must be made in a durable medium or, if the website conditions are satisfied, via the firm's website.

Website conditions — what are these conditions
For a website to count as a "durable medium", it must meet the COBS conditions: the client must have consented to the electronic channel, must have technical access, the material must be stable (the client can return to it later), and the website address must be clearly specified.
4.3.2

Keeping the Client Up to Date

The firm must notify the client in good time about any material change to information previously provided which is relevant to a service being provided to that client.

Rule on notification medium
The notification must be in the same durable medium as the original information. If the terms were originally on paper — the change must be on paper (or another durable medium). If originally via the app — the change must be via the app/email. This prevents situations where the client misses changes because they arrived via an unfamiliar channel.
Example: annual management fee increase
A client has a managed account; the agreement specifies AMC 1.0%. The firm decides to raise it to 1.25%. This is a material change → the client must be notified before the change takes effect (not after!) and in the same medium the terms were originally prescribed in.
4.3.3

Existing Clients — repeat transactions

Logical question: if a client already received a cost disclosure when purchasing, say, Fund A, do they need the full disclosure again when purchasing the same Fund A a month later?

Answer: The firm does not need to treat each transaction in the same financial instrument type as a new or different service. So the full disclosure does not need to be repeated.

However: the firm must ensure the client has received all relevant information in respect of the subsequent transaction — e.g. details of product charges which may differ from those previously disclosed.

Example
The client received full cost disclosure in January and bought Fund A. In March they want to buy more of Fund A. No need to reissue the full disclosure. But if in February the firm raised its brokerage fee on this transaction from 0.5% to 0.75% — that's new information the client must be told about before the March trade.
4.3.4

Compensation Information

▸To whom it applies

The obligation to disclose compensation scheme information applies specifically to firms carrying on MiFID business — in respect of clients who use or intend to use those services.

▸Minimum proactive disclosure

The minimum that must be done proactively, without client request:

  • Information necessary to identify the compensation scheme (e.g. FSCS) or any alternative investor compensation scheme of which the firm is a member, or any alternative arrangement
  • Amount and scope of cover offered by the scheme

▸What must be disclosed on request

If the client requests it, the firm must additionally provide information about:

  • Conditions governing compensation
  • Formalities to be completed to obtain compensation
FSCS — the main UK compensation scheme
The Financial Services Compensation Scheme covers clients of failed financial firms. Key limits:
  • Deposits in banks/building societies: up to £85,000 per depositor per bank
  • Investments: up to £85,000 per client per firm
  • Compulsory insurance: 100% with no limit
  • Non-compulsory insurance: 90% with no limit

▸In what medium

Compensation scheme information must be provided in a durable medium or via a website where the website conditions are satisfied.

Example: a full compensation disclosure
"Your investments are covered by the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS). FSCS is the UK's compensation scheme of last resort for clients of authorised financial firms that are unable to meet their claims. FSCS covers investment business up to £85,000 per claimant per firm. For further information about the conditions and procedures for claiming from FSCS you can visit www.fscs.org.uk or request this from us."

The minimum is given: scheme identity + scope. Detailed conditions and formalities — on request.
📋 Final summary of Section 4
Key principles of this section
• ALL disclosures: "in good time BEFORE" (not after!)
• Form: "durable medium" (or website under conditions)
• Material changes: notify BEFORE they take effect + same medium
• Firm's own costs: ALWAYS itemised separately
• Retail = maximum protection, ECP = minimum
• Safeguarding disclosures = all categories (even ECP!)
  • 4.1 Client Agreement required for: (a) designated investment business with retail; (b) MiFID/ancillary with retail OR professional. Does not apply to ECPs and insurance firms issuing life policies as principal.
  • Form of the agreement — paper or other durable medium. Provision: in good time before the client is bound OR before services commence (whichever is earlier).
  • Exception: if the client themselves requested distance communication + the firm complies with voice-telephone rules → immediately after is acceptable.
  • Material changes: notify before they take effect, in a durable medium.
  • Record retention: longer of (5 years, relationship duration, indefinitely for pension transfers/opt-outs/FSAVC).
  • 4.2.1 Nature & Risks (MiFID): 4 categories — firm/services + investments/strategies+risks + execution venues + costs. To whom: retail + professional (including potential). Non-MiFID: retail + mandatory for derivatives/warrants/stock lending.
  • 4.2.2 Firm Information: retail + professional + ECP (including potential). Elements: name/address, languages (MiFID), methods of comm, authorised status + competent authority, AR/tied-agent disclosure, reporting, conflicts policy.
  • 4.2.3 Managing Investments: meaningful benchmark + details of delegated discretionary management.
  • 4.2.4 Safeguarding: for retail + professional + ECP (including potential). 8 key elements, covering third parties, non-segregation, non-UK jurisdiction, security interest / lien / set-off, securities lending, compensation schemes.
  • 4.3 Costs: mandatory for retail only. Elements: total price + basis for calculation + firm's own costs itemised + currency + third-party costs/taxes + compensation info.
  • 4.3.1 Timing: in good time BEFORE services. Exception: distance comm at client's request + voice-telephone compliance → immediately after.
  • 4.3.1 Medium: durable medium or website (under conditions).
  • 4.3.2 Updates: material change → notify + same medium as the original.
  • 4.3.3 Existing clients: no need for a full disclosure per transaction in the same instrument type. BUT — check for new/changed info.
  • 4.3.4 Compensation: MiFID firms only. Proactive minimum: scheme identity + amount/scope. On request: conditions + formalities. Medium: durable or website.

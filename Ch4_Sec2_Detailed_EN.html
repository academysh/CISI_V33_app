What is this section about?
The largest section of Chapter 4 — covering three interrelated topics: (1) Complaint handling — the DISP 1 Sourcebook, eligible complainants, procedures, timeframes (8 weeks, 15 business days for PSD); (2) the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) — a free, impartial dispute resolution body as an alternative to courts. Compulsory Jurisdiction + Voluntary Jurisdiction; (3) the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS) — an "insurance policy" paid for by all authorised firms, compensating eligible claimants when a firm defaults. Also super-complaints and mass-detriment references. 4-5 exam questions, often on FOS time limits, eligible complainants, and FSCS limits.

2.1

Eligible Complainants — criteria

Not all complainants have the right to subsequently take their grievance to the FOS. Access to the FOS depends on (1) the circumstances falling within the FOS jurisdiction, and (2) the eligibility of the complainant.

▸6 categories of eligible complainants

1
Consumers — any person using or contemplating the services of an authorised firm / appointed representative.
2
Micro-enterprises — any person engaged in economic activity, fewer than 10 persons, turnover / balance sheet ≤ €2 million at the time the complaint is raised.
3
Charities — annual income < £6.5 million at the time the complaint is raised.
4
Trustees of trusts — net asset value < £5 million at the time the complaint is raised.
5
Certain SMEs — annual turnover < £6.5 million + balance sheet < £5 million, OR fewer than 50 employees.
6
Persons acting outside business/profession — or borrowers under a "buy-to-let" credit agreement (even an ECP / professional client may be in scope).

▸Business relationship requirement

Also required — business relationship
The complaint must arise out of a business relationship with the firm:
  • Customer
  • Beneficial interest in a pension scheme
  • Policyholder
  • Personal guarantors of loans to a business they are involved in

▸Who is NOT generally eligible

Typically excluded
  • Other authorised firms (unless the complaint is about an activity for which they do not have permission themselves)
  • ECPs + professional clients (other than pension scheme trustees) — unless the activity is outside their classification
2.2

Complaint Handling Procedures — DISP 1

The FCA requires firms to have appropriate written procedures for handling expressions of dissatisfaction. Procedures must be followed regardless of:

  • Oral or written complaint
  • Justified or not
  • As long as it relates to the firm's provision / failure to provide a financial service

▸Procedures must capture

  • Receiving complaints
  • Acknowledging receipt of complaints
  • Responding to complaints
  • Appropriately investigating complaints
  • Notifying eligible complainants of their right to go to the FOS

▸DISP — Dispute Resolution: Complaints Sourcebook

DISP 1 — "Treating Complainants Fairly"
Complaint Handling Rules are in the FCA's Dispute Resolution: Complaints Sourcebook (DISP 1), entitled "Treating Complainants Fairly". It contains rules + guidance on how respondents should deal promptly and fairly with complaints.

▸DISP 1.2 — Consumer Awareness Rules

Firms must:

  • Publish summary details of their internal complaints process + the right of eligible complainants to take unresolved complaints to the FOS
  • Refer eligible complainants in writing to the availability of summary details at, or immediately after, the point of sale
  • Provide such summary details in writing on request at any time + when acknowledging a complaint

▸DISP 1.3 — Complaint Handling Rules

Firms must have effective and transparent procedures for reasonable + prompt handling of all complaints, without charging the complainant. Complaints may be made by any reasonable means — oral or written.

2.2.1

Timings — key deadlines

▸3 business days — informal resolution

"Next business days" rule
Firms may handle complaints less formally — without sending an acknowledgement letter — if resolved within 3 business days of receipt. After 3 days, firms must send a summary resolution communication.

▸8 weeks — general rule

Firms must send a final response within 8 weeks of receipt of the complaint. The final response must include:

  • The firm's decision
  • FOS referral information — within 6 months of the final response
  • The FOS leaflet

▸PSD / EMD — 15 business days

Payment Services Directive complaints
For PSD (Payment Services Directive) / EMD (Electronic Money Directive) complaints, there is a strict deadline: 15 business days. In exceptional circumstances, extendable to 35 business days.

▸Reporting

Firms must submit complaints data to the FCA twice yearly (bi-annual 'new complaints' return) — detailing the number of complaints received.

▸Complaint "closed"

When a complaint is "closed"
A complaint is closed when:
  • The firm has sent a final response, OR
  • The complainant has indicated in writing that they accept an earlier response

▸Complaint records

Must record:

  • Name of the complainant
  • Substance of the complaint
  • Measures taken to provide a resolution
2.3

FOS — role + jurisdictions + funding

▸Role of the FOS

Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS)
Under FSMA, the FCA has the power to make rules relating to the handling of complaints. An independent body — the FOS (operated by Financial Ombudsman Service Ltd) — administers the dispute resolution scheme.

Role: a free, impartial, efficient way for consumers to resolve complaints about financial products/services, acting as an alternative to the courts. The FOS aims for fair and reasonable outcomes for both consumers and businesses.

▸FOS key features

  • Free to complainants — removes the deterrent of legal costs
  • Minimum formality + quick resolution
  • Does not replace the court system — a complainant can still take civil action if NOT accepting the final FOS decision
  • Accepting the FOS final decision = final and binding on both parties — no civil action possible after

▸2 Jurisdictions

CJ
Compulsory Jurisdiction — automatically applies to all FCA-authorised firms, eligible complainants, regulated activities.
VJ
Voluntary Jurisdiction — firms opt in via a contractual arrangement with the FOS (becoming a VJ participant). Allows non-FCA-authorised firms to use the service.

▸Funding

How the FOS is funded
The service is free for complainants. Regulated firms pay:
  • An annual "levy" — as part of overall regulatory fees
  • "Case fees" — for each case accepted and investigated by the FOS

▸Time limits

Critical deadline — 6 months
The complainant must refer the complaint to the FOS within 6 months of the firm's final response. Miss the deadline — no access to the FOS.

▸FOS awards

After adjudication, the FOS writes to the complainant asking for confirmation — accept or reject the decision. Accepting = final + binding.

Maximum award limits are automatically adjusted on 1 April each year using the CPI for the preceding January. The maximum excludes interest on the award + costs + interest on costs.

2.3.4

Super-complaints + Mass-detriment

▸Super-complaints

Certain consumer organisations are allowed to make super-complaints. The aim is to provide consumer bodies with a way of raising competition and consumer issues with the FCA.

Super-complaint process
The super-complainant (designated by HM Treasury via primary legislation) writes to the FCA setting out why a UK market has a feature that is significantly harming consumer interests.

The FCA must respond within 90 days.

▸Mass-detriment references

Mass-detriment = large-scale damage, harm or loss to consumers. A reference can be made by the FOS or a regulated firm if:

  • There are regular failures by firm(s) to comply + consumers have suffered / may suffer loss + remediable by legal proceedings
  • Firm(s) have acted regularly in a way that a complaint would likely be upheld by the FOS
2.4

FSCS — purpose + eligibility

Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS)
The FSCS was established to pay compensation to eligible claimants in the event of the default of an authorised firm, where the firm's insurance cover is exhausted + the firm enters insolvency. Essentially an "insurance policy", paid for by all authorised firms.

▸3 conditions for a claim

To receive compensation, the person making the claim must:

  • Be an eligible claimant
  • Have a protected claim
  • Against an authorised firm that is in default

▸Eligible claimants

Most clients of a defaulting firm — EXCEPT:

  • Other authorised firms
  • Overseas financial institutions
  • CISs, their trustees / operators
  • Some pension / retirement funds
  • Supranational institutions, governments, central administrative authorities
  • Provincial / regional / local / municipal authorities
  • Directors + managers of the firm in default + their relatives
  • Companies in the same group as the firm in default
  • Persons holding ≥ 5% of the defaulting firm's capital
  • Auditors of the firm in default
  • Any person who has contributed to the firm's default
  • Large companies / partnerships / mutual associations
  • Persons whose claim arises from transactions where they have been convicted of money laundering
Large companies — NOT eligible
Important: large public limited companies (plc) are generally excluded from the FSCS. The FSCS primarily covers private individuals + small businesses.

▸Protected claims

  • Protected deposits — money on deposit with a bank
  • Protected contracts of insurance
  • Protected investment business
  • Claims against the manager / trustee of an authorised unit trust or the ACD / depository of an ICVC
Unregulated CIS — still covered!
Operating an unregulated CIS is designated investment business. Claims against a UK-authorised unregulated scheme operator are eligible under the FSCS.
2.4.2

FSCS Compensation Limits

▸Deposits (from 1 Dec 2025)

CategoryLimit
Deposits (per eligible person, per firm)£110,000 (was £85,000 before 1 Dec 2025)
Joint accounts£220,000
Temporary high balances£1.4 million for 6 months (was £1m before 1 Dec 2025)
Temporary high balances — events
Temporary high balance protection applies when a large sum arises from:
  • Property transactions (e.g. house sale)
  • Marriage / civil partnership
  • Divorce
  • Redundancy
  • Personal injury compensation
  • Inheritance

▸Insurance (firm failed after 8 Oct 2020)

Insurance typeProtection
Compulsory insurance100%
Long-term insurance100%
Professional indemnity insurance100%
Claims from death / incapacity (sickness, injury, infirmity)100%
All other kinds of insurance90%

▸Insurance brokers / financial advisers (no time limit)

  • Compulsory general insurance: 100%
  • All other general insurances: 90%

▸Investments (firm failed after 1 Apr 2019)

Investment limit — £85,000
£85,000 per eligible person, per firm. This is the standard number to remember for the exam.

▸Pensions (firm failed after 1 Apr 2019)

ScenarioProtection
Pension provider fails100% — no upper limit
SIPP operator fails£85,000 per eligible person, per firm
Bad pension advice£85,000 per eligible person, per firm
Exam trap — pension provider vs SIPP operator
Pension provider fails = 100% no limit (full protection).
SIPP operator fails = £85,000 only.
These are different outcomes for different failures in the pension space.

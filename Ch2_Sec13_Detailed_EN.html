Chapter 2, Section 13 — Detailed Deep Dive
Client Assets (CASS) · Syllabus 2.22 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 13 is the final and one of the most critical sections. It is about CASS (Client Assets Sourcebook): the rules on how a firm must protect client money and client assets. The logic is simple but the stakes are enormous: if the firm fails, client money and client assets must NOT end up with the firm's creditors. For that — segregation (separating from the firm's own money), statutory trust, strict reconciliation procedures, and an annual auditor's report. Historically CASS is one of the hottest enforcement topics (FCA fines on firms for CASS breaches run into tens of millions of pounds). On the exam 3-5 questions with specific numbers: CASS 6, CASS 7, CASS 7.17.2R, 20 days, 25 business days.

13.1

Purpose of CASS — why rules on client money and custody exist

The rules relating to custody and safeguarding of client money and client assets are contained in the Client Assets Sourcebook (CASS). These requirements ensure firms take adequate steps to protect those client assets for which they are responsible.

▸Scope — who is covered

CASS generally applies to every firm, with certain specific exemptions. CASS applies directly in respect of activities conducted with or for all categories of client:

  • Retail clients
  • Professional clients
  • Eligible counterparties (ECPs)
A key difference of CASS from many other rules
Unlike many other rules in COBS (which often disapply for ECPs), CASS applies to ALL categories. The reason: this is not about conduct, but about ownership — even an ECP has the right to know that its deposits and assets are not commingled with firm's own money and won't be lost on firm failure.

▸The main goal of CASS — segregation

Within CASS there is a requirement to segregate client money from any other money held by the firm. This ensures that if the firm fails, the money will not be used to repay the firm's creditors.

The logic of CASS — a pre-insolvency mindset
Every CASS rule should be read through the question: "What happens if the firm goes bust tomorrow?" Client money must be clearly identifiable, separate, and reachable by an administrator — so the client gets their money back quickly and in full, rather than standing in line with general creditors.

▸Historical importance

After the collapse of Lehman Brothers (2008), it emerged that US Lehman had not segregated client money properly — and clients spent years trying to recover their money through complex legal proceedings. Since then the FCA has substantially tightened CASS and made it one of the top priorities of supervision. Firms are fined for CASS breaches not on a "rule broken" basis, but on a "client money put at risk" basis — and the fines are enormous.

13.2

Structure of CASS — chapters 6 and 7

ChapterWhat it regulatesScope
CASS 5 Client money — insurance mediation activity Insurance brokers
CASS 6 Custody rules — financial instruments Firms holding client's financial instruments: MiFID business + safeguarding/administering investments (non-MiFID)
CASS 7 Client Money and Distribution Rules UK MiFID investment firm — client money + investment agreement context
CASS 10 CASS resolution pack Documentation for administrator on firm failure

▸CASS 6 — custody rules in detail

CASS 6 contains custody rules that apply when:

  • A firm holds financial instruments of a client in the course of MiFID business
  • A firm is safeguarding and administering investments in the course of non-MiFID business

▸CASS 7 — client money rules in detail

CASS 7 applies to a UK MiFID investment firm either:

  • When it holds client money in the course of MiFID business or in respect of any investment agreement entered into with or for a client
  • When, in the course of non-MiFID business, the firm opts to comply with the MiFID client money rules
  • When it holds money in respect of which CASS 5 (client money — insurance mediation activity) applies and the firm elects to apply CASS 7 provisions
Exam — CASS 6 vs CASS 7
CASS 6 = custody rules = financial instruments (securities) of clients.
CASS 7 = client money rules = cash of clients.
Often asked — important to distinguish.
13.3

Statutory Trust — CASS 7.17.2R

▸Segregation mechanism

Usually, segregation of client money is achieved by the client's money being placed promptly in a separately designated client money account with a bank; the firm then ensures the bank treats this money separately from the firm's own funds.

▸Legal form — statutory trust

CASS 7.17.2R — key legal structure
This is achieved by the firm holding client money IN TRUST. The trust is a statutory trust, created by rules laid down in CASS 7.17.2R.

A statutory trust is a legal construct in which the firm acts as trustee, and the client as beneficiary. The money does not belong to the firm — even if the bank account is in the firm's name, legal ownership remains with the client.

Practical consequence of the trust for insolvency
If the firm goes bust: money on client money accounts is NOT part of the firm's estate. The administrator cannot distribute these funds among the firm's creditors. Clients get their money back directly (through the distribution process under CASS 7A).

Contrast with an ordinary bank deposit: if an ordinary company account (commingling client money and own money), that money becomes part of the estate → clients queue up with creditors → may receive only a fraction.
Example — why the statutory trust matters
Clients of firm X hold £50 million on client money accounts. Firm X goes bust with debts of £80 million. Firm assets total £30 million.

Without CASS trust: the clients' £50M has been commingled with firm money — all goes into the common pool. Creditors (£80M + £50M = £130M debt) vs £30M + £50M = £80M assets → each gets ~62%. Clients lose ~£19M.

With CASS trust (CASS 7.17.2R): the £50M is not part of the estate. Clients receive £50M back in full. The firm's own creditors share only the £30M of firm assets.
13.4

Exemptions from CASS rules

CASS applies generally, but there are specific exemptions. The main ones:

  1. ICVCs (Investment Companies with Variable Capital) — also known as OEICs. They have a separate regulatory framework under the OEIC Regulations 2001.
  2. UCITS-qualifying schemes
  3. Credit institutions acting as "banker" — deposits taken by a bank in its normal banking capacity are not client money. When a bank takes a deposit, the client becomes a creditor of the bank (not a beneficiary of a trust).
  4. Coins held for metal value
  5. Money held in DvP (Delivery-vs-Payment) transactions — unless settlement fails within 3 business days. If DvP does not settle within 3 days — the money becomes client money under CASS.
  6. Money due to the firm (fees, commissions already earned by the firm)
  7. Firm in own name by client agreement (specific structured arrangements)
  8. Full ownership transfers for security purposes — where a client transfers full ownership of assets to the firm as collateral securing present or future obligations

▸Full ownership transfer — in detail

CASS 6 and CASS 7 do not apply to full ownership transfers
Where a client transfers full ownership of safe custody assets or client money to the firm for the purpose of securing or covering present or future, actual, contingent or prospective obligations — CASS 6 and CASS 7 do not apply.

Logic: if the client has handed over full ownership — it is no longer a "client asset", it is a firm asset. CASS protects only client property held by the firm temporarily / for administration.
Full ownership transfer example
Client X provides the firm with securities worth £1M as title transfer collateral for securing a loan facility. Legal title to the securities is transferred to the firm; the firm may use or sell them at any time.

CASS does not apply (the securities are the firm's property, not the client's). Instead of CASS protections, the client has contractual rights to get equivalent securities back once the loan is repaid.

▸Mixed remittances — a cheque with mixed content

If the firm receives a mixed payment (cheque) containing both client money and the firm's own fees — the entire amount must be treated as client money until the firm has segregated its own portion. A protective rule.

Mixed remittance example
A client sends the firm a cheque for £1,000: £900 — client money (to be invested), £100 — the firm's management fee.

Correct: the entire £1,000 is initially treated as client money → placed in the client money account. The firm then transfers £100 to its own account (segregating its own portion).
Wrong: splitting on receipt and putting £100 straight into the firm account before the segregation process.
13.5

Client Bank Account — opening procedure

▸Notice to the bank

When a client bank account is opened, the firm must send a notice to the bank, advising that:

  • The bank must not combine money in the client account with any other accounts held in the firm's name
  • The title of the client account must distinguish it from any other account held by the firm

▸20 days — bank acknowledgement (UK accounts)

For client bank accounts held in the UK, the bank must acknowledge the arrangement (of trustee status) within 20 days, otherwise the firm must:

  • Withdraw the money
  • Deposit it with another bank as soon as possible
Key number — 20 days
20 days — deadline for bank acknowledgement of the trust arrangement. If not acknowledged → withdraw money and deposit elsewhere ASAP.
Classic exam question: "After 25 days the bank has not sent an acknowledgement, what to do?" → withdraw + deposit elsewhere ASAP.

▸Prohibition — no money until acknowledgement

Updated rule
The rules now prohibit the placement of client money in an account UNTIL the relevant acknowledgement letter has been signed and returned to the firm. This applies to all client bank accounts, not just UK accounts.

That is: previously you could deposit, wait for the acknowledgement for 20 days, and if it didn't come — withdraw. Now: you cannot even deposit until a signed acknowledgement is received.

▸Trust letter and acknowledgement — contents

In the notice / acknowledgement letter, the bank must confirm the following:

  • The account is held by the firm as trustee for clients
  • The bank will not combine the money with other accounts of the firm
  • The bank has no right of set-off against money in the client account (cannot use client money to offset the firm's debts to the bank)
  • The bank acknowledges the money is client money, not firm's money
13.6

Custody Assets — CASS 6 safeguarding rules

▸Adequate arrangements for safeguarding ownership

When holding safe custody assets belonging to clients, firms must make adequate arrangements:

  • Safeguard client's ownership rights — especially in the event of the firm's insolvency
  • Prevent client's safe custody assets being used on the firm's own account — except with the client's express consent
  • Introduce adequate organisational arrangements to minimise the risk of loss or diminution of the client's safe custody assets

▸Correct legal title

Firms must register the correct legal title to the assets in the name of the client, nominee, or other authorised person, as appropriate to the nature of the service being provided.

If the firm owns a nominee, it must accept the same level of responsibility for the nominee as if the firm held the assets itself.

▸Express consent — use of client assets

Express consent for securities lending / rehypothecation
By default — the firm CANNOT use client safe custody assets on its own account. But with the client's express consent, this is permitted. Classic use case: securities lending — the firm lends out the client's shares to a hedge fund, the client earns a fee, the firm also earns a spread.

Without express consent this would be misappropriation of client assets — a serious regulatory violation.
Non-segregation country example
A client wants to invest in market X where the legal framework does not allow separate designation of client assets from the custodian's own assets. The firm must explain this to the client in a durable medium and disclose what risks this creates for the client's assets in that jurisdiction. Without such disclosure — breach of safeguarding disclosure rules (Section 4 + Section 13).
13.7

Reconciliation — Standard vs Alternative Approach

CASS requires firms to regularly reconcile their internal records of client money with external records (bank statements). This catches errors, misappropriations, and shortfalls.

▸Standard approach — internal reconciliation

Under the standard approach, the firm must perform:

  • Internal client money reconciliation — at minimum every 25 business days
  • External client money reconciliation — on a regular basis

▸Alternative approach — daily reconciliation

If the firm adopts the alternative approach, internal client money reconciliations must be performed EACH BUSINESS DAY.

When the alternative approach is used
The alternative approach is typically used by firms with many small / frequent movements of client money (e.g. retail brokers). Daily reconciliation lets discrepancies be caught quickly. The standard approach is proportionate for firms with more static client money positions.

▸Shortfall and excess

If reconciliation shows a shortfall (the firm has less client money than it should), the firm must pay in its own money to cover the shortfall, pending investigation. If an excess — the firm must pay it into the client money account. These actions are required as soon as possible.

Shortfall handling example
Friday morning reconciliation: the firm's internal records show £10,000,000 of client money. Bank statements show £9,998,500. Shortfall £1,500.

The firm must immediately:
• Pay £1,500 from its own money into the client money account to cover the shortfall
• Investigate the causes of the shortfall (bank error? internal error? misappropriation?)
• Report serious/unresolved breach to the FCA
13.8

Client Assets Report — the annual auditor's report

▸What a Client Assets Report is

Firms holding client money or client assets must engage an independent auditor for an annual Client Assets Report. This is not an ordinary financial audit — it is specifically an audit of compliance with the CASS rules.

▸Two types of report

TypeWhat it means
Reasonable assurance Positive form — the auditor gives a positive opinion that the firm has complied with CASS. Higher standard
Limited assurance Negative form — the auditor is not aware of anything suggesting non-compliance. Lower standard (for certain smaller firms)

▸Firm's own risk assessment — key exam fact

For a reasonable assurance Client Assets Report — key fact
When the auditor conducts a reasonable assurance Client Assets Report, the auditor must take into account the FIRM'S OWN RISK ASSESSMENT of its client assets arrangements.

The risk assessment forms the basis for determining the scope and focus of the audit.

Logic: each firm has its own unique set of CASS risks (depending on business model, client base, geography, product types). The firm's own risk assessment is the starting point. The auditor does not conduct a generic audit — the auditor focuses on identified risks.

▸Submission to the FCA

The report is submitted to the FCA. If the auditor identifies material CASS breaches — this triggers supervisory attention. In serious cases the FCA may inspect the firm, demand remedial actions, or impose fines.

13.9

CASS Oversight — SMF responsibility

▸CASS oversight officer (SMF18)

Under SM&CR, firms holding client money or assets must appoint a CASS oversight senior manager function (SMF18). This individual is personally accountable for:

  • Overseeing the firm's CASS compliance
  • Reporting breaches to the regulator
  • Ensuring the firm has adequate systems and controls for CASS
  • Ensuring the resolution pack (CASS 10) is up to date

▸CASS Resolution Pack (CASS 10)

CASS 10 requires firms to maintain a resolution pack — a set of documents enabling an administrator to quickly identify and distribute client money / assets on firm failure.

The pack must include:

  • Identification of all client money accounts and custody accounts
  • Contact details of bank counterparties and custodians
  • Information on segregation arrangements
  • List of all clients with their entitlements
  • Keys, passwords, system access for insolvency practitioners

The pack must be retrievable within 48 hours upon request by an insolvency practitioner.

Significance of the resolution pack — lessons from Lehman
The Lehman Brothers collapse in 2008 showed how poorly prepared most firms were for the rapid identification and return of client assets. Clients waited years to recover their holdings. CASS 10 is a direct response: the firm must be prepared to hand over documentation to the administrator within 48 hours so that client returns can be rapid.

What is this section about?
The Disclosure and Transparency Rules (DTR) are part of the FCA Handbook. They are designed to deliver transparency over issuers so that investors can make informed decisions. Key compliance requirements: (1) 3% notifiable interest (UK retained; the EU starts at 5%); (2) PDMR transaction reporting — 3 business days + €5,000 threshold + 30 calendar-day closed period; (3) Section 793 Companies Act 2006 letter for investigating beneficial ownership; (4) global context — US CTA 2021. 1-2 exam questions, often about the 3% threshold + whole percentage points, exceptions, closed period.

8.1

DTR Overview + Inside Information Control

The Disclosure and Transparency Rules (DTR) are part of the FCA Handbook. They are implemented by the FCA in its role as the UK's listing authority.

▸Purpose — transparency

Aim of the DTR
To enable existing + potential investors to assess a company in full knowledge — is there a new significant shareholding, who are the PDMRs, what inside information has been disclosed.

▸Post-Brexit context

Following Brexit, the UK Transparency Directive established disclosure requirements on an ongoing basis for issuers that have securities admitted to trading on a regulated market situated / operated within the UK and/or EU. This includes notification requirements for both issuers and investors in relation to the acquisition / disposal of significant shareholdings.

8.1.1

UK DTR 3% Threshold — notifiable interests

▸EU vs UK thresholds

RegimeStarting thresholdFurther thresholds
EU Transparency Directive5%10%, 15%, 20%, 25%, 30%, 50%, 75%
UK DTR (retained)3%Every whole percentage point above 3%

On implementation of the Directive, the UK decided to retain its thresholds at 3% + every percentage point above. So under UK regulation:

  • An investor is judged to have a notifiable interest in a public company if they hold 3% or more of its shares
  • Above 3%, the investor must notify the company if the holding rises or falls through a whole percentage point
  • Notify if the stake falls back below 3%
Whole percentage point — examples
• 3.7% → 4.1%: notifiable (crosses 4%)
• 3.7% → 3.9%: NOT notifiable (doesn't cross a whole point)
• 5.4% → 4.9%: notifiable (crosses 5%)
• 5.4% → 5.1%: NOT notifiable (doesn't cross a whole point)
• Any holding below 3%: not notifiable under the standard rule

▸Voting rights, not just shares

An interest is also notifiable if the 3% interest is in the form of voting rights, rather than simply holding shares. Voting rights attached to shares are now disclosable — rather than interests in shares. Voting rights may arise from holdings of financial instruments as well as shares.

▸Indirect holdings

Indirect holdings may also trigger a notification requirement when a person may be able to control the exercise of voting rights. For example, appointing a proxy — if the holding reaches the threshold and the appointment includes the power to vote at the proxy holder's discretion.

▸Connected parties

Notifiable interest rules include not only directly held shares, but also:

  • The investor's spouse
  • The investor's children (< 18)
  • Companies controlled by the investor — control is assumed if they hold ≥ 1/3 of voting rights
  • Concert parties — an agreement between 2+ persons to influence the company together (e.g. voting together). If the combined holding reaches 3%+, it becomes notifiable as if a single holding

▸Timing + process

Notification timeline
Shareholders are deemed to have knowledge of a voting rights change no later than 2 trading days following the transaction (or when it becomes unconditional).

Notification to the company — in writing within 2 business days.

If the company is listed — it must report to a primary information provider (the LSE's RNS) by the end of the following business day.

Reporting is not made to the FCA — it goes to the company / RNS.
8.1.2

Voting Rights — 6 Disregarded Categories

Voting rights attached to shares are totally disregarded in 6 cases:

1
Clearing and settlement — shares acquired for the sole purpose of clearing/settlement within the period between the transaction and the 3rd trading day following execution.
2
Custodian / bare nominee — shares held by a custodian or bare nominee. Custodians are completely exempt, provided they cannot control voting.
3
Market maker (< 10%) — shares held by a declared market maker, if less than 10%.
4
Trading book (≤ 5%) — shares held in the trading book of an investment firm / credit institution, not more than 5%.
5
Collateral — shares held as collateral.
6
Stabilisation — shares acquired for stabilisation purposes, as long as the voting rights are not exercised.

▸Fund managers / OEICs — non-beneficial rule

Non-beneficial holders — different thresholds
Fund managers + operators of regulated CIS (AUTs, OEICs) are deemed non-beneficial holders — exempt from the 3% rule. However, they are subject to disclosure:
  • 5% and 10% (but NOT in between)
  • Each percentage above 10%
8.2

PDMR Rules — Closed Periods + Chairman Approval

PDMRs were introduced in Section 3 (Market Manipulation). Here we add the DTR-related rules.

▸PDMR notification thresholds

RuleValue
Notification threshold (aggregate per year)€5,000 (MAR) / €10,000 (some sources)
Notification deadline3 business days after the transaction
NotifyIssuer + FCA

▸Closed Periods

Closed period — 30 calendar days
No trading in the issuer's instruments during the 30 calendar days BEFORE the announcement of the interim / annual financial report.

▸Chairman Approval — exceptional trading

Exceptional circumstances trading
A PDMR wishing to trade during the closed period (exceptional circumstances) must obtain prior WRITTEN approval from the company's chairman (or designated person). Oral consent is not sufficient. FCA approval is NOT required.

Examples of exceptional circumstances:
  • Severe financial difficulty requiring immediate realisation of funds
  • Employee share schemes where there is no change in beneficial ownership

▸No short-term dealing

PDMRs should not engage in short-term dealing in the issuer's instruments. The rule guards against speculative trading by directors.

8.3

Section 793 Companies Act 2006

Section 793 Letter — investigation tool
Under Section 793 of the Companies Act 2006, a UK public company can send a written notice to any person the company knows or suspects to be a shareholder, asking them to confirm that they are holding shares. The notice requests details of the shareholder's total interest in the company.

▸Information requested

The notice can request:

  • Current shareholding details
  • Past shareholdings held at any time in the last 3 years
  • Where a past interest — the identity of the person to whom the shares were sold (if known)

▸Typical recipient — nominee company

It is rare for a notice to be sent to an individual shareholder. Letters are usually sent to nominee companies that appear on the register. A nominee company = the legal owner of shares on behalf of another beneficial owner.

The letter is sent to the company secretary of the nominee company — requesting details of the true beneficial owner of the securities. Section 793 enables the registrar to identify when someone is using a nominee company to hide their identity + accumulate a substantial holding.

▸Response timing + consequences

Ignoring Section 793 — shares frozen
The notice requires a written response within a reasonable time. If requests are persistently ignored, the company can apply to court to have the shares frozen. Consequences:
  • Loss of voting rights on those shares
  • Loss of entitlement to dividends
  • Unable to sell them
No FCA involvement, not criminal — a civil court order.

The company is required to keep a record of Section 793 letters sent + information received, in a separate part of the register of shareholders.

8.4

Global context — US Corporate Transparency Act 2021

Similar disclosure requirements are being implemented globally. The US Congress enacted the Corporate Transparency Act (CTA) in 2021. The CTA requires companies to report information about beneficial owners — using a beneficial ownership registry, maintained by the US Treasury Department's Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN).

▸Required information from beneficial owners

  • Full legal name
  • Date of birth
  • Current residential / business street address
  • Non-expired US passport / US government ID / driver's licence / valid foreign passport number

▸Reporting timeline

Reporting — within 1 year of changes
Reporting must be made within 1 year of beneficial status changes:
  • Ownership exceeds or drops below 25%
  • Substantial change in company ownership
  • Change in contact details of the beneficial owner

Separately, the SEC published disclosure rules for resource extraction issuers — transparency of payments to governments.

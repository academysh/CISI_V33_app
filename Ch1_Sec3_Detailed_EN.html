CHAPTER 1
Chapter 1, Section 3 — Detailed Deep Dive
Fitness and Propriety · SM&CR · FIT · Syllabus 1.3 + 1.7.4 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 3 is about personal accountability of individuals in the financial industry. After the 2008 crisis (and the 2012 LIBOR scandal) it became clear that fining firms alone is not enough — if a bank loses billions, those were specific people making the decisions. In response, the Senior Managers and Certification Regime (SM&CR) was created — a three-component regime that (1) forces the regulator to personally approve senior managers, (2) requires firms to annually certify other risk-bearing employees, and (3) imposes Conduct Rules on everyone. Plus the Fit and Proper Test (FIT) — three criteria against which regulator/firm assess an individual. On the exam 2-3 questions — one of the most frequently tested topics.

3.1

Background — why SM&CR came about

In response to the 2007-08 banking crisis and significant conduct failings (such as the manipulation of LIBOR in 2012), the UK Government set up the Parliamentary Commission for Banking Standards (PCBS) to make recommendations on how to improve standards in the banking sector.

▸PCBS recommendations

The PCBS recommended:

  • A new accountability framework focused primarily on the accountability of senior management
  • Firms to take more responsibility for employees being fit and proper
  • Better standards of conduct at all levels in banking firms

▸Implementation — 2016 and 2018

DateEvent
2016Parliament passed legislation → FCA and PRA introduced SM&CR to the banking sector
2016Parliament introduced further changes — requiring regulators to extend SM&CR to all FSMA-authorised firms
2018Extension to insurers
2019Extension to solo-regulated firms (asset managers, stockbrokers, IFAs)
Historical context
The old regime — the Approved Persons Regime (APER) — had ~30,000 approved individuals (Significant Influence Functions, SIFs). After the crisis it became clear: too many, too diffuse, and the regulator could not actually assess the fitness of each one. The new SM&CR: fewer individuals approved by the regulator (SMFs only) + firms certify the rest themselves. A shift of responsibility: from regulator to firm.

▸Three components of SM&CR

SM&CR comprises:

  1. Senior Managers Regime (SMR) — regulatory approval for senior individuals
  2. Certification Regime (CR) — firms certify other risk-posing staff as fit and proper
  3. Conduct Rules — behavioural standards for all relevant staff
3.1.1

SM&CR scope — who is covered

SM&CR applies to all FSMA-authorised firms, EXCEPT:

  • Appointed Representatives (ARs) — they operate under the principal firm's responsibility; the principal applies SM&CR to its own business
  • Benchmark Administrators — regulated under a separate framework (Benchmarks Regulation)
Only 2 categories excluded
A frequent exam trap — "are solo-regulated firms excluded?" — NO. Solo-regulated firms are included under the proportionate regime. Insurance firms are included. Banks are included since 2016. Excluded are only ARs and Benchmark Administrators.
3.1.2

3 types of solo-regulated firms — proportionate approach

When the FCA applied SM&CR to solo-regulated firms, it implemented a proportionate regime, introducing 3 types of firms with different levels of application:

TypeWhoRequirements
Limited scope Sole traders without employees, limited permission consumer credit firms, oil/energy market participants, authorised professional firms (non-mainstream activities only) Fewer requirements
Core scope Most firms (majority) Baseline SM&CR requirements
Enhanced scope Larger solo-regulated firms (by AUM, revenue, etc.) Significantly greater requirements
Classification examples
Sole trader IFA (advisor, consultant, no staff) — Limited Scope.
Small wealth manager (50 staff, £200M AUM) — Core.
Large asset manager (£10bn AUM, 500+ staff) — Enhanced.
Major stockbroker (500+ employees) — Core or Enhanced.

▸Burden of proof

Senior managers continue to have an obligation to prevent regulatory breaches, but a senior manager will only be guilty of misconduct if the FCA is able to demonstrate that the senior manager failed to take reasonable steps to prevent the breach. The burden of proof rests with the FCA, but senior managers are expected to provide evidence of the reasonable steps they took when made aware of the breach.

"Duty of responsibility" — replaced "reverse burden"
The original SM&CR (in a 2015 draft) had a "reverse burden of proof": senior manager presumed guilty, having to prove they took reasonable steps. In 2016 this was replaced with a "duty of responsibility" — the burden of proof lies with the FCA. This is more pro-defendant.
3.2

Senior Managers Regime (SMR)

The SMR captures certain individuals performing defined Senior Management Functions (SMFs).

3.2.1

SMFs — Senior Management Functions

▸Typical SMFs

SMFs typically comprise members of the firm's senior management team:

  • Chief Executive Officer (SMF1)
  • Chief Finance Officer (SMF2)
  • Executive Director (SMF3)
  • Chief Risk Officer (SMF4)
  • Head of Internal Audit (SMF5)
  • Chair (SMF9)
  • Compliance Oversight (SMF16) — FCA function
  • Money Laundering Reporting Officer / MLRO (SMF17) — FCA function
  • Other Overall Responsibility (SMF18) — Enhanced firms only
  • Partner (SMF27)

Generally — individuals who manage direction and implement strategy of the firm. Plus other individuals who may not perform executive-level functions (head of compliance, MLRO, head of internal audit).

Division between PRA and FCA SMFs
PRA controls SMFs 1-14 for banks (executive + NED functions). FCA controls:
  • SMF16 (Compliance Oversight)
  • SMF17 (MLRO)
  • SMF18 (Other Overall Responsibility — Enhanced firms)

▸Chair and NEDs are also in scope

In line with the PRA's approach in Supervisory Statement 28/15, persons holding SMFs include the Chair and non-executive directors. They can be explicitly held to account for boardroom decisions, and deemed potentially culpable for poor decisions in the same fashion as executive directors.

Persons approved to hold an SMF must show and demonstrate increased understanding of the key business and strategic activities of the entities for which they are responsible + their associated risks.

3.2.2

Form A + 90-day clock + regulatory reference

▸Pre-approval — mandatory

An individual may perform an SMF only after the regulator has granted approved person status. The candidate completes Form A, submitted to the regulator by the firm.

Form A includes a comprehensive list of questions establishing:

  • The person's employment history
  • Fitness and propriety

In certain circumstances an abbreviated form may be completed — e.g. if the person is already approved executing a similar function.

▸Regulatory Reference — 6 years

Regulatory Reference requirement
Prior to Form A being submitted to the regulators, the firm must, under SM&CR, take all reasonable steps to obtain a regulatory reference:
  • From the candidate's current employer
  • From all previous employers within the past 6 years

▸Statement of Responsibilities

As part of the application process, firms must include a statement of responsibilities setting out the aspects of the firm's affairs for which the person will be responsible when performing the function.

Regulators expect that each responsibility is allocated to a single senior manager. Where a responsibility is shared between more than one senior manager, regulators expect the division of responsibility and accountability to be clearly articulated.

▸90-day statutory clock

90 days — statutory determination deadline
FSMA imposes a statutory obligation on the FCA and PRA to reach a determination on a person's fitness and propriety within 90 days of receiving Form A. The actual time varies and depends on additional checks.

If regulators consider they need further information from the firm, they stop the statutory "clock" until the requested information is received.

3.2.3

Prescribed Responsibilities + Management Responsibilities Map

▸Prescribed Responsibilities (PRs)

Prescribed Responsibilities must be allocated to the most senior individuals of those performing SMFs. Captured individuals' key duties are recorded as part of the individual statement of responsibilities, describing the most senior executives' roles and demonstrated through:

  • Day-to-day management
  • Adding scope and accountability to current roles
  • Control and information arrangements
Examples of Prescribed Responsibilities
• Responsibility for performance of the firm's obligations under the SMR
• Responsibility for performance of the firm's obligations under the Certification Regime
• Responsibility for financial crime / AML
• Responsibility for implementation of the Consumer Duty
• Responsibility for compliance with CASS rules
Each PR must have one named SMF accountable.

▸Management Responsibilities Map

The Management Responsibilities Map documents the firm's management and governance arrangements — including how prescribed responsibilities have been allocated. Purpose:

  • Enhanced transparency of individual accountability
  • Increases reporting line and quality
  • Specifies management information requirements

For Core firms — simplified version. For Enhanced firms — comprehensive, mandatory.

3.3

Certification Regime (CR)

▸Firm certifies — not the regulator

Key difference SMR vs Certification
SMR: individuals approved by the regulator (Form A → 90 days → approval).
CR: individuals NOT approved by the regulators — rather, firms make the assessment that they are "fit and proper" and provide an annual certificate.

This introduces an administrative and evidential burden on firms, not regulators.

▸Frequency — recruitment + annually

The CR requires firms to assess fitness and propriety of certain employees:

  • At the recruitment stage
  • Annually thereafter

The firm's senior management must attest annually that individuals captured by the Certification Regime remain fit and proper through arrangements designed and managed by the firm.

▸Who is in scope — "significant harm functions"

The CR captures employees who could pose a risk of significant harm to the firm or its customers. Different approaches between PRA and FCA:

RegulatorApproach
PRALimits scope to "material risk takers" (MRTs), defined in the Capital Requirements Regulation (CRR) — i.e. individuals subject to the Remuneration Code ("Code staff")
FCAWider approach — includes individuals performing a function from the UK or dealing with UK clients

▸Examples of Significant Harm Functions

The FCA approach includes individuals who:

  • Previously performed a significant influence function (SIF) role under APER, but are now outside the SMR (not SMF) — for example:
    • CASS oversight officer
    • Benchmark submitter or administrator
    • Proprietary trader
    • Previous CF29 significant manager function-holders
  • Material risk takers (Code staff)
  • Functions requiring qualifications (investment advisers)
  • Managers of certification employees
  • Client-dealing functions (some)
"Code staff"
The term "code staff" refers to employees whose activities have a material impact on the firm's risk profile. These are employees subject to the Remuneration Code (CRR): bonus deferral rules, clawback, proportional bonus cap.

▸UK nexus requirement

Where an individual is based overseas and does not deal with UK clients and is performing a Certification function (other than MRT), they will not be a certified person.

▸Failed certification

What to do if an individual is not fit and proper
If a certificate is not issued because the individual is deemed not to meet fitness/propriety criteria, the firm must:
  • Give written notice explaining what steps (if any) it proposes to take
  • Notify the FCA of the circumstances
Dismissal is not automatic — the firm must take positive action.
3.4

Conduct Rules — 5 individual + 4 Senior Manager

▸General structure

Conduct Rules are a framework of behavioural standards against which individual conduct is judged. Applicable to all individuals EXCEPT ancillary staff (cleaners, catering staff). The regulator must be notified of breaches + any formal disciplinary action taken.

Conduct Rules structure
INDIVIDUAL CONDUCT RULES (all relevant staff)
5 rules (or 6 if Consumer Duty applies)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
SENIOR MANAGER CONDUCT RULES
+4 additional rules (SC1-SC4) ONLY for Senior Managers

▸Individual Conduct Rules (all relevant staff)

Rule 1
You must act with integrity.
Rule 2
You must act with due skill, care and diligence.
Rule 3
You must be open and cooperative with the FCA, PRA and other regulators.
Rule 4
You must pay due regard to the interests of customers and treat them fairly.
Rule 5
You must observe proper standards of market conduct.
Rule 6
You must act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers. (New rule under Consumer Duty — FCA-only firms)
Rule 4 vs Rule 6 — mutually exclusive
Following implementation of the Consumer Duty, Rule 6 applies to individuals working for a firm subject to the Consumer Duty. In that case Rule 4 ceases to apply (no overlap).

Rule 6 does NOT apply for firms out of scope of the Consumer Duty — therefore Rule 4 continues for those firms. Only 5 individual conduct rules apply.

▸Senior Manager Conduct Rules (additional for SMFs)

SC1
You must take reasonable steps to ensure that the business of the firm for which you are responsible is controlled effectively.
SC2
You must take reasonable steps to ensure that the business of the firm for which you are responsible complies with relevant requirements and standards of the regulatory system.
SC3
You must take reasonable steps to ensure that any delegation of your responsibilities is to an appropriate person — and you oversee discharge of the delegated responsibility effectively.
SC4
You must disclose appropriately any information of which the FCA or PRA would reasonably expect notice.

▸Breach notification obligation

Always notify the regulator of breaches
Conduct Rules breach + formal disciplinary action → MUST notify the regulator. This applies to ALL staff, not only senior managers. Even a junior analyst — if they breach Rule 1 (integrity) and the firm disciplines them — the FCA is notified.

▸COCON — where the Conduct Rules live

Additional details of SM&CR, including the list of SMR applicable functions and prescribed responsibilities, are located in the FCA's SYSC (Senior Management Arrangement, Systems and Controls) sourcebook:

  • SYSC 23 — Senior managers and certifications: Introduction and classification
  • SYSC 24 — Allocation of prescribed responsibilities
  • SYSC 25 — Management responsibilities maps and handover procedures
  • SYSC 26 — Overall and local responsibility
  • SYSC 27 — Certification regime
  • COCON — contains the Code of Conduct Rules + guidance
3.5

Fit and Proper Test (FIT) — 3 criteria

▸Who does the assessment

Individuals performing SMFs within authorised firms must be approved by the regulator as fit and proper before taking on the function. The PRA and FCA may grant an application if satisfied that the candidate is a fit and proper person to perform the function applied for.

Responsibility lies with the firm making the application — to satisfy the regulator(s) that the candidate is fit and proper.

▸The three main FIT criteria

In assessing fitness and propriety, regulator(s) will look at factors against a set of criteria, of which the most important are the person's:

  1. Honesty, integrity and reputation
  2. Competence and capability
  3. Financial soundness

▸(1) Honesty, Integrity, Reputation

The FCA will have regard to whether the person has been:

  • Convicted of a criminal offence — particular consideration given to offences of fraud, dishonesty, financial crime
  • Subject to an adverse finding or settlement in a civil case — particular consideration for cases involving financial businesses and fraud
  • Subject to previous investigation or disciplinary proceedings by the FCA, PRA, another regulatory authority, including previous regulators
  • Subject to a justified complaint in relation to regulated activities
  • Refused a licence to trade, or had a licence/registration revoked
  • Involved in an insolvent business
  • Disqualified as a director, or dismissed from a position of trust

▸(2) Competence and Capability

The FCA considers:

  • Whether the person meets relevant training and competence requirements
  • Whether the person has demonstrated due skill, care, diligence in their work history
  • Whether the person has experience relevant to the SMF applied for
  • Whether the person has the qualifications required (RDR, professional standards)

▸(3) Financial Soundness

The FCA considers whether the person has been:

  • Declared bankrupt
  • Subject to a judgement debt or award (especially unpaid)
  • Entered into an Individual Voluntary Arrangement (IVA)
  • Made any arrangements with creditors (e.g. composition)
Financial soundness ≠ wealth
The FCA does not require a senior manager to be wealthy. Financial soundness is about how the person has handled financial obligations in the past. Bankruptcy or unpaid debts may indicate poor judgment — and therefore raise concerns about fitness to manage a firm's affairs.

▸Non-exhaustive criteria

The list is NOT exhaustive
The criteria above are examples of the type of information the regulator will want. This does not constitute a definitive list of matters that might be relevant. The regulator can consider anything that raises concerns about fitness.
FIT assessment example
The firm applies to approve a candidate for the CEO role (SMF1). The candidate has:
• MBA and 20 years' experience ✓ Competence
• £2M house, stable financial position ✓ Financial soundness
• But: fined in civil court £100K for misrepresentation in a previous role (even if long ago) ⚠️

The regulator is entitled to refuse approval even if everything else is fine — on the basis of honesty/reputation. In the worst case — a prohibition order (ban from the industry).

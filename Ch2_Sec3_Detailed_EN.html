Chapter 2, Section 3 — Detailed Deep Dive
Accepting Clients · Client Categorisation · Syllabus 2.3 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
This is one of the most content-packed sections in the chapter — and one of the most important on the exam (expect 3-5 questions). It governs how a firm must "label" its clients — retail, professional, or eligible counterparty (ECP). The category determines the level of protection the client receives: from maximum (retail) to minimum (ECP). The section is very "numerical" — you need to memorise many thresholds in euros and pounds.

INTRODUCTION

Why client categorisation exists at all

The core idea of UK financial services regulation is to protect those who need protecting. An ordinary pensioner putting their savings into shares and a Dutch pension fund with €100bn under management need entirely different levels of protection. The pensioner needs to be shielded from everything: unsuitable products, aggressive promotions, hidden fees. The pension fund knows the market better than many brokers.

That is why the FCA (following MiFID) divides all clients into three categories with different levels of protection.

MORE PROTECTION
Retail Client
Maximum protection: full COBS rules, FOS, FSCS, suitability, appropriateness
MEDIUM LEVEL
Professional Client
Core rules apply, but fewer protective requirements
LESS PROTECTION
Eligible Counterparty (ECP)
Minimum protection: many COBS rules disapplied (see Section 1)
◄ — — — MORE PROTECTION · LESS PROTECTION — — — ►
The key categorisation principle
"Any client is a retail client unless they meet the criteria of a professional investor or eligible counterparty." This is a favourite exam question phrased as "what is the overriding principle". Retail is the default, residual category. When in doubt — treat as retail.

When a client is classified correctly, everyone is happy: ECPs get fast service without unnecessary paperwork, retail clients get the full protection package. When classified incorrectly (e.g. a retail client "upgraded" to professional just to sell them a complex product), this can be a serious FCA breach.

3.1

When categorisation is required

A firm must categorise its clients if it carries on designated investment business. In other words: a pure deposit-taking bank — no categorisation needed. Any kind of investment business — yes.

▸MiFID vs Non-MiFID — two parallel systems

MiFID laid down the categorisation rules for MiFID business. For non-MiFID business, the FCA adopted the same terminology (retail / professional / ECP) but modified the criteria in some places. The result is two parallel systems with identical terminology but different thresholds.

The mixed-business rule
If a firm provides both MiFID and non-MiFID services to the same client — it must use the MiFID categorisation, unless the MiFID business is conducted separately. The logic: the stricter system applies by default to avoid confusion.
Mixed-business example
An adviser simultaneously advises a client on:
1. Buying units in a UCITS fund (MiFID service)
2. A life insurance policy (NOT MiFID)

Result: must use MiFID categorisation for both services. If the insurance business were done in a separate subsidiary, by a separate team, under a separate contract — then non-MiFID categorisation could be used for the insurance.

▸3.1.1 Definition of a Client

COBS defines a "client" as a person to whom a firm provides, intends to provide, or has provided a service in the course of regulated activity. For MiFID business, it also includes any ancillary service.

Very important: the definition is not limited to those with signed contracts. It covers:

  • Potential clients — people the firm is in discussions with but hasn't started working with yet.
  • Persons acting as agents for another (default rule: the agent is the client, see 3.2).
  • For financial promotion rules — any persons the firm communicates with, whether or not they are actually clients.
  • Clients of an appointed representative (AR) or tied agent — also treated as clients of the principal firm.
Example of the broad client definition
Brokerage firm "BrokerCorp" sends a marketing email about a new ETF product to a database of 10,000 potential clients. None of them are currently BrokerCorp clients — no contracts. But for financial promotion rules purposes, all 10,000 people are "clients" — and the full protective machinery (fair, clear, not misleading) applies to them.
The term "Customer" — umbrella term but not complete
The FCA Handbook uses the term "customer" (do not confuse with "client"!). It covers retail + professional. ECPs are not included in "customer". Some rules are written for "customers" (i.e. apply to retail and professional), others for all "clients" (including ECPs).
3.1.2

The three client categories — overview

Category Protection level Typical examples
Retail Client Maximum Individual, small business, anyone not professional and not ECP
Professional Client (per se or elective) Medium Banks, investment firms, pension funds, large companies
Eligible Counterparty (ECP) (per se or elective) Minimum Banks, central banks, governments, regulated institutional players (but NOT large undertakings)
Key logic of sub-categories
Both professional and ECP have two sub-categories:
Per se = "by definition", "automatic", based on the nature of the client itself (e.g. a bank is already a professional simply because it is a bank).
Elective = "by choice", "on request", when a client asks for a lower level of protection and passes the required tests.
3.1.2 pt. 2

Per se Professional Clients — in detail

These are clients who are professional "by definition", without having to prove anything. The COBS list is exhaustive.

▸Group 1: Regulated financial institutions

  • Credit institution (a bank)
  • Investment firm
  • Any other authorised / regulated financial institution
  • Insurance company
  • CIS or management company of such a scheme (collective investment scheme)
  • Pension fund or management company of a pension fund
  • Commodity or commodity derivatives dealer
  • Any other institutional investor

▸Group 2: Large Undertakings — a key exam topic

These are companies which are large and sophisticated enough to count as professional without being a financial institution. But the rules are different for MiFID and non-MiFID business.

For MiFID business For Non-MiFID business
Meets any 2 of 3:
• Balance sheet ≥ €20m
• Net turnover ≥ €40m
• Own funds ≥ €2m 		Alternative tests (any one):
Option A: Called-up share capital or net assets ≥ £5m (now or in the past 2 years)

Option B: Any 2 of 3:
• Balance sheet ≥ €12.5m
• Net turnover ≥ €25m
• 250 employees on average during the year
Exam — don't mix up the numbers!
MiFID: €20M / €40M / €2M (in euros, any 2 of 3).
Non-MiFID alternative 1: £5M share capital / net assets (in pounds).
Non-MiFID alternative 2: €12.5M / €25M / 250 employees.
These are among the most frequent numerical exam questions.

▸Special cases for Non-MiFID

  • Partnership or unincorporated association: net assets ≥ £5m (now or in past 2 years). For limited partnerships — calculated without deducting loans from partners.
  • Trustee of a trust (other than pension): assets ≥ £10m (now or in past 2 years).
  • Trustee of a pension scheme (occupational, SSAS, personal, stakeholder): ≥ 50 members AND assets under management ≥ £10m (now or in past 2 years).
Pension trust — BOTH conditions must be met
50 members + £10m assets. Not "or" — "AND". A trust with 60 members and £5M assets is NOT per se professional. A trust with 40 members and £20M assets is also NOT per se professional.

▸Group 3: Government bodies

  • National / regional governments
  • Certain public bodies
  • Central banks
  • International / supranational institutions (World Bank, ECB, IMF, etc.)

▸Group 4: Institutional investors

Institutional investors whose main business is investment in financial instruments.

Example — a small UK company
A small UK company with net assets of £200,000 approaches an investment firm for equity investment advice. How should it be classified?

Answer: Retail. It does NOT meet the MiFID large undertaking thresholds (€20M/€40M/€2M), and does NOT meet the non-MiFID £5M test. Companies are not automatically professional just because they are companies — they have to pass the tests. If they don't pass — retail by default.
3.1.2 pt. 3

Eligible Counterparties (ECP) — in detail

ECP is the highest category, with the lowest level of protection. The ECP list is very similar to the per se professional list, but with one key difference.

KEY DIFFERENCE: ECP vs Per se Professional
ECP = per se professional MINUS large undertakings.

That is, a large company that passed the tests to become per se professional does NOT automatically become an ECP. It remains professional. This is a much-loved exam question.

▸Full list of per se ECPs

  • Credit institution
  • Investment firm
  • Another financial institution authorised / regulated under UK or EEA-state law (including regulated institutions in banking, insurance, and securities sectors)
  • Insurance company
  • CIS authorised under the UCITS Directive, or its management company
  • Pension fund or its management company
  • National government or its corresponding office (including the public-debt body)
  • Supranational organisation
  • Central bank
  • Undertaking exempted from MiFID under Article 2(1)(k) (certain own-account commodity/commodity-derivatives dealers) or Article 2(1)(l) (locals)

▸ECP status applies only to specific services

Very important: ECP status only applies when the firm is providing the client with a narrow set of services:

  1. Dealing on own account
  2. Execution of orders on behalf of clients
  3. Reception and transmission of orders
  4. Ancillary services directly related to the above
  5. Arranging in relation to business which is not MiFID or equivalent third country business

If an ECP wants investment advice or portfolio management — they must be classified as per se professional for those services. ECP status does not extend to them.

Example: insurance company wants advice
Insurance company "InsuranceCo" is classified as an ECP. It approaches a brokerage firm for investment advice on portfolio diversification. How should it be classified for this service?

Answer: Per se professional, not ECP. Because investment advice is not on the list of ECP-eligible services. The company is still a professional — it cannot suddenly be dropped to retail. But the full protective package for professionals applies (including suitability).
Why some ECPs want more protection
Some clients who would be per se ECPs may prefer a higher level of protection and ask to be treated as professionals. This is called recategorisation to a higher level of protection, see 3.3.1.
3.2

Agents — the "agent as client" rules

What happens if person A approaches a firm but is acting as an agent for person B? Who is the firm's client — A or B?

The default rule and its exceptions
DEFAULT:
Person A (the agent) → served by the firm
Firm knows A is acting for B
A is treated as the firm's client (the agent)

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
EXCEPTIONS (B becomes the client):
① Firm and A have agreed in writing
   to treat B as the client.
② A's involvement is mainly designed
   to reduce the firm's duties towards B
   → B is still the client.

Default rule: if the firm knows that A is acting as agent for B, A is treated as the firm's client.

Exception 1: the firm may agree in writing with A that B will be treated as the client instead. This is permitted.

Exception 2 (anti-avoidance): if A's involvement in the arrangement is mainly designed to reduce the firm's duties towards B — then B is still the client regardless. This protects against schemes where unscrupulous firms use agents as a "screen" to avoid their duties to the ultimate beneficiary.

Example: family office as agent
Family office "FamilyOffice Ltd" manages funds for the Smith family and approaches a broker to execute trades. Under the default rule, the broker's client is FamilyOffice (the agent). But they may agree in writing that the Smith family itself will be the client — then the broker owes its duties directly to the Smiths, not through an intermediate link.
3.3

Recategorising Clients — to a lower level of protection

Section 3.3 governs the situation where a retail client wants to become professional, or a professional wants to become ECP. This is reducing the protection level, so the FCA sets strict conditions.

▸Elective Professional Clients — from retail to professional

A retail client may become an "elective professional" if they pass:

Two tests for elective professional
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ TEST 1: QUALITATIVE │
│ Firm assesses the client's expertise, │
│ experience, knowledge and believes │
│ they can make their own investment │
│ decisions and understand the risks │
│ → REQUIRED for both MiFID and │
│ non-MiFID business │
└─────────────────────────────────────────┘
      + (MiFID only)
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ TEST 2: QUANTITATIVE │
│ Any 2 of 3: │
│ • 10+ significant transactions/quarter │
│ over past 4 quarters │
│ • Portfolio > €500,000 │
│ (cash + financial instruments) │
│ • ≥ 1 year in financial sector │
│ in a relevant role │
└─────────────────────────────────────────┘
A very important nuance
For MiFID business → BOTH tests are needed (qualitative + quantitative).
For non-MiFID business → ONLY the qualitative test is needed.
Quantitative thresholds (10 trades, €500K, 1 year experience) apply only to MiFID.
MiFID example: Maria
Maria wants to become an elective professional for MiFID services. Her details: 12 trades per quarter over the past year, €300K portfolio, 3 years as a compliance officer at a bank.

Quantitative test:
• 12 > 10 trades → ✓ PASS
• €300K < €500K → ✗ FAIL
• 3 years at a bank → ✓ PASS
Total 2 of 3 → PASS. If the firm also confirms the qualitative test — Maria becomes an elective professional.
MiFID example: James
James wants to become an elective professional. His details: €600K portfolio, 2 years as an analyst, but only 3 trades per quarter.

Quantitative test:
• 3 < 10 trades → ✗ FAIL
• €600K > €500K → ✓ PASS
• 2 years as analyst → ✓ PASS
2 of 3 → PASS.

▸The written procedure — three steps

Even when the client passes the tests, the reclassification procedure is strictly formal:

  1. The client writes to the firm: requesting reclassification, specifying the services/products the reclassification should apply to.
  2. The firm responds with a written warning: clearly setting out what protections the client will lose.
  3. The client confirms in a separate written document: that they understand the consequences of losing those protections.
What a retail client loses by becoming elective professional
The client loses the following protections:
  • Communications rules (fair, clear, not misleading)
  • Information about the firm, its services, and remuneration
  • Suitability assessment
  • Appropriateness (for non-advised services)
  • Dealing / carrying out orders — best execution rules apply in a limited way
  • Reporting information to clients
  • Remuneration and incentivisation of staff rules
  • Client reporting
  • Investor compensation (FOS and FSCS) — critically important
  • Exclusion of liability (limits on firm's liability)
  • Share Trading Obligation

▸The client's duty to keep the firm updated

After becoming an elective professional, the client is obliged to inform the firm of any changes in their circumstances that may affect their categorisation. If the firm becomes aware that the client no longer meets the conditions, it must take action. If that means moving the client back to retail, the firm must notify them of the new categorisation.

▸Elective Eligible Counterparties — from professional to ECP

A professional client may become an "elective ECP" if:

  • It is an undertaking (an organisation, not a natural person)
  • It is a per se professional client (but NOT one who is only professional because they are an institutional investor)
  • It requests ECP treatment
3.3 pt. 2

Local Authorities — special regime post-MiFID II

Another very important exam topic. MiFID II introduced extra protection for local authorities and municipalities, because in the past they lost huge sums on complex derivative products they didn't understand (see the famous Hammersmith & Fulham case in the 1990s, for instance).

Key MiFID II change
Local authorities are RETAIL CLIENTS by default.
Previously they could be professional. Now — only retail. They can be moved to elective professional, but with tougher thresholds.

▸Special tests for local authorities

The qualitative test is the same as for ordinary retail clients (adequate assessment of expertise/experience/knowledge).

The quantitative test for a local authority is special:

Ordinary retail (MiFID) Local authority (MiFID)
Portfolio > €500,000 Portfolio > £10,000,000 (in pounds, not euros!)
PLUS one of the following:
Any 2 of 3 criteria Any 1 of 3:
(a) 10+ trades per quarter over past 4 quarters
(b) Person authorised to carry out transactions on behalf of the authority has ≥1 year in the financial sector in a professional role
(c) Authority is an administering authority of the LGPS (Local Government Pension Scheme)
Exam — don't confuse the thresholds
Ordinary retail: €500,000 and 2 of 3.
Local authority: £10,000,000 (!) and 1 of 3 (including the special LGPS route).
The local authority threshold is in pounds and over 20x higher in value.
Example: local authority with LGPS role
A local authority with a £12m portfolio, also serving as the administering authority of the LGPS, wants to become an elective professional.

Check:
• Portfolio £12M > £10M → ✓ PASS
• LGPS administering authority → ✓ PASS (one of three alternatives)
→ Quantitative test passed. Plus the qualitative test (adequate assessment of competence) is required — and then reclassification is possible.
3.3.1

Recategorising to a Higher Level of Protection

The other direction: a professional wanting to become retail, or an ECP wanting to become professional/retail. This is increasing protection, so the procedure is simpler.

▸Who may request it

  • Professional clients may request to be treated as retail.
  • ECPs may request to be treated as professional or retail.

▸Firm's own initiative

The firm may on its own initiative (or at the same time as a client's request):

  • Treat per se professional clients as retail clients
  • Treat per se ECPs as professional or retail clients

▸Different categorisations for different instruments

A firm may classify a client differently for different financial instruments. Recategorisation can be:

  • On a general basis (for the whole portfolio)
  • On more specific terms — for example, for one specific transaction only
Practical note
In practice, most firms classify a client once for all financial instruments. Different categorisations for different instruments are theoretically possible, but they create complex internal procedures and risk of errors. Simpler to have one category.

▸Notification obligations

New clients must be notified of their categorisation BEFORE services are provided. The firm must also advise of:

  • The client's right to request a different categorisation
  • Any limits on protections resulting from the current classification

▸Record-keeping

The firm must:

  • Have written internal policies on categorisation
  • Record each notice and agreement on categorisation
  • Retain the records for the relevant period after ceasing business with the client
📋 Final summary of Section 3
Key numbers for the exam
MiFID large undertaking: €20M / €40M / €2M (2 of 3)
Non-MiFID large undertaking: £5M share cap/net assets
Non-MiFID alt. 2-of-3: €12.5M / €25M / 250 employees
Trust (non-pension): £10M assets
Pension trust: 50 members + £10M assets
Elective professional (MiFID): 10 trades / €500K portfolio / 1 year experience (2 of 3)
Local authority: £10M + 1 of 3 (including LGPS)
  • 3 categories: Retail (max protection) / Professional (per se or elective, medium protection) / ECP (per se or elective, min protection).
  • Categorisation required for designated investment business. For mixed MiFID+non-MiFID — MiFID rules apply.
  • Retail = default. Any client is retail unless professional or ECP.
  • "Customer" (FCA Handbook term) = retail + professional. ECPs NOT included.
  • Per se professional: regulated financial institutions + large undertakings (by tests) + governments/central banks + institutional investors.
  • ECP = per se professional MINUS large undertakings. Key exam distinction.
  • ECP status applies only to: dealing on own account + execution + reception/transmission + related ancillary services + arranging (non-MiFID). For advice and portfolio management — classify as professional.
  • Conflicts of interest (SYSC 10) always apply, even for ECPs.
  • Agents (3.2): default — agent is client. Exceptions: written agreement + anti-avoidance rule.
  • Elective professional (MiFID): qualitative + quantitative tests (2 of 3). Non-MiFID: qualitative only.
  • Elective professional procedure = 3 written steps: (1) client request, (2) firm warning, (3) client's separate written confirmation.
  • Local authorities post-MiFID II: retail by default. Elective professional only with £10M portfolio + 1 of 3 (including LGPS administering authority).
  • Higher protection level: simpler procedure, firm can initiate. May be general basis or per-transaction.
  • Notifications: new clients BEFORE services + advise of right to recategorise + limits on protections.
  • Records: written policies + record all notices + retain after ceasing business.

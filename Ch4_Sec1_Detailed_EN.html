What is this section about?
Chapter 4 — Complaints and Redress — covers the UK's consumer protection framework. Section 1 is the Consumer Rights Act 2015 (CRA), consolidating consumer protection laws. The Act establishes: (1) statutory rights for consumers in contracts for goods, digital content, services; (2) statutory remedies when rights are breached; (3) reform of the unfair terms regime in consumer contracts. The FCA is a qualifying body under the CRA — it challenges unfair terms in financial services consumer contracts. Also in this section: cancellation rights (14 calendar days for non-life / non-pension; 30 for life and pension) + Consumer Duty (Principle 12). 2-3 exam questions, often about unfair terms, the grey list, and cancellation periods.

1.1

CRA 2015 — 3 parts + aims

The Consumer Rights Act 2015 (CRA) is a UK law that consolidates consumer protection laws, particularly for contracts involving goods, digital content, services. The Act outlines statutory rights + remedies available when rights are breached.

▸Aims of the CRA

The CRA is designed to
Enhance consumer confidence + simplify enforcement:
  • Give consumers statutory rights relating to consumer contracts for goods / digital content / services
  • Give consumers statutory remedies when those rights are breached
  • Reform + consolidate the law relating to unfair terms in consumer contracts (Part 2)

▸3 parts

1
Part 1 — consumers' rights and remedies in relation to contracts for the supply of goods, digital content, services.
2
Part 2 — provisions relating to unfair terms in consumer contracts and notices.
3
Part 3 — miscellaneous and general provisions, including investigatory powers for consumer law enforcers + provisions relating to secondary ticketing.
1.2

Unfair Terms — definition + grey list

▸Definition of an unfair term

Unfair term — test
A term in a consumer contract is unfair if, contrary to the requirement of good faith, it causes a significant imbalance in the parties' rights and obligations to the detriment of the consumer.

▸Individually negotiated — also covered

Applies to individually negotiated terms too
The CRA explicitly states that unfair-terms provisions apply even to individually negotiated terms. There is no blanket exemption. This differs from the earlier UTCCRs 1999, under which individually negotiated terms were often exempt.

▸How fairness is assessed

Fairness is determined by considering:

  • The nature of the subject matter of the contract
  • All circumstances at the time the term was agreed
  • All other terms of the contract (and related contracts)

▸The grey list — indicative, non-exhaustive

The CRA "grey list"
The CRA contains an indicative and non-exhaustive list of terms that may be regarded as unfair — the "grey list". Being on the grey list does not automatically make a term unfair — fairness must be determined on the facts.
Grey list examples
• Terms allowing a firm to unilaterally change the contract without a valid reason
• Terms excluding liability for death / personal injury
• Terms forcing a consumer to bear an unreasonable burden of proof
• Terms giving the firm a right not available to the consumer

▸Transparency requirement

Transparency failure ≠ automatically unfair
The CRA requires terms to be transparent (plain / intelligible language). However, if a term fails the transparency test, it is not automatically unfair. Unfairness must still be determined, considering all relevant factors.
1.3

FCA as Qualifying Body

FCA powers under the CRA
Under the CRA, the FCA has statutory power to challenge unfair terms in financial services consumer contracts. The FCA is deemed a "qualifying body" under the Unfair Terms in Consumer Contracts Regulations 1999 (now CRA 2015).

▸Available FCA actions

The FCA can:

  • Seek undertakings from firms to amend or remove unfair terms
  • Apply to court for an injunction to prevent use of such terms against future customers
  • Publish information about unfair terms / the firms using them
Injunctions — limitations
Important nuance: an injunction prevents the firm using the term against FUTURE customers, but does not retroactively invalidate existing contracts. Existing customers with unfair terms must pursue redress through other routes (complaints / FOS).
1.4

Cancellation Rights — 14 / 30 days

FCA cancellation rights apply to:

  • Firms providing retail financial products based on designated investments / deposits
  • Distance contracts with consumers relating to deposits / designated investments

▸Cancellation periods

Product type / contextCancellation period
Life and pension products (advised non-distance)30 calendar days
Life and pension products (distance contracts)30 calendar days
Non-life / non-pension products (advised non-distance)14 calendar days
Non-life / non-pension products (distance contracts)14 calendar days

▸How to exercise cancellation

Consumer notification
The consumer must notify the firm within the cancellation period. A notification dispatched BEFORE the deadline is sufficient, even if received AFTER the deadline. This protects consumers from postal delays.

▸Impact of market movements

The cancellation shortfall
If the consumer invokes cancellation rights on an investment product (e.g. a UCITS fund), they may not get back the full invested amount. They receive the proceeds from the sale of their holding — impacted by price movement of the fund's underlying holdings. The market may have moved against them.

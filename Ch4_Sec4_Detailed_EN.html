Whistleblowing · PIDA 1998 · SYSC 18 · Syllabus 4.4 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
The final section of the book — covering whistleblowing (reporting wrongdoing to the authorities). Two main sources of rules: (1) the Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998 (PIDA) — protects workers who make protected disclosures from detriment or dismissal; (2) FCA / PRA rules — in SYSC 18, setting out the requirements for firms. Key concepts: protected disclosure, qualifying wrongdoing, reasonable belief, prescribed regulator (the FCA is such a regulator), and the whistleblowers' champion (mandatory for banks + Solvency II insurers). 1-2 exam questions, often about qualifying wrongdoing, reasonable belief, disclosure routes, and the role of the champion.

4.1

PIDA 1998 — overview + qualifying wrongdoing

Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998 (PIDA)
PIDA 1998 protects individuals from retaliation when they inform regulatory authorities of concerns that come to their attention in the workplace. Generally referred to as "whistleblowing". Protects workers who make "protected disclosures" from detriment or dismissal.

▸Qualifying wrongdoing — 6 categories

For a disclosure to be protected under PIDA, it must relate to qualifying wrongdoing:

1
Criminal offence — committed / being committed / likely
2
Health and safety danger
3
Environmental damage
4
Miscarriage of justice
5
Breach of legal obligation
6
Concealment — any of the above being deliberately concealed
NOT qualifying wrongdoing
Management performance decisions (e.g. a denied promotion, negative performance review) are NOT qualifying. PIDA is about legal / regulatory / ethical wrongdoing — not internal employment grievances.
4.2

Protected Disclosure — reasonable belief + 3 routes

▸Reasonable belief — not certainty

Standard — reasonable belief
The worker need not be correct about the wrongdoing. The standard is "reasonable belief" that the information tends to show wrongdoing. If it later turns out there was no wrongdoing, the worker is still protected as long as the belief was reasonable.

▸3 disclosure routes

1
Internally — to the employer (usually the preferred first route)
2
Prescribed regulator — e.g. the FCA, PRA, ICO. The worker can go directly to the regulator without prior internal reporting and still receive PIDA protection
3
Wider public disclosure — e.g. press / MPs — only in specific circumstances (higher threshold) — reasonable belief + no other route suitable + a pattern of concealment
FCA — prescribed regulator
The FCA is a prescribed regulator under PIDA. Workers in financial services can make a protected disclosure directly to the FCA without first reporting internally and receive PIDA protection. The FCA operates its own confidential whistleblowing service.
4.2.1

Remedy — Employment Tribunal

Remedy if the worker suffers detriment / dismissal
A worker who suffers detriment or dismissal for making a protected disclosure can:
  • Bring a claim before an Employment Tribunal
  • No cap on compensation for dismissal claims (unlike standard unfair dismissal, which has a cap)

▸False / malicious disclosures

PIDA does not protect false / malicious disclosures
Nothing prevents firms from taking action against workers who make false and/or malicious disclosures. PIDA protection is only for genuine protected disclosures based on reasonable belief, not deliberate false allegations.
4.3

FCA / PRA Rules — SYSC 18 (7 requirements)

▸Background — PCBS 2013

In 2013, the Parliamentary Commission on Banking Standards (PCBS) recommended that banks:

  • Put in place additional mechanisms for employees to raise concerns internally
  • Appoint a senior person to take responsibility for the effectiveness of those arrangements

▸FCA / PRA whistleblowing rules (SYSC 18)

The FCA and PRA whistleblowing rules require firms to establish, implement and maintain appropriate + effective arrangements for the disclosure of reportable concerns. 7 requirements:

1
Effective handling — of disclosures of reportable concerns, including:
  • Confidentiality requested / anonymity
  • Range of communication methods
2
Escalation — effective assessment + escalation to the FCA / PRA where appropriate
3
Anti-victimisation measures — reasonable measures to ensure that no individual under the firm's control engages in victimisation of the whistleblower
4
Feedback — provide the whistleblower with feedback on the reportable concern where feasible + appropriate
5
Records + procedures:
  • Appropriate records of reportable concerns + the firm's treatment (incl. outcome)
  • Up-to-date written procedures readily available to UK-based employees
6
Reports:
  • Annual report to the governing body / board on the operation + effectiveness of systems / controls. Maintain confidentiality of individual whistleblowers
  • Prompt reports to the FCA / PRA for each case the firm contested but lost at an Employment Tribunal, where the claimant successfully based the claim on:
    • Detriment suffered as a result of a protected disclosure (s.47B Employment Rights Act 1996)
    • Unfair dismissal (s.103A ERA 1996)
7
Training:
  • UK-based employees
  • Managers of UK-based employees (wherever the manager is based)
  • Employees operating the firm's internal arrangements
4.3.1

Whistleblowers' Champion

▸Which firms must appoint

Mandatory appointment under SM&CR
Certain firms are required to appoint a whistleblowers' champion:
  • Banks with deposit-taking permission
  • Insurance companies subject to Solvency II

▸Seniority

The FCA expects most firms to appoint a non-executive director (NED) as champion. If there is no NED, a senior manager is acceptable. For insurers — a director / senior manager is expected.

▸Responsibilities of the champion

The champion is responsible for:

  • Integrity of the firm's whistleblowing policies
  • Independence of processes
  • Effectiveness of arrangements
  • Overseeing the handling of disclosures
  • Reporting to the board
  • Protecting whistleblowers from victimisation

▸Access + authority

The champion must have
Sufficient authority + independence:
  • Access to independent legal advice
  • Training
  • Information required to perform the function

▸No operational role required

No operational role required
The champion does not need a day-to-day operational role. They can be based anywhere, as long as they can effectively perform the function. Often it is a NED with only an oversight role.

▸Board reporting + tribunal losses

  • Annual board report on the operation + effectiveness of whistleblowing systems. Confidentiality of whistleblowers is maintained
  • Prompt FCA notification — the firm lost an Employment Tribunal where the claimant successfully based the claim on whistleblowing detriment / dismissal
SM&CR whistleblowing summary
• Who must appoint a champion: deposit-taking banks + Solvency II insurers
• Seniority: NED (or senior manager if no NED)
• No day-to-day ops role needed
• Annual board report on effectiveness
• Prompt FCA report on lost tribunals

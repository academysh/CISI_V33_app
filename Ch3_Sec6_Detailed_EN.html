Chapter 3, Section 6 — Detailed Deep Dive
The Bribery Act 2010 · Syllabus 3.6 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
The Bribery Act 2010 is one of the toughest anti-bribery laws in the world. It creates 4 main offences (Sections 1, 2, 6, 7) plus interpretive Sections 3, 4, 5. Key features: (1) extraterritorial application — it applies to any commercial organisation carrying on business in the UK; (2) Section 7 — corporate strict liability offence with the only defence being "adequate procedures"; (3) facilitation payments are prohibited (unlike some US FCPA exceptions). Penalties: 10 years prison for individuals, unlimited fine for corporates. The MoJ published 6 principles of adequate procedures. 1-2 exam questions, often scenarios about facilitation payments, hospitality, or the 4 offences.

6.1

Overview — 4 offences

The Bribery Act 2010 creates 4 offences:

1 (s.1)
Offering a bribe — offering, promising or giving a bribe to another person
2 (s.2)
Receiving a bribe — requesting, agreeing to receive, or accepting a bribe from another person
3 (s.6)
Bribing a foreign public official
4 (s.7)
Corporate failure to prevent bribery — strict liability offence for commercial organisations

▸Territorial scope

Extraterritoriality
The Bribery Act applies to activities outside the UK if a firm carries on business in the UK — even if the activities have no direct link to the UK. A foreign company with a UK office can be caught for bribery in Asia or Africa.
6.2

Sections 1-7 — Detailed

s.1
Bribing another person — a person offers, gives or promises to give a financial or other advantage to another individual in exchange for improper performance of a relevant function/activity.
s.2
Being bribed — defined as requesting, accepting, or agreeing to accept such an advantage in exchange for improper performance of such a function/activity.
s.3
Relevant function or activity — definition:
  • "Any function of a public nature;
  • any activity connected with a business, trade or profession;
  • any activity performed in the course of a person's employment;
  • any activity performed by or on behalf of a body of persons, whether corporate or unincorporated"
Applies to both private and public industry. Activities outside the UK — included even without a link to the country.
s.4
Improper performance — activity is considered improperly performed when:
  • The expectation of good faith or impartiality is breached
  • The function is performed in a way not expected of a person in a position of trust
s.5
Standard of a reasonable person in the UK — the standard expected of a reasonable person in the UK, including what might be expected of a person in such a position.

If the breach occurred in a jurisdiction outside the UK — local practices or customs should be disregarded unless they form part of the written law of the jurisdiction. Written law = constitution / statute / judicial opinion set down in writing.
s.6
Bribery of foreign public officials — a specific offence targeting corruption in international business deals.
s.7
Corporate failure to prevent bribery — a commercial organisation fails to prevent persons associated with it from bribing. Strict liability — the company is guilty even if senior management did not know. Applies to all commercial organisations carrying on business in the UK — regardless of size.
Section 7 — the only defence
The only defence to Section 7 is "the organisation had adequate procedures" in place to prevent bribery. This is the basis of MoJ guidance + the 6 principles (below).
6.2.1

Penalties

OffenceMax penalty
Sections 1, 2, 6 (individuals)10 years' imprisonment + unlimited fine
Section 7 corporate offenceUnlimited fine (no prison — it's corporate)
Almost any size of bribe
There is no minimum threshold. Any financial or other advantage — if offered with intent to bribe — is caught.
6.3

MoJ 6 Principles + Hospitality + Facilitation Payments

▸MoJ 6 Principles of Adequate Procedures

The Ministry of Justice published statutory guidance — 6 principles defining "adequate procedures" for the s.7 defence:

1
Proportionate procedures — anti-bribery procedures must be proportionate to the bribery risks faced, the nature/scale/complexity of activities.
2
Top level commitment — senior management actively demonstrates the organisation does not tolerate bribery. Personal demonstration + a clear message to staff/partners.
3
Risk assessment — periodic, informed, documented assessment of the nature/extent of exposure to potential external + internal bribery risks.
4
Due diligence — knowing exactly who the firm is dealing with — conducting checks before engaging third parties. Protects from associating with less trustworthy persons.
5
Communication (including training) — communicating anti-bribery policies to staff and others — enhancing awareness + deterring bribery. Includes regular training.
6
Monitoring and review — bribery risks and the effectiveness of procedures may change over time. Firms should keep their steps under review.

▸Corporate Hospitality

When hospitality is NOT bribery
Corporate hospitality does not amount to a bribe if it:
  • Has a legitimate business aim
  • Is reasonable
  • Is proportionate
  • Is appropriate in the circumstances
There is no fixed monetary threshold. Context matters — a £100 dinner may be fine; £500 football tickets for a foreign official before a tender — already a problem.

▸Facilitation Payments

Facilitation payments — an offence in the UK
Facilitation payments — small payments to government officials to expedite routine actions (e.g. processing permits, customs clearance).

Under the Bribery Act, this is an OFFENCE, unless:
  • Made under threat of violence / duress
  • Permitted by the written law of the foreign official's country
Unlike the US FCPA, which has an explicit carve-out for facilitation payments, the UK takes a zero tolerance approach. This is a major compliance risk for multinationals.
Scenario — facilitation payment
A UK company's employee in Nigeria offers $50 to a customs officer to speed up cargo release. That is a facilitation payment. Under Nigerian written law — it is NOT permitted. The employee committed an offence + the company is potentially guilty under s.7 (unless it has adequate procedures).

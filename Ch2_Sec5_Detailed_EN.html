Chapter 2, Section 5 — Detailed Deep Dive
Communicating with Clients, including Financial Promotions · Syllabus 2.6 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 5 is one of the largest and most exam-heavy sections in COBS. It governs all firm communications with clients — and especially financial promotions (marketing and advertising of investment products). The central standard here is: fair, clear and not misleading. This is not just "good manners" — it's a hard regulatory requirement. Expect 5-7 exam questions, with many nuances about cold calls, past performance, real-time vs non-real-time, FPO exemptions, and anti-greenwashing.

INTRODUCTION

Why communication and financial promotion rules exist

The FCA Handbook contains 12 Principles for Businesses (covered in Chapter 1, Section 2). These Principles apply to all regulated activities, including dealing as principal, ancillary activities, home finance, insurance, deposits — and to the communication and approval of financial promotions.

The common theme of the communication rules is fair play, together with recognition of the information imbalance between firm and client. The firm usually knows far more about its products than its clients. The COBS rules aim to level this playing field.

Principles for Businesses relevant to communications
Two key Principles directly relate to this section:
Principle 6 (Customers' interests): a firm must pay due regard to the interests of its customers and treat them fairly.
Principle 7 (Communications with clients): a firm must pay due regard to the information needs of its clients and communicate information to them in a way which is fair, clear and not misleading.
The detailed rules in COBS 4 are the implementation of these two Principles.

What is a financial promotion? Under FSMA, it is any invitation or inducement to engage in investment activity. That's a huge definition — it covers everything: newspaper ads, email campaigns, push notifications, video ads, social media posts, flyers, telemarketing, conference presentations, influencer posts, and so on.

5.1

Application of the rules on communicating with clients

A firm must ensure that all communications relating to designated investment business, including financial promotions, are fair, clear and not misleading.

This fundamental rule applies:

  • To communications with any category of client (retail, professional, and even ECP — although some rules are disapplied for ECPs, the basic honesty standard remains)
  • To all types of communications — verbal, written, electronic
  • To all designated investment business
5.1.2

Fair, Clear and Not Misleading — the central standard

This is the central standard of the whole section. The way it is satisfied must be appropriate and proportionate, taking into account the type of communication and the information being conveyed.

▸Difference in standard between client types

The principle is the same — but the presentation must differ:

For whom Level of detail
Professional client Can be more detailed and complex — client is assumed to understand technical terminology
Retail client Should be less detailed but clearer — with explanation of key concepts
Important nuance
This does NOT mean that for professional clients you can "hide" risks in a long technical wall of text. It means that technical language is acceptable — but the fair/clear/not misleading standard itself stays the same.

▸Specific rules for financial promotions

Firms must ensure:

  • If the client's capital may be at risk — this must be made clear. It cannot be hidden or softened.
  • If yields are quoted — a balanced impression must be given of short-, medium- and long-term prospects for the investment.
  • If the product is complex or multiple elements of remuneration apply — this must be communicated fairly, clearly and not misleadingly, taking account of the information needs of the recipient.
  • If the FCA is named as the firm's regulator, but the communication also covers non-FCA-regulated matters — it must be made clear which aspects are not FCA-regulated.
  • Promotions about packaged or stakeholder products not produced by the firm itself must give a fair, clear and non-misleading impression of the producer or manager of the product.
Example of a fair/clear/not misleading breach
A promotional email for a structured product: "Your capital grows with the market — with no downside risk." If capital is actually at risk (structured products often have a barrier beyond which capital is lost), this is a blatant breach. The fact that capital is at risk must be prominently and explicitly stated.
Example on "balanced impression" of yield
A promotion says: "Fund yield — 15% per annum!" But this is the result of one lucky year. A balanced impression is needed: what about 3, 5, 10-year returns; what a "bad" year looks like; how volatile it has been. Cherry-picking a single outstanding result is misleading.
Code of Conduct for advertising deposit accounts
FCA guidance notes that firms may take into account the Code of Conduct for the Advertising of Interest-Bearing Accounts, produced by UK Finance and the Building Societies Association (BSA), when drafting financial promotions for deposit accounts.
5.1.3

Identifying Promotions — types of promotions

Firms must ensure that financial promotions they communicate or approve, and which are addressed to clients, are clearly identifiable as such.

There are exceptions: for example, if the financial promotion is made to a professional client or ECP — it does not need to be labelled as a promotion.

▸Types of communication

Type Examples Note
Real-time Face-to-face meetings, telephone conversations, presentations, interactive dialogue Happens live; no initial record
Unsolicited real-time (aka cold calls) Cold calls, uninvited visits Real-time + client did not request it
Non-real-time Emails, letters, websites Usually a record exists; client reads at own convenience
Direct offer financial promotion Promotions making an offer to enter into an agreement + form of response or specified response method "Click here to buy" — can be executed without further firm contact
Classification example
An email to a client with an offer to buy a fund and a link to an online order form is simultaneously non-real-time (email) AND a direct offer (offer + response mechanism).
A phone call where an analyst discusses a structured product is real-time. If the client did not request the call — it's additionally a cold call (unsolicited real-time).
5.1.4

Anti-Greenwashing Rule — protection against "green deception"

The anti-greenwashing rule is a relatively new piece of regulation that came into effect in 2024. It was introduced because of a widespread practice where firms brand their products as "green", "sustainable", or "ESG-friendly" without any real basis for the claim.

▸To whom it applies

The rule applies to all FCA-regulated firms when undertaking client communications, marketing, or making statements about the sustainability characteristics of a product or service.

It applies regardless of where the communication was developed or written — if it is distributed by an FCA-regulated firm to its clients (both retail and professional), the FCA rules apply.

▸Purpose of the rules

To ensure that sustainability claims are:

  • Consistent with the actual sustainability characteristics of the products/services
  • Fair, clear and not misleading

▸FCA's four criteria for sustainability statements

Any sustainability references should be:

  1. Correct and capable of being substantiated.
  2. Clear and presented in a way that can be understood.
  3. Complete — they should not omit or hide important information and should consider the full life cycle of the product or service.
  4. Comparable — accurate in comparisons with other products/services.
What "client communications" covers
Client communications include: statements, assertions, strategies, targets, policies, information and images. Even a picture of a green forest on the homepage of a fund's site could fall under this rule, if it creates the impression that the fund is "green".

▸Relationship with other rules

The FCA notes that the anti-greenwashing rule is intended to complement and be consistent with existing Handbook rules (Principles for Businesses, COBS, Consumer Credit). It does not replace them.

The FCA also explicitly links anti-greenwashing with Consumer Duty — the rule is consistent with the requirement that firms act in good faith towards retail customers.

Example of greenwashing
A fund markets itself as the "Eco-Friendly Growth Fund" with wind turbines on its website. In reality, 40% of the portfolio sits in oil and gas companies, of which only 5% invest in "green" technologies. This is obvious greenwashing. The name and imagery create an impression that doesn't match the portfolio. Breaches: criterion (1) "not substantiated" and (3) "misses important info".
5.2

Exceptions to the Financial Promotion Rules

The financial promotion rules are disapplied in certain cases. These are called "excluded communications". They are communications that are:

  • Aimed at high-net-worth individuals (Article 48 of the FPO) and sophisticated investors (Articles 50 and 50A of the FPO), or
  • Exempt under the Financial Promotion Order (FPO) — promotions exempt from the regime if communicated by an unauthorised person, or originating outside the UK and which cannot have an effect within the UK, or
  • From outside the UK, and would be exempt under the FPO if the office from which they are communicated were a separate unauthorised person (even though it is not), or
  • Subject to (or exempted from) the Takeover Code, and
  • One-off promotions that are not cold calls
Key limitation
The exemption for excluded communications does NOT generally apply to UK MiFID business. So for MiFID business, the core financial promotion requirements are almost always retained.

▸2024 HM Treasury updates

In November 2023, HM Treasury published its consultation response on exemptions for HNW and sophisticated investors. Changes that came into effect in 2024:

  • Requirement for businesses to provide details of themselves in any communications using the exemptions
  • Updating the title of the certified high-net-worth individual exemption — dropping the word "certified"
  • Updating the investor statements for HNW individuals and self-certified sophisticated investors
  • Applying these changes to the equivalent exemptions for promotions of collective investment schemes
Important practical effect of the 2024 changes
Investor statements that complied with the previous requirements are NO LONGER VALID. Investors must sign new statements under the updated criteria.
FPO — what it is
The Financial Promotion Order (FPO) is secondary legislation under FSMA that contains a long list of cases where the general prohibition in FSMA on promotions by unauthorised persons does not apply. The FPO is a fine-tuning tool: it lets specific categories of sophisticated investors or specific situations (takeovers, one-off contacts) operate without the full regulatory burden.
5.3

Prospectus Advertisements

COBS 4 (the part about communications to clients) does not apply where the Prospectus Regulation Rules (PRR 3.3) apply. This is a separate regulatory regime for public offerings of securities.

▸When PRR 3.3 applies

PRR 3.3 applies to offers, or admission to trading, of transferable securities where:

  • A prospectus is required under Section 85 FSMA, or
  • An election has been made to produce a prospectus under Section 87 FSMA

A prospectus advertisement is any type of communication (TV, press, email, letter, text, brochure) that specifically promotes the offer to subscribe for or acquire securities.

▸4 requirements for a prospectus advertisement

An advertisement relating to such offers or admissions to trading must not be issued unless it:

  1. States that a prospectus has been (or will be) published, and where investors can obtain it.
  2. Is clearly recognisable as an advertisement.
  3. The information in it is not inaccurate or misleading.
  4. Is consistent with the information that is, or is required to be, in the prospectus.
Mandatory statement in a written advertisement
Any written prospectus advertisement must prominently and in bold state that it is NOT a prospectus but an advertisement, and that investors should not subscribe to the securities except on the basis of the information in the prospectus.
Example of a prospectus advertisement
A newspaper ad: "New Growth Capital plc — IPO on the LSE Main Market. Shares offered at £2.50. The full prospectus is available at www.ngc-ipo.com."
Must also contain, in bold: "This is not a prospectus but an advertisement. Investors should make decisions only on the basis of the information in the full prospectus."
5.4

Approval of Financial Promotions — for unauthorised persons

Under FSMA, an unauthorised person cannot communicate a financial promotion itself. But if an authorised firm approves such a promotion (using the "gateway"), the unauthorised person may then distribute it.

▸Duties of the approving firm

COBS requires: before an authorised firm approves a financial promotion for an unauthorised person, it must confirm compliance with the financial promotion rules.

If the firm later becomes aware that the promotion no longer complies:

  • It must withdraw its approval
  • It must notify everyone known to be relying on that approval, as soon as is reasonably practicable
Approval rules — same as for own promotions
The rules for approving a financial promotion apply in the same way as if the firm were communicating the promotion itself. That is, the approving firm carries the same responsibility for the content as the author.

▸Restrictions on type of promotion

Firms may NOT approve real-time financial promotions — i.e. promotions to be made in the course of personal visits, telephone conversations or other interactive dialogue. Only non-real-time promotions can be approved for unauthorised persons.

▸Unregulated collective schemes

Authorised firms are restricted in the promotion of unregulated collective schemes. They may approve a financial promotion for such a scheme only if they would be able to legitimately promote it themselves — for example, through an exemption or in compliance with specific rules.

▸Limited approvals

If a firm approves a financial promotion for which some financial promotion rules are disapplied, it must do so on the basis that the approval is limited to those circumstances.

For example, if approval is for communication only to professional clients or ECPs — this limit must be stated. If an unauthorised person then communicates the promotion outside that limited approval, they are committing an offence.

▸The 2023 Gateway — a fundamental change

Before 2023: under FSMA, any authorised person could approve financial promotions for unauthorised persons.

From 2023: the FCA introduced a "gateway" — firms wishing to approve promotions for third-party (unauthorised) firms must obtain specific FCA permission. Firms without this permission can only approve their own promotions.

Influencers and social media
The FCA has been clear: influencers who promote financial products on social media without approval from an FCA-authorised firm (with the right permission) could be committing a criminal offence. Firms are responsible for all their promotions, including those by influencers they work with.
5.4.2

Firms Relying on Promotions Approved by Another Party

What if a firm is neither the author nor the approver of a promotion — but wants to communicate it further? This is a common scenario in distribution chains.

For non-MiFID business, a firm is not in breach of the rules if it communicates a financial promotion produced by another party, where three conditions are met:

  1. Takes reasonable care to establish that another authorised firm has confirmed the promotion complies with the rules.
  2. Takes reasonable care to communicate the promotion only to the type of recipient intended at the time of confirmation.
  3. As far as the firm is (or should be) aware, the promotion is still fair, clear and not misleading, and has not been withdrawn by the other party.
Example: distributor of a non-MiFID product
A distributor firm receives marketing material from a fund provider (non-MiFID). The distributor did not create the material, did not approve it, but wants to send it to its clients. To avoid a breach:
1. Verify that another authorised firm (in this case the provider) has confirmed compliance with the rules.
2. Distribute only to the intended client type (e.g. retail only).
3. Continue to monitor — if the promotion becomes misleading or is withdrawn, stop distribution.
5.5

Communicating with Retail Clients — additional rules

For retail clients, additional stricter rules apply. When a firm provides information about designated investment business, or issues / approves a financial promotion that is likely to be received by a retail client, it must comply with a set of additional COBS rules.

▸General principle

The additional requirements cover:

  • Clarity of wording and structure
  • Mandatory risk warnings
  • Balance of positive and negative aspects of the investment
  • Rules on comparisons and figures
  • Restrictions for specific types of investment (speculative illiquid securities)

▸Restrictions on Speculative Illiquid Securities (SIS)

Speculative illiquid securities are high-risk, low-liquidity securities (often mini-bonds and similar products). Following a series of high-profile scandals (London Capital & Finance and others), the FCA significantly restricted their promotion to retail clients.

Key restrictions: ban on mass-marketing SIS to retail clients; promotion permitted only to specific categories (certified sophisticated, HNW, restricted investors); strict risk warnings.

▸Risk warning for high-risk investments

A financial promotion for a high-risk investment to a retail client must carry a mandatory risk warning, including the phrase: "investors could lose all of the money they invest". This is the standard wording prescribed by COBS 4.

5.6

Past, Simulated Past and Future Performance

Performance figures are one of the most powerful persuasion tools in promotions. And one of the easiest areas to mislead in. The rules are strict.

▸Past Performance — actual historical returns

If a promotion presents actual past performance:

  • A prominent warning must be included that past performance is not a reliable indicator of future performance
  • Information about the relevant period and source must be given
  • If the figures are gross (without costs) — the effect of commissions, fees, tax and other charges must be disclosed

▸Simulated Past Performance

For a new product with no track record, the firm may show simulated performance — but under rules:

  • The simulation must be based on actual past performance of one or more investments or indices that are the same as, or underlie, the investments being simulated.
  • A prominent warning must state that: (a) the figures are simulated past performance, AND (b) past performance is not a reliable indicator of future performance.
Example of simulated past performance
A new smart-beta ETF launches in 2026. It has no real track record. The firm may show a "backtest": what would have happened if this ETF had existed from 2015, applying the same selection rules — it would have returned X%. That is simulated. It must explicitly say: "THESE FIGURES ARE SIMULATED — they are not the fund's actual history. Past performance is not a reliable indicator of future performance."

▸Future Performance — projections

Projections of future returns must be based on:

  • Reasonable assumptions
  • Supported by objective data

Projections MUST NOT be based on, or refer to, simulated past performance.

Why simulated past cannot be used for future projections
Simulated past is "what would have been". Future projection is "what will be". Mixing them creates the illusion that a modelled past = expected future return. This is an obvious manipulation of the client and is therefore directly prohibited.
5.7

Direct Offer Financial Promotions

A direct offer financial promotion is a promotion containing an offer to enter into an agreement AND a form of response, or a specified method of responding. The client can accept the offer directly, without a separate interaction with the firm.

▸8 mandatory information categories

A direct offer promotion likely to be received by a retail client must include prescribed information on:

  1. The firm and services
  2. Investment management (if relevant)
  3. Safekeeping of assets
  4. Costs and charges
  5. Nature and risks of the investment
  6. Prospectus statement (if applicable)
  7. Combined investment risk explanation
  8. Third-party guarantee details

▸Exception: reference to another document

Required information elements need not be included in the promotion itself if the client can refer to another document (e.g. a prospectus) containing that information in order to respond to the offer.

Example: direct offer with a reference
An IPO prospectus for company X with a tear-off share subscription form is a classic direct offer. Rather than duplicating 80 pages of prospectus into a marketing flyer, the flyer can contain summary information + a reference: "See the Prospectus, available at www.xyz.com/prospectus, for the full prescribed information." This is lawful, provided the client actually has access to the full prospectus before making the decision.
5.8

Cold Calls, Unwritten Promotions and Overseas Persons

▸5.8.2 Unsolicited Real-Time Communications (Cold Calling)

"Cold calling" is the common term for the practice of authorised or exempt persons contacting people without a prior appointment, with a view to communicating a financial promotion.

Firms must not make cold calls, except in three cases:

  1. Existing client who expects such a call: the recipient has an existing client relationship with the firm and would envisage or expect to receive such a call.
  2. Generally marketable packaged product: the call relates to a generally marketable packaged product that is NOT a higher-volatility fund or a life policy linked to such a fund.
  3. Controlled activity with limited investments: the call relates to a controlled activity involving a limited range of investments — including deposits and readily realisable investments, other than warrants or generally marketable non-geared packaged products.
High-volatility funds — outside the cold call exemption
Even for an existing client, the firm CANNOT cold-call them about a high-volatility fund or a life policy linked to such a fund. This is an explicit exception to the packaged product permission. Warrants are also outside the exemption.

▸Unwritten Promotions — 4 conditions

If a firm initiates an unwritten promotion (verbal, face-to-face) to a specific person outside its premises, 4 conditions must be met:

  1. Appropriate time of day.
  2. Identify self and firm, state purpose of the call/visit.
  3. Clarify if the client wants to continue or terminate (and terminate if requested).
  4. Give a contact point if an appointment is arranged.

▸5.8.3 Overseas Persons

Firms are NOT permitted to communicate or approve financial promotions for overseas firms, unless the promotion:

  • States which firm has approved / communicated the promotion
  • (If relevant) explains:
    • That the rules for the protection of investors will not apply
    • The extent to which the UK compensation scheme is available (and if it is not, that fact)
    • If the communicator wishes — details of any overseas compensation and/or deposit protection scheme
Fundamental requirement
A firm must not communicate and/or approve a promotion unless it has no doubt that the overseas firm will deal with UK retail clients honestly and reliably. Formal approval is not enough — real due diligence on the overseas partner is required.
Example: promoting a US fund to UK retail clients
A UK firm wants to promote a US-based fund to UK retail clients. The promotion must contain: (a) the name of the UK firm that approved/communicates it; (b) notice that US investor protection rules (SIPC etc.) may not apply to UK investors; (c) that the UK FSCS does not cover the fund; (d) if there is overseas compensation — its details. The UK firm must also conduct due diligence on the US provider to be satisfied of their reliability.

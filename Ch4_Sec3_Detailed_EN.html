Chapter 4, Section 3 — Detailed Deep Dive
Data Protection · DPA 2018 · UK GDPR · Syllabus 4.3 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 3 covers data protection. The key sources of UK law are the Data Protection Act 2018 (DPA 2018) (which replaced DPA 1998) + the UK GDPR (the retained post-Brexit version of EU GDPR). They work together — the DPA 2018 supplements the UK GDPR and covers areas not covered by GDPR (law enforcement, national security). The regulator is the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO). 1-2 exam questions, often on the 6 DPA 2018 principles, the 7 UK GDPR principles, data subject rights, 72-hour breach notification, and the retention rule "no longer than necessary".

3.1

DPA 2018 — scope + 6 principles

▸Background — DPA 1998 → DPA 2018

The Data Protection Act 1998 provided how personal data must be dealt with, protecting the rights of persons concerned. Personal data relates to living individuals who are identifiable by that data.

Following the implementation of EU GDPR in 2018, DPA 1998 was repealed and replaced by the new UK DPA (Data Protection Act 2018).

▸DPA 2018 — wider remit than GDPR

Why the DPA 2018 is broader than GDPR
The DPA 2018 has a wider remit than GDPR:
  • Part dealing with processing not falling within EU law — e.g. immigration. Applies GDPR standards, amended for UK national context
  • Part that transposes the EU Data Protection Directive 2016/680 (Law Enforcement Directive) into UK domestic law — Part 3 sets out requirements for processing personal data for criminal "law enforcement purposes"
  • National security — outside EU law. UK provisions based on the Council of Europe Data Protection Convention 108 apply to intelligence services
  • Separate parts covering the ICO (duties, functions, powers) + enforcement provisions

▸6 DPA 2018 Principles

1
Lawful and fair — processing must be lawful and fair.
2
Specified purposes — purposes of processing must be specified, explicit, legitimate.
3
Adequate + relevant — personal data must be adequate, relevant, not excessive.
4
Accurate + up to date — personal data must be accurate and kept up to date.
5
Not kept longer than necessary — personal data kept for "no longer than necessary".
6
Processed in a secure manner — personal data must be processed securely.
Classic exam question — retention
A favourite in sample papers: "How long should personal data be held?" Answer = "No longer than necessary" (Principle 5). Not "indefinitely", not "3 years", not "5 years" — concrete numbers are wrong.
3.2

ICO — Information Commissioner's Office

ICO mission
The ICO's mission: "to uphold information rights in the public interest, promoting openness by public bodies and data privacy for individuals".

▸ICO role

  • Safeguarding + empowering individuals
  • Fostering responsible innovation
  • Promoting transparency
  • Ensuring compliance with data protection + e-privacy legislation

▸Registration requirement

Data controllers — must register with the ICO
Data controllers (those that collect + process personal data) must register with the ICO. This is an annual obligation + fee.
3.3

UK GDPR — scope + 7 principles

▸Post-Brexit retention

Following Brexit, the GDPR requirements were retained in UK domestic law as the UK GDPR. The high-level requirements remained unchanged.

▸Scope — key differences from the old DPA

AspectDPA (old)UK GDPR
Applies toOnly controllersBoth controllers + processors
ExtraterritorialNoYes — non-UK controllers/processors if offering goods/services or monitoring UK data subjects
AccountabilityImplicitExplicit obligation to demonstrate compliance
Data exportLimited rulesAllowed if compliant with UK GDPR conditions

▸DPOs — Data Protection Officers

Mandatory DPO designation
The UK GDPR makes it mandatory for certain organisations to designate a DPO:
  • Public authorities / bodies
  • Organisations that regularly + systematically monitor data subjects on a large scale

▸7 UK GDPR Principles

1
Lawfulness, Fairness, Transparency — processing must have a valid basis, be fair, be transparent to data subjects
2
Purpose limitation — data collected for specified, explicit, legitimate purposes; not further processed in an incompatible manner
3
Data minimisation — data adequate, relevant, limited to what is necessary
4
Accuracy — data accurate + kept up to date
5
Storage limitation — kept for no longer than necessary
6
Integrity + confidentiality (security) — appropriate security measures
7
Accountability — the controller is responsible + must demonstrate compliance with all other principles
DPA 6 vs UK GDPR 7 principles
DPA 2018 has 6 principles. UK GDPR has 7 principles. The difference: UK GDPR adds "Accountability" as an explicit principle. A classic exam trap is distinguishing between the two regimes.
3.4

Rights of individuals — 7 rights under UK GDPR

Under the UK GDPR, data subjects have specific rights regarding their personal data:

  • Right to be informed — about how data is being used
  • Right of access — to access their data (Subject Access Request)
  • Right to rectification — correct inaccurate data
  • Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten") — in certain circumstances
  • Right to restrict processing
  • Right to data portability — receive / transfer data
  • Right to object — to processing

▸Right to erasure — when it applies

"Right to be forgotten" conditions
The right to erasure applies when:
  • Data is no longer necessary for the original purpose
  • Consent is withdrawn (if consent was the basis)
  • Individual objects + no overriding legitimate grounds
  • Unlawful processing
  • Legal erasure obligation
3.5

6 Lawful Bases for Processing

Principle 1 (lawfulness) requires a lawful basis for processing. The UK GDPR lists 6 lawful bases:

1
Consent — clear, affirmative, specific, informed
2
Contract necessity — processing necessary for a contract with the data subject
3
Legal obligation — compliance with a legal obligation
4
Vital interests — protecting vital interests of the data subject / another person
5
Public task — performing a task in the public interest / official authority
6
Legitimate interests — pursued by the controller / third party, not overridden by rights
Examples of bases in practice
• Bank KYC check → legal obligation (MLR 2017)
• Online subscription form → contract necessity
• Marketing emails → consent (GDPR requires opt-in)
• Fraud prevention → legitimate interests
3.6

Breach Notification

▸72-hour ICO notification

Strict deadline — 72 hours
Notifiable personal data breaches must be reported to the ICO within 72 hours of becoming aware. "Notifiable" = the breach is likely to result in a risk to the rights + freedoms of individuals.

▸Individual notification — HIGH risk only

Notify affected individuals
Data subjects must be notified when the breach is likely to result in HIGH risk to their rights + freedoms. Higher threshold than the ICO notification.
Who to notifyWhenThreshold
ICOWithin 72 hours"Risk" to rights
Data subjects"Without undue delay"HIGH risk to rights
Scenario
A bank leaks 1,000 customer records (names, addresses, account numbers) via a phishing attack.
→ ICO notification — within 72 hours (risk to rights is present).
→ Individual notification — if HIGH risk, e.g. financial fraud is likely.

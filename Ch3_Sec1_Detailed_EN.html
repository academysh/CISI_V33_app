CHAPTER 3
Chapter 3, Section 1 — Detailed Deep Dive
Market Abuse · Syllabus 3.1 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 1 of Chapter 3 is the foundation of all UK market integrity regulation. Market abuse is the deliberate distortion of how financial markets work: insider dealing, manipulation, false disclosure. The seriousness: it is a statutory offence under FSMA. The UK has a dual regime: civil (under UK MAR — administrative penalties by the FCA) + criminal (under CJA 1993 / FS Act 2012 — criminal prosecution). Key building blocks: UK MAR (onshored from the EU after Brexit), the MAR Sourcebook (FCA guidance), and STORs (Suspicious Transaction and Order Reports — firms must report to the FCA). Chapter 3 has around 19 exam questions; 3-4 of them come from Section 1.

1.1

The Statutory Offence — origin and scope

Market abuse is a statutory offence covering financial market manipulation and insider dealing. It is a serious offence that damages investor confidence and the integrity of financial markets.

Market abuse — origin
The UK market abuse regime (an offence under the Financial Services and Markets Act (FSMA)) came into force in 2001. This was the first comprehensive UK-wide market abuse prohibition.

▸Why market abuse is such a threat

Market abuse undermines:

  • Investor confidence — if investors believe the market is manipulated, they exit
  • Market integrity — prices stop reflecting fundamental value
  • Market efficiency — capital is misallocated; cost of capital rises
  • Fairness — insiders profit at the expense of ordinary investors
Why a statutory offence
While market abuse was only a civil wrong, the preventive deterrent was insufficient. Making it a statutory offence means: (1) the FCA has formal enforcement powers; (2) criminal prosecutions are possible; (3) civil penalties rest on a legislative basis — not on private contract.
1.1.1

Implementation of the Market Abuse Regulation (MAR)

▸Timeline

DateEvent
2001UK market abuse regime came into force under FSMA
2016Market Abuse Regulation (MAR) came into effect — updating and enhancing the existing EU Market Abuse Directive (MAD)
2019Market Abuse (Amendment) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019 — prepared for Brexit
2020+Post-Brexit UK MAR — onshored from the EU

▸What MAR changed in 2016

MAR updated and enhanced the EU MAD, for example by:

  • Extending scope — to include financial instruments traded on platforms other than regulated markets (bilaterally or OTC)
  • Adapting rules for new technology and specific behaviours
  • Clarifying that market abuse across spot commodity and related derivative markets is prohibited
  • Reinforced cooperation between financial and commodity regulators
  • Ensuring regulators have the information needed to detect and sanction market abuse
  • Tailored rules for SME issuers — so costs don't become a barrier to accessing financial markets
The MAR Sourcebook after implementation
Following MAR implementation, the FCA updated the Handbook to provide guidance on matters governed by MAR. It does not take the form of binding rules as it previously did. The Handbook only provides guidance where the FCA decided it was appropriate. The Handbook is not the sole source for provisions relating to market abuse.
1.1.2

Post-Brexit — UK MAR + 3 key amendments

Following Brexit, the UK "onshored" (also referred to as "retained" or "assimilated" EU law) the EU MAR into the UK statute book. The provisions remain largely unaltered. However, MAR is now referred to as UK MAR.

▸3 key amendments post-Brexit

Market Abuse (Amendment) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019
These regulations ensure similar EU MAR requirements and protections are in place after Brexit. 3 key amendments:
1
No obligation to share information with EU authorities without a guarantee of reciprocity. Before Brexit — automatic data exchange with EU regulators. After — only where reciprocal.
2
The FCA took over regulatory responsibilities previously held by ESMA (European Securities and Markets Authority) and the EC (European Commission). The UK now has complete regulatory autonomy.
3
Supplementary legislation is subject to HM Treasury — not the European Commission. HMT is now the policy authority for any additions.
Practical effect of Brexit
Firms trading cross-border between the UK and EU now have to comply with both — UK MAR (for UK operations) and EU MAR (for EU operations). This created a dual compliance burden. However, the substance of the rules is very similar — both derived from the same framework.
1.2

FCA Market Conduct Handbook Guidance — Civil & Criminal Regime

▸Dual regime structure

The UK — dual regime
The UK has TWO parallel regimes for market abuse:
CIVIL: UK MAR + MAR Sourcebook. FCA administrative penalties, public censure. Standard of proof — balance of probabilities.
CRIMINAL: CJA 1993 (insider dealing) + FS Act 2012 Sections 89-91 (misleading statements). Criminal court prosecution. Standard of proof — beyond reasonable doubt. Sentences — prison.

The FCA can choose — civil or criminal.

▸FSMA alongside insider dealing legislation

The first 2 types of behaviour potentially constituting market abuse — insider dealing and improper disclosure — are likely to give a false or misleading impression of supply/demand/value of the investments concerned.

They are to an extent already covered by UK legislation:

  • Insider dealing — the Criminal Justice Act 1993 (CJA) — see Sec 2
  • Misleading statements / practices — Sections 89-92 of the FS Act 2012
1.2.1

Behaviours constituting Market Abuse

The MAR Sourcebook includes aspects of insider dealing + expands on the behaviours constituting market abuse + specifies the civil actions available to the FCA.

▸Typical market abuse hallmarks

Market abuse behaviours are normally behaviours that are likely to give a false or misleading impression of:

  • Supply of investments
  • Demand for investments
  • Value of investments

▸FCA civil actions

The MAR Sourcebook specifies the civil actions the FCA can take in respect of firms — and which may also be taken against senior managers and other individuals:

  • Financial penalties
  • Public censure
Public censure — why it is effective
Public censure helps promote high standards of regulatory / market conduct — both:
  • Deterring firms/individuals from committing further breaches
  • Warning others
A financial firm's reputation depends on public trust. Censure can be far more damaging than a fine.
1.2.2

FS Act 2012 — Sections 89-91 + 3 Defences

The Financial Services Act 2012 introduced 3 offences relating to the making / carrying on of misleading statements and practices — replacing Section 3.97 of FSMA:

89
Making misleading statements — Section 89. Deliberately making false statements to induce another person to enter into a financial transaction.
90
Creating false or misleading impressions — Section 90. Causing impressions of the market/price of financial instruments that are false or misleading.
91
Making misleading statements relating to benchmarks — Section 91. After the LIBOR scandal of 2012 — a specific offence around benchmark manipulation (LIBOR, EURIBOR, FX fixes).

▸3 potential defences

A person accused under Sections 89-91 has 3 possible defences:

1
Reasonable belief — the person reasonably believed that their act / conduct would not create an impression that was false or misleading.
2
Price stabilisation rules — actions / statements / forecasts made in conformity with the FCA's price stabilisation rules. These rules allow market participants (investment banks) to support the price of a new issue of securities for clients — aim preventing the market from being excessively volatile. The rules require certain disclosures + restrict the support to a particular period.
3
Control of information rules — actions / statements / forecasts made in conformity with the FCA's control of information rules. These rules relate to statements made on the basis of limited information. The remainder may be known to the firm but rests behind "information barriers" (Chinese walls) — not known to the relevant individual.
Practical example — Chinese walls defence
An M&A banker at a firm knows about an upcoming acquisition. An equity research analyst at the same firm publishes a bullish note on the target — without knowing about the M&A. If the analyst defends themselves — acted in conformity with the control of information rules (Chinese wall separated M&A and research) — that is a valid defence.
1.2.3

FS Act 2021 — new penalties

The Financial Services Act 2021 introduced new maximum sentences for insider dealing and financial services offences (misleading statements under the FS Act 2012).

Conviction typeOffences up to 31 Oct 2021Offences from 1 Nov 2021
Summary conviction Fine not exceeding the statutory amount or jail ≤12 months, or both
Conviction on indictment Jail ≤7 years + fine, or both Jail ≤10 years + fine, or both
Key date — 1 November 2021
Offences committed up to 31 October 2021 → max 7 years.
Offences committed from 1 November 2021 → max 10 years.
This 3-year increase represents a serious escalation of the UK's stance on financial crime.

▸MAR 1 vs MAR 2

Firms and individuals participating in financial markets are required to observe standards of conduct. The FCA's Market Conduct Sourcebook ("MAR") sets these standards:

  • MAR 1 — requirements relating to market abuse
  • MAR 2 — price stabilising rules

The FCA's MAR Sourcebook provides guidance on UK MAR in terms of:

  • What does and does not amount to market abuse
  • Factors taken into account in determining whether market abuse has occurred
1.3

Prevention + Surveillance — System Requirements

Where firms have a reasonable suspicion that an order / transaction in any financial instrument (placed / executed on or outside a trading venue) could constitute insider dealing, market manipulation, or attempts — they are required to report the regulator without delay (the FCA in the UK).

▸System requirements

UK MAR requires firms to establish and maintain effective arrangements, systems and procedures:

  • Appropriate and proportionate to the scale, size, nature, and complexity of business activity
  • Regularly assessed — at least through an annually conducted audit and internal review, updated when necessary
  • Clearly documented in writing, including changes / updates — documented information maintained for 5 years
  • Allow analysis, individually and comparatively, of each and every transaction executed and order placed/modified/cancelled/rejected in trading venue systems + transactions executed outside a trading venue
  • Produce alerts indicating activities requiring further analysis
  • Cover the full range of trading activities

▸Human analysis + training

Firms should:

  • Put in place and maintain arrangements — ensuring an appropriate level of human analysis (not just automation) in monitoring
  • Provide the competent authority with information demonstrating the appropriateness/proportionality of systems, including the level of automation
  • Provide effective and comprehensive training to staff involved in monitoring/detection. Training on a regular basis, appropriate to scale/size/nature of business
Third-party vendors
Many organisations use third-party vendor solutions — automated compliance tools that monitor employee activities + alert teams to potential violations. Examples: Nasdaq SMARTS, Behavox, Aquis Surveillance.
1.3.2

STOR — Suspicious Transaction and Order Reporting

▸Basic STOR principle

STOR — the backbone of the prevention regime
Where a person has reasonable suspicion regarding an order or transaction in any financial instrument, whether placed / executed on or outside a trading venue (OTC / bilateral), that it could constitute market manipulation or insider trading, they must notify the FCA without delay.

▸Attempts are also included

STOR applies to attempts too
The requirement extends to attempts to commit the offences — where no execution took place but there was intent to distort the market ("attempted market abuse"). An attempted insider trade — even if never executed — already triggers a STOR.

▸Timing — when to report

Firms report directly to the FCA, in a timely manner, once a full investigation has been undertaken — to assess whether an event is considered suspicious and required to be reported.

Important nuance
Firms should not second-guess whether the FCA would consider an event suspicious. They report what the firm considers suspicious. It is the firm's duty — the FCA assesses afterwards.

▸STOR report content

UK MAR outlines the content of a STOR report — includes:

  • Identification of the person submitting the STOR — the capacity in which the person operates (own account / executing orders for third parties)
  • Description of the order / transaction:
    • Type of order, type of trading (particularly block trades), where the activity occurred
    • Price and volume
  • Reasons why the order/transaction is suspected to constitute insider dealing / market manipulation / attempted
  • Means of identifying any person involved — the person who placed/executed + the person on whose behalf
  • Any other information and supporting documents relevant for detecting/investigating/enforcing

▸STOR vs Market Observation

ReportWhen used
STOR The firm is directly involved — has complete information about the transaction/order. A formal regulatory report.
Market Observation The firm observed suspicious activity but is not involved — does not have complete information to meet the defined STOR requirements. An informal alert to the FCA.

▸Reports go directly to the FCA

STORs are submitted directly to the FCA — the competent authority in the UK. Not the NCA, not HMT, not ESMA.

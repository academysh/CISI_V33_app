Chapter 2, Section 11 — Detailed Deep Dive
Dealing and Managing · Syllabus 2.15-2.19 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 11 is the "working engine" of an investment firm's day-to-day operations. It governs how a firm must execute client orders: obtain the best possible result (best execution), properly handle order sequence (client order handling), fair allocation on aggregation, deal with limit orders, and ensure employees do not misuse knowledge of pending trades (personal account dealing). On the exam — 5-7 questions with details on the 6 execution factors, 3 conditions for aggregation, and the 3 personal account dealing prohibitions.

11.1

Application of Rules on Dealing and Managing

▸What COBS rules cover

The COBS rules on dealing and managing include:

  • Best execution (COBS 11.2, 11.2A, 11.2B and 11.2C)
  • Client order handling (COBS 11.3)
  • Client limit orders (COBS 11.4)
  • Record keeping, client orders and transactions (COBS 11.5A)
  • Personal account dealing (COBS 11.7)
  • Recording telephone conversations and electronic communications (SYSC 10A)

▸To whom they apply

The rules on dealing and managing (other than personal account dealing) apply generally to authorised firms; there are some variations in application depending on the nature of business and location of the firm.

  • Certain provisions (those marked in the FCA Handbook) apply to non-MiFID firms as if they were rules.
  • The rules on personal account dealing apply to the designated investment business of an authorised firm, in relation to activities carried on from an establishment in the UK.
11.2

Best Execution — the "best possible result"

▸3 COBS subsections on best execution

SectionFor whom
COBS 11.2Alternative Investment Fund Managers (AIFMs) and residual CIS operators
COBS 11.2AMiFID investment firms
COBS 11.2BUCITS management companies

▸The central obligation

The best execution rules require firms to execute orders on the terms most favourable to the client. The general rule: they apply if the firm owes contractual or agency obligations to its client and is acting on the client's behalf.

"All sufficient" vs "all reasonable steps"
MiFID firms (COBS 11.2A): obligation to take all sufficient steps — the stricter MiFID II standard.
AIFMs and residual CIS operators (COBS 11.2): continue to operate under the previous FCA obligation — all reasonable steps — a slightly lesser burden.

▸6 execution factors

Execution factors are the parameters the firm must take into account when determining the best possible result:

  1. Price
  2. Costs
  3. Speed
  4. Likelihood of execution and settlement
  5. Size
  6. Nature or any other consideration relevant to execution

▸Determining relative importance

The relative importance of each factor depends on the criteria, characteristics and circumstances of:

  • The client, including their categorisation
  • The client order
  • The financial instruments involved
  • The execution venues to which the order can be directed
Best execution ≠ best price
Best execution does NOT mean simply "the best price". Any of the 6 factors can take precedence over price depending on the circumstances. For example, for illiquid instruments, likelihood of execution may be more important than price — sending an order just anywhere at the best quoted price may simply not get filled.
Example — when likelihood of execution beats price
Client wants to buy £5M of an emerging markets issuer's corporate bond. Venue A quotes 98.50 (better price), but low liquidity — only 20% of the order can fill. Venue B quotes 98.55 (slightly worse), but deep liquidity — the whole order fills. Venue B is best execution, because likelihood of execution outweighs marginal price improvement.
11.3

Order Execution Policy (OEP) — requirements

▸MiFID business requirements

MiFID II requires firms to have an order execution policy that clearly articulates to clients how the firm seeks to obtain the best possible result.

Firms must:

  • Disclose the OEP to the client prior to undertaking any MiFID investment business for that client
  • Obtain prior consent from the client to the OEP — either by general agreement or in respect of individual transactions

▸Annual review requirement

Firms must review the ongoing appropriateness of the OEP and their order execution arrangements:

  • At least annually, OR
  • When there are material changes affecting the firm's ability to continue to obtain the best possible result
Exam numbers
OEP review — at least annually (not quarterly, not every 2 years). OR upon a material change. Firms are required to update policies and notify clients of such updates.

▸Non-MiFID business — differences

For non-MiFID firms, the OEP must include, for each class of financial instrument in which the firm deals:

  • Information about different execution venues where the firm executes client orders
  • The factors affecting choice of venue used

Firms must give clients appropriate information about their execution policies; more detailed for retail clients. Firms must obtain clients' consent prior to their OEP (although this can be implied consent where the client continues to trade with the firm after receiving the MiFID Terms of Business).

▸Portfolio managers and receivers/transmitters

Portfolio managers, receivers and transmitters of orders MUST MAINTAIN order execution policies, but they do NOT need to obtain client consent to the policy.

11.4

Specific Client Instructions

▸The rule on following specific instructions

The firm is deemed to have satisfied its obligation to obtain the best possible result if it follows the client's specific instructions — but only in relation to the part or aspect of the order related to those instructions.

Firms must adhere to the client's instructions even if an alternative means of execution would have given a better result.

▸Partial instructions

If the client's specific instruction relates only to one part or aspect of the order, the firm is still required to adhere to best execution principles in relation to any other parts of the order that are not subject to the specific instructions.

The firm must not induce the client
Firms must not induce clients to instruct an order in a particular way — by expressly indicating or implicitly suggesting the content of the instruction — if they know that any such instruction would likely prevent obtaining the best possible result.
Example — a legitimate specific instruction
Client gives the firm a specific instruction: "Execute my order for 10,000 XYZ shares only on LSE, not on dark pools". The firm executes on LSE, even though a dark pool offered a better price. The firm has satisfied best execution for this aspect (venue), because the client specifically instructed. But if the client did not specify timing and the firm chose poor timing — the firm breached best execution for that aspect.
11.5

Client Order Handling

Firms must apply procedures and arrangements that provide for the prompt, fair and expeditious execution of client orders, relative to other orders or trading interests of the firm.

▸Execution sequence

Procedures must allow comparable client orders to be executed in the order in which they are received.

▸4 firm duties

Firms must ensure:

  1. Executed client orders are promptly and accurately recorded and allocated
  2. Comparable orders are executed sequentially and promptly, unless impracticable or the client's interests require otherwise
  3. Retail clients are informed of any material difficulty in the prompt execution of their order IMMEDIATELY upon the firm becoming aware
  4. If the firm is responsible for overseeing / arranging settlement — assets / money must be delivered promptly and correctly

▸Misuse of information prohibition

Firms must not misuse information relating to client orders and must take steps to prevent its abuse (e.g. to profit by dealing on their own account).

11.6

Aggregation and Allocation — 3 conditions

▸3 conditions for aggregation

Firms may aggregate their deals with those of a client (or aggregate two or more clients' deals), only if:

  1. This is unlikely to disadvantage any of the aggregated clients
  2. The fact that aggregation may work to their disadvantage is disclosed to the clients
  3. An order allocation policy is established, providing (in sufficiently precise terms) for the fair allocation of transactions. Must cover:
    • How volume and price of orders affect allocation
    • How partial allocations will be dealt with

▸Partial execution

If the aggregated order is executed only partly, the firm must allocate the trades in accordance with the allocation policy.

▸Own-account aggregated with client orders

If the firm has own-account orders aggregated with client orders — it must not allocate in a way detrimental to clients.

In particular, the firm must allocate client orders in priority over its own — UNLESS it can show that without the inclusion of its own order, less favourable terms would have been obtained. In such circumstances, the firm may allocate deals proportionately.

Key priority rule
Aggregation of firm's own + client orders + partial execution:
→ Client orders first (priority).
→ Exception: if the firm's own order helped obtain better terms, it may allocate proportionately.

▸Prevent reallocation

The firm must, as part of its allocation policy, put in place procedures to prevent reallocation, in a way detrimental to the client, of transactions for its own account that are executed in combination with client orders.

Example — correct allocation
Firm receives orders: Client A buy 10,000; Client B buy 5,000; Client C buy 5,000. Plus own-account: buy 5,000. Firm aggregates: combined 25,000 share order. Only 15,000 execute.

Correct allocation: 100% to client orders first (20,000 share demand), but only 15,000 available. Allocate per policy pro-rata among clients: A gets 7,500; B — 3,750; C — 3,750. Firm's own order gets 0 — unless the firm can show that without including its 5,000 the clients would not have obtained even these terms.
11.7

Client Limit Orders — obligation to make public

Unless the client instructs otherwise, a firm receiving a client limit order for shares listed on a regulated market and which cannot immediately execute under prevailing market conditions, must make the limit order public (in a manner easily accessible to other market participants).

This must be done IMMEDIATELY, so that the order can be executed as soon as possible.

▸Exception — orders above normal market size

The firm need NOT do so for orders over normal market size. Logic: a large order could disrupt the market if made public.

▸2 ways of making a limit order public

  1. Transmitting the order to a regulated market or MTF operating an order book trading system, OR
  2. Ensuring the order is made public and can be easily executed as soon as market conditions allow
What is a limit order
A limit order is an order to buy or sell a security at a specific price or better. For example, "buy XYZ at £5.00 or lower". If the market is currently trading at £5.20, the order cannot be executed immediately. The firm must make it public so other market participants can trade with it when the price falls to £5.00.
11.8

Personal Account Dealing — restrictions on employees

▸Who are "relevant persons"

The rules apply to employees who are relevant persons:

  • Directors, partners of the firm or its appointed representatives
  • Other personnel involved in regulated activities either directly or via an outsourcing arrangement

▸3 personal account dealing prohibitions

Firms must have arrangements in place to prevent employees who are relevant persons from:

  1. Entering into a personal transaction that:
    • Is contrary to UK MAR (market abuse), OR
    • Involves misuse or improper disclosure of confidential information, OR
    • Conflicts with the firm's duties to a customer
  2. Improperly advising or procuring anyone else to enter into a transaction that (if completed by the employee) would fall foul of point 1 or a relevant provision
  3. Improperly disclosing, other than in the normal course of employment/contract, information or opinion to any other person, if the relevant person knows (or reasonably ought to know) that as a result of the disclosure the other person will or would be likely to take steps to:
    • (a) Enter into a transaction in designated investments that would fall foul of (1) or a relevant provision
    • (b) Advise or procure another person to enter into such a transaction

▸Relevant provisions

"Relevant provisions" includes:

  • Rules on personal account transactions undertaken by financial analysts
  • Rules on misuse of information relating to pending client orders

▸Records

Firms must keep records of all personal transactions notified to them, and of any authorisation or prohibition made in connection with them.

▸Core rule

COBS 11.7 core requirement
Under COBS 11.7, the core personal account dealing obligation is that personal transactions MUST NOT CONFLICT with the interests of clients. Pre-clearance is required.
Prohibition (1) breach example
A portfolio manager plans to sell a large holding in XYZ across all discretionary client accounts tomorrow morning. His sister — in the compliance team — overhears the plan and sells XYZ from her personal account this evening, ahead of the client trades.

Prohibition (1) violated: personal transaction conflicts with firm's duties to customer + contrary to UK MAR (misuse of information about pending client orders). Classic front-running — could lead to criminal prosecution under MAR.

▸Compliance arrangements

Arrangements must ensure affected employees are aware of restrictions on personal transactions and of the firm's procedures. In practice this means:

  • Pre-clearance of any personal trade through compliance
  • Restricted list of securities where trading is prohibited or limited (usually those where the firm holds material non-public information)
  • Holding period requirements (cannot sell shares immediately after purchase)
  • Mandatory disclosure of all trading accounts held by employees

Chapter 2, Section 12 — Detailed Deep Dive
Reporting of Transactions · Syllabus 2.20-2.21 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 12 is the "reporting" side of an investment firm's work. After each trade the firm must report it across several layers: to the regulator (transaction reporting — for market abuse surveillance), to the market (trade reporting — for price transparency), to a trade repository (EMIR for derivatives, SFTR for securities financing), and to the client itself (trade confirmations + periodic statements). Each regime has its own deadline, scope, and exemptions. On the exam — 5-7 questions with specific numbers: T+1, 1 minute, 5 minutes, 3 minutes, 3:30pm, 8pm, 6 months.

12.1

Reportable Transactions — what must be reported

Reportable transactions are trades carried out by regulated investment firms in 3 types of instruments:

  1. Financial instruments admitted to trading (or traded) on a trading venue, or for which a request for admission to trading has been made (i.e. even pending admission already triggers reporting).
  2. Financial instruments where the underlying is a financial instrument traded on a trading venue.
  3. Financial instruments where the underlying is an index or basket composed of financial instruments traded on a trading venue.

▸Scope — where the trades must have happened

The obligation to report applies irrespective of whether transactions were carried out on trading venues in the UK, Gibraltar, or EU. Transactions also need to be reported if the trade did not take place on a trading venue (OTC trades in reportable instruments are also in scope).

▸Deadline — T+1

UK MiFIR transaction reporting deadline
Complete and accurate details of transactions must be reported to the FCA as soon as possible, and no later than the close of the next business day — i.e. T+1.

▸Whose duty to determine reportable

It is the UK investment firm's responsibility to determine whether an instrument it has traded is a reportable financial instrument. You cannot simply pass responsibility to an ARM or counterparty.

▸Definition of UK investment firm

What is a UK investment firm
"Any person whose regular occupation or business is the provision of one or more investment services to third parties or the performance of one or more investment activities on a professional basis."

I.e. any person whose regular occupation / business is to provide investment services to third parties or carry on investment activities on a professional basis.

The regulations contain a number of exemptions from the obligation to report, which are outside the scope of this workbook.

Reportable vs non-reportable examples
✓ REPORTABLE:
• Buying Shell shares on LSE (listed on a trading venue)
• OTC equity swap where underlying is Apple stock (NASDAQ-listed)
• Option on the FTSE 100 index (basket of trading-venue instruments)
• Government bond traded on an MTF
✗ NOT REPORTABLE:
• Spot FX trade (spot FX is not a financial instrument under MiFID)
• Physical commodity purchase (not a financial instrument)
• Private company equity (not admitted to a trading venue)
12.2

Role and Purpose of Trade and Transaction Reporting

The role and purpose of reporting are defined in three main regulations:

  • UK MiFIR — transaction reporting + trade reporting (two distinct processes!)
  • UK EMIR — derivatives reporting
  • UK SFTR — securities financing transactions reporting

Plus UK SSR (Short Selling Regulation) — a separate notification regime for net short positions.

12.2.1

UK MiFIR — Transaction vs Trade Reporting (two distinct processes!)

This is one of the exam's favourite traps: MiFIR requires two different reports that are easily confused.

FeatureTransaction ReportingTrade Reporting
Purpose Provide info to regulator → market abuse surveillance Feed back to market → price formation, market transparency
Audience FCA (Transaction Monitoring Unit — TMU) Market (publicly)
Sides Dual-sided — both counterparties report (if both UK financial) Single-sided — only one side reports
Deadline T+1 (close of next business day) 1 minute for equities; 5 minutes for non-equities
Instrument scope All reportable financial instruments Equities + non-equities on RM/MTF/OTF

▸Transaction Reporting — details

Under UK MiFIR, Transaction reporting is carried out to provide information to the regulator, enabling review of transactions after the fact. The information is used by the regulator to:

  • Monitor for market abuse, including insider dealing
  • Aid general surveillance of the market

In the UK, the FCA's Transaction Monitoring Unit (TMU) monitors compliance with the requirements for authorised firms to report transactions, and also approves the reporting mechanisms that can be used.

▸ARMs — Approved Reporting Mechanisms

Approved Reporting Mechanisms (ARMs) include, for example, UnaVista and TRAX. An ARM is a third-party system that submits reports to the FCA on behalf of the firm. A firm may outsource the technical reporting process, but it remains responsible for accuracy.

Responsibility for accuracy rests with the firm
Using an ARM does not transfer responsibility away from the firm. If the ARM submits an erroneous report — the firm is responsible vis-à-vis the FCA.

▸Trade Reporting — details

Trade reporting is a mechanism to feed back to the marketplace on market depth and liquidity — a measure of market transparency — and assists in price formation.

Trade reporting is single-sided: it shows only the side of the reporting institution. In contrast, transaction reporting can be dual-sided.

▸MiFID II post-trade transparency — non-equities too

MiFID II introduced additional pre-trade and post-trade transparency reporting obligations for investment firms and market operators. The biggest change in post-trade reporting: it now covers not just equities but also non-equity financial instruments admitted to trading on regulated trading venues (Regulated Markets, MTFs and/or OTFs).

▸Post-trade reporting deadlines

Unless waivers and/or deferrals apply, reporting must be undertaken by regulated firms within:

  • 1 minute for equity transactions
  • 5 minutes for in-scope non-equity financial instruments

The new non-equity post-trade reporting requirements remove many post-trade reporting requirements that did not provide full transparency when certain instruments were deemed illiquid.

Key reporting numbers
T+1 — transaction reporting under UK MiFIR to FCA
1 minute — equity post-trade reporting
5 minutes — non-equity post-trade reporting
Do not confuse these with LSE-specific deadlines (see 12.3).
12.2.2

UK EMIR — derivatives trade repository reporting

Under UK European Market Infrastructure Regulation (UK EMIR), all trades in relation to derivatives contracts must be reported to a trade repository.

▸Dual-sided reporting

UK counterparties report under UK EMIR, and EU counterparties report under EU EMIR, even if one of them is not a financial institution.

Although there are some exceptions for mandated reporting by financial counterparties, both counterparties must report each trade. By prior arrangement, one party can report on behalf of both counterparties. Where one report is made on behalf of both, this must be indicated in the report.

▸Non-UK entities — NO reporting obligation

Critical distinction for branches
Under UK EMIR, non-UK entities have NO reporting obligation. This includes UK branches of third-country firms, such as the London branch of a Canadian bank.

This contrasts with UK investment firm requirements under MiFIR, where a UK branch of a third-country firm is in scope.

▸Delegation of reporting

Either counterparty to the trade may delegate reporting to a third party, such as:

  • Central counterparty (CCP)
  • Trading venue / platform
  • Counterparty bank

Delegation shifts operational work, but not responsibility — that remains with the counterparty.

EMIR reporting example
A UK asset manager does an interest rate swap with the London branch of a Canadian bank.

Reporting:
• UK asset manager: reports under UK EMIR (UK financial counterparty)
• London branch of Canadian bank: does NOT report (it is a non-UK entity — a third-country firm with a UK branch)

The UK asset manager may delegate reporting to the CCP through which clearing occurs, but remains legally responsible.
12.2.3

UK SSR — Short Selling Regulation

The Short Selling Regulations (SSR) introduced a private and public notification regime for investors holding net short positions in certain financial instruments.

▸FCA intervention powers

The SSR gives the FCA powers of intervention during adverse market conditions that pose a serious threat to financial stability or market confidence. The powers may extend to:

  • Extending scope of the notification and disclosure regime to include additional financial instruments
  • Requiring lenders of financial instruments to notify any significant change in their fees
  • Restricting short selling, or other transactions conferring a financial advantage in the event of a price decrease of a financial instrument — across classes or all instruments
  • Restricting or limiting entering into sovereign credit default swap transactions

▸Timing — 3:30pm trading day +1 and midnight calculation

Two different times for UK SSR
Notification deadline: firms must notify the FCA via their Electronic Submission System by 3:30pm on the trading day after the day the position was reached.

Calculation timing: All calculations must be made as at MIDNIGHT on the trading day the position was reached.

▸Exemption — market makers and authorised primary dealers

Market makers and authorised primary dealers may obtain an exemption from the UK SSR disclosure requirements. Logic: these market participants regularly take short positions as part of their normal business of providing liquidity — otherwise the market would not function.

SSR timeline example
A hedge fund reached a net short position of 0.2% in Company X shares at 15:00 on Monday.

Calculation: position measured at midnight Monday (23:59 closing value).
Notification deadline: by 3:30pm Tuesday (trading day after) via the FCA's Electronic Submission System.

If the hedge fund were an authorised primary dealer in gilts, it could obtain an exemption from this disclosure.
12.2.4

UK SFTR — Securities Financing Transactions Regulation

The reporting obligations of the UK Securities Financing Transactions Regulation (SFTR) aim to enhance transparency and enable regulators to monitor risk by introducing:

  • Reporting requirements for securities finance transactions (SFT), similar to those already in place for derivatives under UK EMIR
  • Limitations on the reuse of collateral

▸What an SFT is

Securities Financing Transactions
Main types of SFT: repurchase (repo) transactions, reverse repos, securities lending and borrowing, buy-sell backs, margin lending transactions. These are transactions in which securities are used as collateral for financing.

▸Dual-sided reporting

Reporting can be dual-sided where both parties to the transaction are deemed in scope. Reporting is made by firms to a trade repository, which submits the information to the FCA.

▸Exemption — non-financial counterparties

Key UK-specific exemption
Under UK SFTR, non-financial counterparties (i.e. corporate companies) are EXEMPTED by HM Treasury from having to report their securities financing transactions under UK SFTR.

This is a UK-specific difference — under EU SFTR corporate counterparties must report.
SFTR scope example
Scenario 1: UK bank repo with UK asset manager. Both financial counterparties → both report (dual-sided).
Scenario 2: UK bank repo with UK Corporate (non-financial, obtaining funding by pledging shares as collateral). UK bank reports, UK Corporate EXEMPT (HM Treasury exemption).
Scenario 3: Two corporates repo between themselves. Neither reports (both exempt).
12.3

Reporting Channels and Responsibility — LSE-specific rules

▸Two LSE systems

The LSE runs two main trading systems:

  • Stock Exchange Electronic Trading Service (SETS) — UK equities
  • International Order Book (IOB) — depositary receipts

▸Automatic trade reporting — order book trades

Trade reporting is automatic for all trades executed on the LSE's electronic order books — embracing UK equities traded on SETS and depositary receipts traded on the IOB.

Because the trades are executed automatically on the order books, they generate automatic trade reports — and there is no need for participants to report trades manually.

▸Off-order book transactions — 3 minutes

Off-order book transactions are trades executed by one or more member firms in securities (including gilts), traded away from the order book, often over the telephone.

Such trades must be reported by the member firm to the exchange as close to real time as possible, and in any case within 3 minutes of execution by reference to the trade reporting period.

▸Trade reporting period: 7:15am – 5:15pm

The trade reporting period is the period when the LSE system is able to accept trade reports. It runs from 7:15am to 5:15pm every business day.

If a trade is executed between 7:15am and 8:00am — the report must be submitted before 8:00am or within 3 minutes, whichever is later.

▸Who reports — seniority rules

LSE responsibility rules
Equal seniority: the selling member firm reports.
Different seniority: the more senior party reports (typically a market maker).

▸CREST deadline — 8pm

For LSE domestic transactions that require settlement via CREST: transaction reports for settlement purposes must be submitted via CREST by 8:00pm each business day.

LSE-specific numbers
3 minutes — off-order book trades on the LSE
7:15am–5:15pm — trade reporting period
8:00pm — CREST deadline for LSE domestic settlement
Equal seniority → selling firm reports; different → more senior reports.
Practical example
A UK broker-dealer buys £10M in gilts from a market maker (over the telephone) at 9:30am.

3-minute rule: the market maker (more senior) must report the trade to the LSE by 9:33am.
CREST settlement: the transaction report for settlement via CREST must be submitted by 8:00pm.
MiFIR transaction reporting: separately to the FCA by T+1 close.
12.4

Reporting to Clients — trade confirmations and periodic statements

▸Trade confirmations — ASAP / no later than next business day

When a firm executes an order for a retail client, it must provide confirmation / dealing note in a durable medium:

  • As soon as possible AFTER execution
  • No later than the first business day after execution (T+1)
  • Or — if confirmation is received by the firm from a third party — no later than the first business day after receipt of the third-party confirmation

▸Trade confirmation contents

The confirmation must contain essential information: firm identification, client name, trading day, trading time, type of order, venue identification, financial instrument identification, buy/sell indicator, nature of order, quantity, unit price, total consideration, commissions and expenses charged. For retail — additionally the total sum of commissions + expenses.

▸Periodic statements — 6-monthly default for retail

Retail periodic statements
A firm providing a portfolio management service must send a retail client a periodic statement in a durable medium. Default frequency — every 6 months — unless agreed otherwise between firm and client.

Exceptions / variations:

  • Client may request quarterly statements (more frequent)
  • If the portfolio includes leveraged transactions — statement monthly
  • Client given online access with up-to-date valuation — at least annual is sufficient (but at least once a year)

▸Professional clients — upon agreement

For professional clients, the frequency and content of periodic statements are a matter of agreement between firm and client. There is no hard 6-monthly default.

▸10% depreciation notification — retail

If a retail client's portfolio loses 10% of value (and multiples thereof), the firm must inform the client by the end of the business day on which the threshold is exceeded. If that day is a non-business day — by the end of the next business day.

The same rule applies to leveraged financial instruments or contingent liability transactions.

▸Record-keeping periods

Business typeRetention period
MiFID business records5 years (under MiFID II)
Non-MiFID general records3 years
Life policies, pension contracts5 years
Pension transfers, opt-outs, FSAVCsIndefinitely
Common frequency misconception
Many think that periodic statements are monthly for all retail. That is NOT so. Default for retail = 6 months. Monthly only if there are leveraged transactions. Quarterly — upon client's request.
10% notification example
A retail client has a portfolio worth £100,000 at the start of the quarter. In mid-March a market crash — portfolio value falls to £88,000 (12% depreciation).

The firm must: notify the client by the end of the business day on which the threshold was crossed (passing the -10% mark). Do not wait for the next periodic statement.
If the client hits -20% — another notification (multiples of 10%).

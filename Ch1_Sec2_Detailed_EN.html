Principles for Businesses · Syllabus 1.2 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 2 is the FCA's moral code for all authorised firms. The 12 Principles for Businesses (PRIN) are high-level rules: short, abstract, yet legally binding. Breach of any Principle triggers disciplinary sanctions. In 2023 the FCA added Principle 12 — Consumer Duty, radically raising the bar on consumer protection. The section also covers Fair Treatment of Customers (FTOC): what applies to firms outside the Consumer Duty scope. On the exam ~2-3 questions. Key distinction tested: Consumer Duty (4 outcomes) vs FTOC (6 outcomes).

2.1

12 Principles for Businesses — high-level rules

The Principles for Businesses (PRIN) are legally binding on all authorised firms. If a firm breaches any of the Principles, it is liable to disciplinary sanctions. This means the FCA can punish a firm for breaching a Principle even if the firm has not technically breached any specific rule in COBS or SYSC. The Principles are the high-level, in-principle code of conduct.

▸All 12 Principles

1
Integrity — a firm must conduct its business with integrity.
2
Skill, care and diligence — a firm must conduct its business with due skill, care and diligence.
3
Management and control — a firm must take reasonable care to organise and control its affairs responsibly and effectively, with adequate risk management systems.
4
Financial prudence — a firm must maintain adequate financial resources.
5
Market conduct — a firm must observe proper standards of market conduct.
6
Customers' interests — a firm must pay due regard to the interests of its customers and treat them fairly.
7
Communications with clients — a firm must communicate information to clients in a way that is clear, fair and not misleading.
8
Conflicts of interest — a firm must manage conflicts fairly — both between itself and customers and between customers and other clients.
9
Customers: relationships of trust — a firm must take reasonable care to ensure the suitability of its advice and discretionary decisions for any customer entitled to rely upon its judgment.
10
Clients' assets — a firm must arrange adequate protection for clients' assets when it is responsible for them.
11
Relations with regulators — a firm must deal with its regulators in an open and cooperative way and disclose to the PRA/FCA anything of which they would reasonably expect notice.
12
Consumer Duty — a firm must act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers. Firms need to consider the characteristics and objectives of their customers. Action + evidence that outcomes are being met.
Principle 12 — new since 2023
Principle 12 is the newest. It was added by the FCA in July 2023 as part of the Consumer Duty initiative. Before that there were 11 Principles.
What "legally binding" means
PRIN is not soft guidance. A breach of a Principle is like a breach of any rule: it can lead to fines, public censure, restrictions on the firm's permission, personal action against senior managers.
2.2

Consumer Duty — Principle 12 in detail

The Consumer Duty initiative comprises:

  • Principle (Principle 12) — high-level obligation
  • Rules — detailed rules in the FCA Handbook
  • Expected outcomes — 4 outcomes the FCA expects
  • Formal guidance — FG22/5 (Finalised Guidance)

▸Aims and purpose

The Consumer Duty sets higher and clearer standards of consumer protection across financial services and requires firms to put their customers' needs first. Plus cross-cutting rules that provide greater clarity + rules relating to the outcomes the FCA expects to see.

▸Implementation dates

StageDate
Rules in force for new and existing products/services31 July 2023
Rules in force for closed products and services (only for existing customers, no new investments, or winding down)31 July 2024
2.2.1

Scope of Consumer Duty — who is covered

The Consumer Duty applies to products and services offered to retail customers. The definition covers:

  • Consumer credit — including Claims Management (CMCOB) and Funeral Plan (FPCOB)
  • Deposit-taking activities (BCOBS) — for consumers, micro-enterprises, charities with turnover < £1M, and natural persons acting in a trustee capacity (outside their trade/business/profession)
  • Insurance (ICOBS) — but not reinsurance
  • Investments (COBS) — business with a customer who is not a professional client. If the client elects to be treated as professional, the Duty does not apply — but it applies to the process of determining client status
  • Mortgages (MCOB) — regulated mortgages are in scope. Unregulated buy-to-let or commercial lending are not
Prospective customers are also in scope
The Consumer Duty applies not only to existing customers, but also to prospective customers. This is important — the firm must consider the Duty at the marketing and targeting stage.

▸Wholesale firms — when they are in scope

Wholesale firms (B2B) can fall in scope when they have the potential to impact retail customers in a distribution chain — and their actions (when creating products) could determine or materially influence retail customer outcomes.

Examples of material influence:

  • Design or operation of retail products/services — including price and value
  • Distribution of retail products/services
  • Preparing and approving communications for retail customers
  • Engaging in customer support for retail

The Consumer Duty is applied proportionately — a firm remote from retail customers, with no direct client relationship, has limited obligations.

▸Wholesale exclusions

Excluded from the Consumer Duty:

  • Manufacture of products only for wholesale (if they meet the "retail market business" definition) — including financial instruments intended to be traded on a regulated market/venue
  • Activities relating to non-retail financial instruments — minimum denomination £50,000
  • Market activities for certain instruments meeting the criteria
  • Insurance — large risks for commercial customers or risks located outside the UK
  • Group insurance policy distribution
  • Benchmark administration
Key number — £50,000
Wholesale exclusion for non-retail financial instruments — minimum denomination £50,000. A frequent exam number.

▸UK only

The Consumer Duty applies only to firms conducting regulated activity in the UK.

▸Principle 12 vs Principles 6 and 7

Displacement rule
When Principle 12 applies (the firm is in scope of the Consumer Duty), Principles 6 and 7 DO NOT apply to the services/products provided to retail customers. I.e. P12 displaces P6 and P7 in respect of retail.

Firms not subject to the Consumer Duty continue to comply with P6 and P7 (but not P12).

▸Mixed business — retail + non-retail

If a firm has clients both in and out of the Consumer Duty scope, it must comply with the relevant requirements for the services/products provided to the different customer types. This creates an operational challenge — dual procedures. The firm may elect to apply Consumer Duty standards to all clients to simplify — this is at the firm's discretion.

▸Agent as client

Firms acting under the "agent as client" rules must ascertain whether retail customers are at the end of the distribution chain, and whether the firm can determine or materially influence outcomes for them. If yes — the Consumer Duty applies proportionately.

Attempts to game the system — NOT permitted
Firms cannot incorrectly classify a product/service as "non-retail" with the aim of avoiding the Consumer Duty and then distribute to retail customers. This will be treated as a breach.
2.2.2

Cross-Cutting Rules — 3 universal rules

The cross-cutting rules are 3 rules that cut across all 4 outcomes. Firms must:

  1. Act in good faith towards retail customers
    • Standard of conduct — honesty, fair and open dealings
    • Acting consistently with reasonable expectations of retail customers
    • Firms should consider this at the design stage of the product or service
  2. Avoid foreseeable harm to retail customers
    • Firms must ensure retail customers understand and accept inherent risk in products
    • Firms do NOT have a responsibility to protect customers from all foreseeable harm
    • Firms must be clear and transparent
  3. Enable and support retail customers to pursue their financial objectives
    • The conclusion depends on the products/services requested/purchased
    • Firms are not responsible for customers' own decisions and choices
    • Firms must be clear and transparent when providing material and information
An important nuance — "avoid foreseeable harm"
Many think this is absolute protection — the firm must prevent all foreseeable harm. That is NOT the case. The FCA has specifically stated that firms do NOT have a responsibility to protect customers from all foreseeable harm. Customers remain responsible for their decisions. Firms must be clear and transparent + help customers understand the risks.
2.2.3

4 "Good" Outcomes — structural goals of Consumer Duty

4 Consumer Duty outcomes
(1) Products and Services
(2) Price and Value
(3) Consumer Understanding
(4) Consumer Support

▸Outcome 1: Products and Services

The FCA expects products and services to be designed to meet the needs and objectives of the target group. The Consumer Duty interacts with the requirements of the PROD (Product Intervention and Product Governance Sourcebook).

Product providers (manufacturers):

  • Responsible for ensuring the product/service is designed to meet a specific need or objective of an identified target market
  • Required to carry out regular reviews of how the product performed

Product distributors:

  • Responsible for distribution/marketing in a way that focuses on and reflects the identified target market audience
  • Responsible for taking all reasonable steps to prevent a product being offered to customers outside the intended target market

Where multiple firms are involved in the distribution chain, each firm is required to take responsibility for the outcome in a manner reflecting its ability to influence choices about product design, operation, and distribution.

▸Outcome 2: Price and Value

The FCA does not regulate pricing, but expects firms to offer products/services that represent fair value. Key points:

  • Fair value does not mean all retail consumers achieve the same outcomes — those who shop around gain confidence in value
  • Firms do not have to price products the same for new and existing retail customers — but must demonstrate that any discounts for new customers are communicated clearly to both groups so both could receive fair value
  • The price represents "fair" value for customers in the target market/group
  • Value is not only price paid by the customer: the firm must ensure a relationship between the price paid and the benefit the customer receives
Example — Fair value is NOT the cheapest price
Firm A sells a basic bank account for £0/month. Firm B sells a premium account for £10/month with travel insurance, airport lounge access, credit monitoring. Both can be fair value — if the premium account's benefits are proportionate to £10. Fair value is about the relationship between price and benefit, not about the cheapest option.

▸Outcome 3: Consumer Understanding

Firms must communicate with customers in a consistent manner so they can make informed decisions. The FCA expects firms to provide required information:

  • At the right time
  • Presented in a way — clear and easily understood
  • Enabling customers to understand and assess:
    • Features of products/services
    • Likely costs, associated risks, benefits
    • Whether products/services meet financial needs/objectives

Firms must consider the target market audience — use this to design marketing material:

  • Firms should put themselves in the shoes of retail customers and sense-check — is the information aimed at the right level in terms of detail and understanding

▸Outcome 4: Consumer Support

Firms are expected to provide an appropriate level of service meeting customers' needs throughout their relationship.

  • Firms must ensure communications are fair, clear and not misleading, so customers can make informed decisions
  • Firms must present information in a manner that will be understood by customers
  • Firms need to understand the "financial awareness" of the target market audience
Example — Consumer Support breach
Firm X makes onboarding easy (sign-up in 5 minutes online), but to cancel a subscription, the customer must phone during business hours, wait 30 minutes, speak to several operators. This is a breach of Outcome 4 (Consumer Support) + the cross-cutting rule "avoid foreseeable harm". "Sludge practices" — deliberately difficult exit pathways — are one of the FCA's main targets under the Consumer Duty.
2.2.4

Accountability — Conduct Rule 6 + SM&CR

▸New Conduct Rule

As part of the Consumer Duty, the FCA introduced a new Conduct Rule (SM&CR) for firms in scope of the Duty:

Conduct Rule 6 (individual rule under COCON)
"You must act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers".

If the firm is NOT in scope of the Consumer Duty, Conduct Rule 6 does not apply. In that case existing Conduct Rule 4 ("treat customers fairly") continues to apply.

▸COCON standards — what "good faith" means

The FCA has identified in the COCON rules that:

  • Acting in good faith is a standard of conduct characterised by honesty, fair and open dealing + acting consistently with the reasonable expectations of retail customers
  • Firms and individuals must avoid foreseeable harm to retail customers
  • Firms and individuals must enable and support retail customers to pursue their financial objectives

▸Senior Manager accountability

The FCA holds senior managers accountable via the SM&CR for the implementation and ongoing compliance with the Consumer Duty. Firms must ensure senior managers are clear about what they are accountable for and how they ensure the firm complies with the Duty on an ongoing basis.

Consumer Duty "champion" — removed in Feb 2025
Initially the FCA required firms to appoint a Consumer Duty "champion" on the Board. This requirement was removed in February 2025. Senior manager accountability continues through the standard SM&CR (e.g. via SMF16 or CEO). Do not confuse — a champion is NOT required now.
2.3

FTOC — Fair Treatment of Customers

FTOC applies to firms OUT of scope of the Consumer Duty — either because they do not deal directly with retail customers and cannot materially impact good outcomes for them; or because their products/services fall within exemptions from the Consumer Duty.

The concept of fair treatment is at the heart of the regulatory system. It is expressed in Principle 6 plus an important conduct of business rule:

Client's Best Interests Rule
"A firm must act honestly, fairly and professionally in accordance with the best interests of its client"
This is the "client's best interests rule".

▸FTOC scope

Firms regulated by the FCA, not in scope of the Consumer Duty, must adhere to Principle 6 ("A firm must pay due regard to the interests of its customers and treat them fairly").

▸FTOC goals

The FTOC initiative raises standards in the way firms carry on business:

  • Helps customers fully understand features, benefits, risks, and costs of financial products
  • Minimises sale of unsuitable products — encouraging best practice before, during, and after sale

▸6 FTOC Outcomes — memorise these

  1. Consumers are confident dealing with firms where fair treatment is central to corporate culture
  2. Products/services marketed and sold in the retail market are designed to meet the needs of identified consumer groups + targeted accordingly
  3. Clear information is provided to consumers before, during, and after sale
  4. Where consumers receive advice, the advice is suitable and takes account of their circumstances
  5. Products perform as firms have led consumers to expect
  6. Consumers do not face unreasonable post-sale barriers to change, switch, claim, or complain

Exam trap
Typical trap: FTOC = 6 outcomes, Consumer Duty = 4 outcomes. Don't confuse! Also remember: Outcome 6 of FTOC (no post-sale barriers) — a scenario about an unreasonable switching time frame.
FTOC Outcome 6 scenario
An insurance firm makes the claims process deliberately difficult — requiring notarised documents, multiple forms, telephone interviews — to reduce claim frequency. This is a breach of FTOC Outcome 6 (unreasonable post-sale barriers to claim). May also be a breach of Principle 6 (customers' interests).

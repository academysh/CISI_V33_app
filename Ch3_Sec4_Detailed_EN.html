Chapter 3, Section 4 — Detailed Deep Dive
Money Laundering and Financial Crime · Syllabus 3.4 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
The largest section of Chapter 3 — the UK's Anti-Money Laundering (AML) regime. The primary legislation is the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 (POCA), with 5 offences: Concealing (s.327), Arrangements (s.328), Acquisition/Use/Possession (s.329), Failure to Disclose (s.330), Tipping Off (s.333A). The ML process has 3 stages: Placement → Layering → Integration. Firms must appoint an MLRO / Nominated Officer who reports SARs to the NCA. Detailed rules come from the MLR 2017 (implementing MLD4). Industry guidance — JMLSG (endorsed by HM Treasury, NOT mandatory). 3-4 exam questions — many scenarios about defences, sentences, reporting.

4.1

Definitions — Money Laundering + Criminal Property

Money Laundering (POCA 2002)
Money laundering = the process of turning money derived from criminal activities into funds appearing to come from legitimate sources. In short: "dirty" money → "clean" money.
Criminal Property
Criminal property under POCA is property that:
  • Constitutes a benefit from criminal conduct (or represents such a benefit), AND
  • The alleged offender knows or suspects that the property constitutes / represents such a benefit
Two elements of criminal property
For property to be "criminal", both elements must be present:
(1) Benefit from crime — a fact that objectively exists
(2) Knowledge/suspicion — subjective mental element in the person

If a person did not know and did not suspect that the property was a criminal benefit → the property is not "criminal" to them → no offence.
4.2

POCA 2002 — 5 key ML offences

The Proceeds of Crime Act 2002, Part 7, sets out 5 offences relating to money laundering:

s.327
Concealing — a person conceals, disguises, converts, transfers criminal property, or removes it from England/Wales/Scotland/NI. Max penalty: 14 years prison + unlimited fine.
s.328
Arrangements — a person enters into / is concerned in an arrangement that facilitates the acquisition, retention, use or control of criminal property by / on behalf of another. Widely interpreted — includes advisers giving advice on relevant transactions. Max: 14 years + fine.
s.329
Acquisition, Use, Possession — a person acquires, uses, OR has possession of criminal property. Good faith defence — if the person believed the property was legitimate (e.g. an employee acquired criminal property but believed it was legitimately obtained). Max: 14 years + fine.
s.330
Failure to Disclose — 3 conditions, all of which must be met:
  • (1) The person knows or suspects — or has reasonable grounds to know/suspect (objective test)
  • (2) The information arose in the regulated sector
  • (3) The person failed to disclose to the MLRO "as soon as practicable"
Max: 5 years prison + fine. There is no minimum threshold for transaction value.
s.333A
Tipping Off — a regulated-sector person discloses an SAR or investigation, knowing/suspecting that the disclosure is likely to prejudice an ML/TF investigation. Can be committed even without knowing that a report was actually made. Max: 2 years (the "mildest" POCA ML offence).

▸Defence under s.327-329

Disclosure defence
Defence to s.327/s.328/s.329 — the person made a required disclosure to the MLRO, OR, if the person IS the MLRO, disclosure to the NCA. Separately, the good faith defence for s.329 — believed the property was legitimate.

▸Section 342 — Prejudicing Investigations

Part 7 POCA also contains s.342 — Prejudicing Investigations — which applies generally, not only the regulated sector:

  • Makes a disclosure likely to prejudice an investigation
  • Falsifies, conceals, destroys documents relevant to an investigation

Max: 5 years + fine. Defences: didn't know the disclosure would prejudice; disclosure part of a POCA duty; legal adviser in a professional capacity; didn't know documents were relevant.

Tipping off vs Prejudicing — difference
s.333A Tipping Off — only the regulated sector, specific disclosure of an SAR.
s.342 Prejudicing Investigations — applies generally, to all persons, broader scope (includes destroying documents).
4.3

Three Stages of Money Laundering

ML process — 3 stages (correct order)
A successful ML operation has 3 stages:
Placement → Layering → Integration
1
Placement — the introduction of criminal proceeds (cash / other assets) into the financial system. Typically — placing criminally derived cash into a bank / building society account / bureau de change. This is the most vulnerable stage for criminals — cash physically appears at the bank.
2
Layering — moving criminal proceeds around the financial system through a series of complex transactions — to make it difficult for the authorities to link the placed funds to the original source + ultimate beneficiary. Examples: buying/selling foreign currencies / shares / bonds in rapid succession, investing in CIS, insurance-based investments, cross-border transfers.
3
Integration — the final stage, when layering has been successful and the ultimate beneficiary appears to hold clean money. The money is integrated into the legitimate financial system — it appears to have a legitimate source. Purchase of real estate, luxury goods, legitimate businesses.
AML focus — placement + layering
AML provisions are aimed at identifying customers and reporting suspicions at the placement and layering stages + keeping adequate records — to prevent the integration stage being reached. Once integration happens — it is very hard to recover.
4.4

CDD + JMLSG + MLR 2017

▸MLR 2017 — Money Laundering Regulations 2017

MLR 2017 implemented the Fourth EU Money Laundering Directive (MLD4). Detailed rules for firms — CDD, record-keeping, training, risk assessment.

▸Customer Due Diligence (CDD)

3 levels of CDD
Standard CDD — the baseline: identify the customer, verify identity, understand the nature of the business relationship.
Simplified Due Diligence (SDD) — lower-risk customers (e.g. listed companies, other regulated firms).
Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) — higher-risk situations: PEPs, high-risk third countries, complex/unusually large transactions, non-face-to-face customers.

▸PEPs — Politically Exposed Persons

A PEP is an individual who is or has been, at any time in the preceding year, entrusted with prominent public functions — other than as a middle-ranking / junior official. A firm's EDD obligations cease after the PEP has left office for 1 year (or longer, if the firm considers it appropriate).

Family members of a PEP — spouse / partner, children + their spouses, parents. EDD for family members ceases when the PEP is no longer entrusted with a prominent public function.

For PEPs, EDD — firms must:

  • Have risk-management systems to determine whether the customer / beneficial owner is a PEP
  • Senior management approval — for establishing / continuing the business relationship
  • Establish source of wealth + source of funds
  • Enhanced ongoing monitoring

▸JMLSG — Joint Money Laundering Steering Group

JMLSG — status
The JMLSG is an industry body publishing guidance on practical implementation of AML requirements. Status:
  • NOT approved by the FCA or PRA
  • Endorsed by HM Treasury
  • NOT mandatory — industry guidance on best practices
  • Courts consider adherence as evidence of compliance

▸Credit Reference Agencies

As part of KYC (Know Your Customer) activities — may involve using credit reference agency data: electoral registers, credit history (+ financial associates), updates.

▸Sourcebook reference

FCA expectations for AML systems + controls are primarily in SYSC 6.3.

4.5

MLRO + Nominated Officer + SARs

▸MLRO vs Nominated Officer

RoleMLRO (FCA)Nominated Officer (POCA / MLR)
Primary roleOversees the firm's entire AML complianceDesignated point of contact to receive internal reports
Legal dutyFCA regulatory obligationsMust report suspicions to the NCA. Failure = criminal offence
InternalPolicies, training, monitoringReceives + reviews SARs from staff
External reportingFinal report to the NCAMakes the external report to the NCA
In practice
In most firms the same person = MLRO + Nominated Officer. The terminology highlights legal obligation (NO) vs broader compliance function (MLRO). The MLRO reports at least annually to the governing body.

▸SAR — Suspicious Activity Report

Reporting chain
Staff (suspicion) → MLRO / Nominated Officer (internal disclosure) → NCA (external SAR)

The NCA (National Crime Agency) is the external regulator that receives SARs. Not the FCA, not HMRC.

▸MLRO Duties

  • Policy development — creating/maintaining AML policies + framework
  • Monitoring + reporting — transaction monitoring, automated alerts, receiving internal reports, making SARs to the NCA
  • Risk management — company-wide risk assessments, mitigation strategies
  • Training — AML training programmes, fostering a culture of compliance
  • Regulatory liaison — staying up-to-date with regulations

▸CDD records retention

Record retention — 5 years
Under MLR 2017, CDD records must be retained for 5 years from the end of the business relationship (or occasional transaction date).

▸Common scenarios — the right action

Common exam traps — wrong actions an employee might take:

  • ❌ Delaying disclosure by several weeks — breaches "as soon as practicable"
  • ❌ MLRO does not share suspicion, does not report to NCA — criminal offence s.330
  • ❌ Disclosing to the suspect's solicitor who advises the suspect — can be tipping off / prejudicing
  • ❌ MLRO travels abroad, delays reporting — breach of the "as soon as practicable" duty
  • ✅ Staff → MLRO ASAP; MLRO reviews; if suspicion confirmed → SAR to NCA
4.6

Criminal Finances Act 2017 (CFA 2017)

CFA 2017 created 2 new corporate criminal offences — failure to prevent:

1
Failure to prevent facilitation of UK tax evasion
2
Failure to prevent facilitation of FOREIGN tax evasion
Corporate strict liability
CFA 2017 applies on a strict liability basis — the company becomes criminally liable if an associated person (employee, agent, contractor) facilitates tax evasion. Defence — show that the company had reasonable prevention procedures.

▸SOCPA 2005 — Spanish bullfighter problem

The Serious Organised Crime and Police Act 2005 (SOCPA) addressed the so-called "Spanish bullfighter" problem:

  • Granted a defence for conduct legal in the country where it occurred
  • Subject to Secretary of State exceptions (e.g. serious tax evasion)

For example: bullfighting is legal in Spain but illegal in the UK. Money earned legitimately in Spain (even from an activity illegal in the UK) is not criminal property under POCA, thanks to the SOCPA defence.

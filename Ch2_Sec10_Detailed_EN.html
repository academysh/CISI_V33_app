Chapter 2, Section 10 — Detailed Deep Dive
Inducements and Payment for Research · Syllabus 2.14 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 10 is a continuation of the logic of the previous section (conflicts of interest). It governs inducements — any payments, commissions or non-monetary benefits the firm receives from or pays to third parties in connection with servicing a client. The general rule is: everything not expressly permitted is prohibited. Separately covered are the rules on payment for research — after MiFID II, firms must either pay for research from their own resources or through a dedicated Research Payment Account (RPA). On the exam — 4-6 questions, often scenarios about specific benefits.

10.1

Inducements — the general rule and scope

The inducements rules should be viewed as a "payment rule": they prohibit any payment unless expressly permitted. This is a strict regime.

▸Scope of the rules

The rules apply to firms carrying on designated investment business that is MiFID business, as well as non-MiFID business. But the rules apply only to professional clients and retail clients — investment firms undertaking ECP business are NOT subject to the detailed inducement provisions.

Scope of inducements rules
Applies to: professional + retail clients.
Does NOT apply to: ECP business.
This matches the general philosophy: ECPs are the sophisticated market participants themselves, able to defend their own interests.

▸Independent advice / Restricted advice / Portfolio management to retail

The requirements also cover cases where a firm provides:

  • Independent advice to a retail client
  • Restricted advice to a retail client
  • Portfolio management services to a retail client

Although minor non-monetary benefits are permitted to be received — even under these strict regimes.

10.1.1

Minor Non-Monetary Benefits — 4 criteria + 4 examples

▸4 criteria for a minor non-monetary benefit

A minor non-monetary benefit must meet ALL 4 criteria:

  1. Clearly disclosed prior to the provision of the relevant service to the client
  2. Capable of enhancing the quality of service provided to the client
  3. Of a scale and nature that it could NOT be judged to impair the firm's compliance with its duty to act honestly, fairly and professionally in the best interests of the client
  4. Reasonable, proportionate and of a scale unlikely to influence the firm's behaviour in any way detrimental to the client's interests

▸4 categories of permitted minor non-monetary benefits

The benefit must consist of:

  1. Information or documentation relating to a financial instrument or investment service, generic in nature or personalised to reflect the circumstances of an individual client.
  2. Written material from a third party, commissioned and paid for by a corporate issuer or potential issuer to promote a new issuance by the company.
  3. Participation in conferences, seminars and other training events on the benefits and features of a specific financial instrument or investment service.
  4. Hospitality of reasonable de minimis value, such as food and drink during a business meeting or a conference, seminar or other relevant training events.
Critical limitation — de minimis value
Hospitality only qualifies if it is reasonable de minimis value. Food and drink during a business meeting — OK. Premier League football ticket, lavish dinner, trip to the Maldives — do NOT qualify as a minor non-monetary benefit. That is a direct inducement, prohibited unless it falls within one of the three categories.
Examples — what qualifies and what doesn't
✓ QUALIFIES:
• A research report on a fund from an asset manager whose products the firm works with
• Corporate issuer research on an IPO
• Attendance at a fund house seminar with lunch
• A sandwich and coffee at a training event
✗ DOES NOT QUALIFY:
• A Rolling Stones concert ticket from a fund house
• A weekend trip to a spa resort
• An expensive bottle of wine as a gift
• A dinner at a 3-Michelin-star restaurant without a business purpose
10.1.2

3 categories of permitted payments (MiFID business)

In relation to MiFID business, firms are prohibited from paying or accepting any fees, commissions or non-monetary benefits, other than three categories:

  1. Fees, commissions or non-monetary benefits paid TO OR BY THE CLIENT, or someone on their behalf (e.g. management fees).
  2. Proper fees that are necessary for the provision of the service (e.g. custody costs, legal fees, settlement fees) and which cannot, by their nature, give rise to conflicts.
  3. Fees, commissions or non-monetary benefits paid TO OR BY A THIRD PARTY (or someone on their behalf) — only if 3 conditions are met.

▸3 conditions for third-party payments

Third-party payments are permissible only if they:

  1. Do NOT impair compliance with the firm's duty to act in the client's best interests
  2. Are designed to enhance quality of service to the client
  3. Are disclosed in accordance with set standards prior to provision of the service to the client
Mnemonic for the 3 conditions
N-E-D: Not impair + Enhance + Disclose
Third-party payments are permissible only if they do not impair, enhance quality, and are disclosed.

▸How to satisfy the disclosure obligation

Firms can satisfy disclosure obligations if they:

  • Disclose the essential arrangements for such payments/benefits in summary form
  • Undertake to the client that further details will be disclosed on request
  • And, in fact, give such details on request

▸Record-keeping

Firms must also keep full records of such payments/benefits made to other firms, for all UK MiFID business.

10.1.3

Non-MiFID Firms — separate regime

The inducements provisions have not been fully implemented for non-MiFID firms/business. In relation to third-party payments, these firms need only comply with:

  • The "does not impair compliance" test only
  • The other two tests (disclosure and enhancement) do NOT apply

▸Retail non-MiFID for packaged products

Retail non-MiFID business for the sale of packaged products is subject to the disclosure requirements of the inducement provisions. However, the extension of the provision does not expect firms to disclose details of reasonable non-monetary benefits for non-MiFID business.

Firm typeWhich tests apply
MiFID firm All 3 tests: not impair + enhance + disclose
Non-MiFID firm (not retail packaged products) Only the "not impair" test
Non-MiFID retail for packaged products "Not impair" + disclosure requirements (but not for reasonable non-monetary benefits)
10.2

Inducements and Research — a separate charge for research

UK regulated firms producing research must apply a separately identifiable charge for research when providing it to an entity that is not permitted to receive research for free.

▸Who may NOT receive research for free (in-scope firms)

This is a regulated firm that either:

  • Provides portfolio management services, or
  • Provides independent or restricted advice to a retail client and/or professional client

▸2 permitted payment methods (for in-scope firms)

In-scope firms may only receive (consume) research if they pay using one of two methods:

  1. Directly from their own resources (the firm pays itself, without passing costs to clients).
  2. From a Research Payment Account (RPA), controlled by the firm and funded by a specific charge to clients.

▸Out-of-scope bodies

The provisions do NOT apply to:

  • Non-UK regulated bodies producing research
  • Firms located outside the UK consuming research

▸FCA gold-plating — AIFs and CIS

When implementing MiFID II into its Handbook, the FCA gold-plated (went beyond the Directive's requirements) some requirements. However, it did NOT apply the requirements to:

  • AIFs
  • CISs whose core investment policy does not involve investing in MiFID financial instruments

This means private equity business (investing in companies) and collective investment funds investing in commercial property are NOT caught by the prohibition on receiving research for free, since those investments are not designated MiFID financial instruments.

▸Macro-economic research — must be paid for

FCA rules mean UK asset managers must continue to pay for macro-economic research, since it does not meet the definition of "FICC" research.

Common confusion
Many think that macro-economic research is "general info", generic, not requiring separate payment. That is NOT so. Under FCA rules, macro-economic research = not FICC research = must be paid for.
10.3

Research Payment Account (RPA) — rules of use

▸Funding the RPA

The RPA must be funded ONLY by a specific research charge to clients, agreed with clients, developed by the firm as its research budget for the purpose of establishing the amount needed for third-party research in respect of the investment services provided to its clients.

▸Only for research

The RPA must be only used to pay for research, and NOT goods and/or services that do not fall within the definition of research.

▸Non-exhaustive list of what is NOT research for RPA purposes

  • Post-trade analytics
  • Price feeds or historical price data that has not been analysed or manipulated in order to present the firm with meaningful conclusions
  • Seminar fees
  • Corporate access service
  • Order and execution management services
  • Administration of an RPA
Key distinction: what is research and what isn't
RESEARCH (can be paid via RPA): equity analysis, sector outlook, company fundamentals, investment strategy, currency movements with analysis.
NOT RESEARCH (cannot be paid via RPA): seminar fees, corporate access, order/execution management, unanalysed price feeds, RPA administration.

▸Industry trend — paying from own resources

There has been substantial debate over the use of RPA and fund managers charging clients for research. A number of fund managers took the decision to pay for research themselves, taking steps to reduce the amount of research they receive (and pay for), and to focus on relevant and appropriate research that will add value to investment decision-making.

This also means that research received can be used to benefit all clients of the firm.

10.4

Payment Optionality — August 2024 changes

The FCA has introduced changes to the existing rules governing how payments for investment research are made.

▸Who gets flexibility

In the UK, buy-side firms have been given greater flexibility in how they purchase investment research:

  • Asset managers
  • Pension schemes
  • Fund managers (managers of collective investment schemes)

▸What is permitted from August 2024

They are permitted to "bundle" payments for third-party research and execution services, provided they meet the relevant requirements around execution and research costs.

What "bundle" means
Previously (under MiFID II): research and execution had to be paid for separately. Brokers could not include research in execution commission — that was mandatory unbundling. From August 2024: UK buy-side firms can again include research payment in the commission at execution, if cost requirements are met. This is a return of flexibility that existed before MiFID II.
Example before and after August 2024
Before August 2024: an asset manager trades through broker X. Broker charges 5 bps execution commission. Separately, the asset manager receives research from broker X and pays £50K per year for research through the RPA.

From August 2024: the asset manager may again bundle — the broker charges a 7 bps commission, which includes both execution and research. The key is meeting requirements on execution and research costs.
Exam number
Remember: August 2024 — UK buy-side firms can bundle research + execution payments. This is an important change, asked about on the exam.

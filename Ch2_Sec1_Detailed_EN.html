INTRODUCTION

What COBS is and why it exists at all

COBS stands for Conduct of Business Sourcebook. It is part of the FCA Handbook, and specifically sits within the Business Standards block.

Structure of the FCA Handbook — where COBS lives
The FCA Handbook is divided into large blocks. Here are the main four:
  • High Level Standards — includes PRIN (the 12 Principles for Businesses), SYSC (systems and controls), FIT (Fit & Proper Test).
  • Business Standards — this is where COBS lives, along with CASS (Client Assets).
  • Regulatory Processes — SUP (supervision), AUTH (authorisation of firms).
  • Specialist Sourcebooks — rules for specific sectors (insurance, mortgages, etc.).

Why do we need COBS? Its main goal is to move regulation away from a "prescriptive" approach (where the regulator dictates literally every step) towards an "outcomes-based" approach (focused on results). Instead of "you must do X, Y, Z in exactly this sequence," the regulator says: "Here is the outcome you must deliver to the client — how you achieve it is up to you."

Prescriptive vs outcomes-based example
Prescriptive (old approach): "A firm must send the client a trade confirmation by paper letter in an A4 envelope, no later than 48 hours after order execution, in font no smaller than 11pt."

Outcomes-based (COBS approach): "A firm must ensure that the client receives timely information about the trade in a form they can understand." How exactly — email, SMS, push notification in a mobile app — is for the firm to decide, but the client must get clear information on time.

COBS also implements the provisions of MiFID (Markets in Financial Instruments Directive) — the EU directive on financial instruments, which itself contains high-level standards without prescribing exact methods of execution. This aligned perfectly with the FCA's outcomes-based approach.

Common exam mistake
COBS is not only about MiFID. Many people think that if a firm does not carry on MiFID business, COBS does not apply to it. This is wrong. COBS contains separate provisions for non-MiFID investment firms too. For example, a life insurance adviser does not do MiFID business, but still falls under COBS.
1.1

Firms Subject to COBS — which firms fall under COBS

The core principle: COBS applies on a geographical basis. In other words, it does not really matter what the firm does or which instrument it trades first and foremost — what matters is where it does it from.

▸Breaking down the rule

Part 1 — Firm has an establishment in the UK. If the firm has a permanent place of business in the UK (office, branch, or its appointed representative — AR), then all its activity from that establishment is subject to COBS.

Part 2 — Firm does business with a UK client from an establishment overseas. Even if the establishment is outside the UK, if the firm serves a client located in the UK, COBS generally applies anyway. Exceptions exist for "overseas persons" who meet certain criteria (this topic has its own block in Chapter 1).

Practical example of establishment
Broker firm ABC Ltd has its headquarters in London. It also has a representative office in Singapore. From London, staff serve UK clients — COBS applies in full. From Singapore, staff serve clients in South-East Asia — COBS generally does not apply (regulated by the local regulator MAS). But if Singapore staff decide to contact a client in Manchester — COBS may again apply to that particular transaction.

▸Brexit consequences for COBS application

Before Brexit, the so-called "passporting" regime applied: a UK firm could open a branch in, say, France, and that branch continued to follow UK COBS while serving French clients. Brexit changed this.

After Brexit, the following scheme applies:

Situation Does COBS apply?
UK firm carrying on MiFID business from a UK office for a UK client ✓ YES, in full
UK MiFID firm carrying on MiFID business from an establishment in an EEA member state (e.g. Paris branch) ✗ NO — post-Brexit, that branch follows local rules (France)
Non-UK MiFID firm (third-country firm) carrying on MiFID business through its UK branch (e.g. a US bank via its London branch) ✓ YES — for the MiFID-derived parts
EEA firm that, post-Brexit, operates through a UK branch Classified as a "third-country firm" for MiFID business in the UK → COBS applies
Key point about third-country firms
The term "third-country firm" for UK purposes means any non-UK firm, including EEA firms post-Brexit. Previously, an EEA firm was not a third country (they shared the single market), but now they are placed on the same footing as US, Swiss, or any other non-UK firms.

▸Important exception: the "home state basis" rule

There is one exception to the geographical rule. It covers two topics:

  • Investment research
  • Personal account dealing — trades carried out personally by staff of the firm

For these two topics, COBS applies on a "home state basis". This means these rules apply to a UK firm wherever it operates — even if the activity is being conducted overseas.

Home state basis example
UK-authorised firm XYZ publishes investment research on Apple stock. The analysts who wrote it sit in its New York office. Even though the establishment is in the US, the COBS rules on investment research still apply to that report, because the firm is a UK firm. The same goes for personal account dealing by the New York analyst — they must comply with COBS personal account dealing rules.
Exam — key takeaways for 1.1
Basic rule: COBS applies geographically — where the establishment is.
Post-Brexit: UK firm's EEA branch is NO longer under COBS (for MiFID business).
Third-country firm with a UK branch → COBS applies.
Exception (home state basis): investment research + personal account dealing → UK firm is always under COBS, regardless of location.
1.2

Activities Subject to COBS — which activities are captured

So, we have sorted out which firms fall under COBS geographically. Now let's work out which specific activities of these firms are regulated by COBS.

▸Three categories of activity under COBS

Activity COBS coverage Examples
Designated investment business In full Dealing in shares, bonds, derivatives; portfolio management; investment advice
Long-term insurance business — only for life policies In full Selling life policies, pension annuities, endowment policies
Accepting deposits Partial Only the financial promotions rules and the rules on preparing/providing product information

▸Why are deposits only partial?

Accepting deposits is the business of banks and building societies. It is a simple and low-risk activity: the client deposits money, the bank pays interest. Applying the full set of complex COBS rules (best execution, suitability, appropriateness and so on) to this does not make sense — those rules are tailored for investment products, where the client needs serious protection.

But how a bank markets its deposit product and what information it provides about it does matter, because that is what the client bases their decision on. That is why the following apply to deposits:

  • Financial promotion rules — rules on marketing and advertising (promotions must be fair, clear, and not misleading)
  • Product information rules — rules on preparing and providing information about the product (interest rate, withdrawal conditions, etc.)
Typical exam trap
Do not confuse: banks are dual-regulated (PRA + FCA). The PRA regulates them prudentially (capital, liquidity), while the FCA regulates their conduct. Partial COBS application does not mean banks escape regulation — they are regulated both under COBS (for promotions/information) and under other sourcebooks.

▸COBS rules disapplied for ECP business

ECP stands for Eligible Counterparty. This is the highest client category — typically large financial institutions, central banks, governments. The logic is simple: they are so professional and knowledgeable about the market that they do not need most of the protective rules designed for retail and professional clients.

Client category hierarchy (for context)
Retail Client → maximum protection (full COBS protection).
Professional Client → medium protection.
Eligible Counterparty / ECP → minimum protection (many rules switched off).
Covered in detail in Section 3 (Accepting Clients).

For ECP business, the following COBS chapters are switched off:

COBS Chapter About Status for ECP
Parts of COBS 2 Conduct of business obligations: inducements, agent as client, reliance on others Disapplied (in part)
Much of COBS 4 Communicating with clients (fair, clear and not misleading), financial promotions, direct offer, information disclosure before providing services Disapplied
COBS 6 Information on costs, charges, compensation Disapplied
COBS 8A Client agreements Disapplied
COBS 10 Appropriateness (for non-advised services) Disapplied
Parts of COBS 11 Best execution, client order handling Disapplied
Parts of COBS 12 Labelling of non-independent research Disapplied
COBS 16 Reporting to clients Disapplied
A very important exam trap!
Conflicts of Interest (SYSC 10) are NOT disapplied for ECPs. Even if you are dealing with the largest hedge fund or a central bank — the rules on managing conflicts of interest still apply. This is a very frequent exam question: "Which of the following rules still applies even to ECP business?" — the correct answer: conflicts of interest.
Practical ECP disapplication example
JPMorgan (UK branch) trades derivatives with Goldman Sachs (also an ECP). In this trade:
• No need to send Goldman a fancy client agreement (COBS 8A disapplied).
• No need to carry out an appropriateness assessment (COBS 10 disapplied).
• No need to guarantee best execution in the standard form (part of COBS 11 disapplied).
• BUT conflicts of interest must still be managed (SYSC 10 always applies).
• AND the trade must still be reported under UK MiFIR (this is transaction reporting to the regulator, not to the client).

▸Special rule for participants on a regulated market

There is one more specific rule: members and participants of a regulated market (for example, traders on the LSE — London Stock Exchange) are not required to comply with COBS 11.4 (client limit orders) in respect of each other. The logic is the same: they are professional market participants who know how to handle orders.

BUT — when that same trader is executing an order on behalf of a client, COBS 11.4 kicks back in in full. Because now there is a client in the chain who needs to be protected.

Example — participants on a regulated market
A UBS trader on the LSE places a limit order to sell 10,000 HSBC shares. This order is seen by a Barclays trader. Between the two of them, COBS 11.4 does not apply — both are ECPs, both are market participants. But if UBS accepted a limit order from a retail client and is routing it to the market — COBS 11.4 applies in full, because there is a client behind that order who must be protected.
Exam — key takeaways for 1.2
3 activities under COBS: designated investment business (in full) + long-term insurance/life (in full) + deposits (partial — only promotions and product information).
Disapplied for ECP business: COBS 2 (parts), 4 (most of it), 6, 8A, 10, 11 (parts), 12 (parts), 16.
Always applies — even to ECP: conflicts of interest (SYSC 10).
Members of a regulated market: do not apply COBS 11.4 between themselves, but do apply it when executing client orders.
📋 Final summary of Section 1
WHO + WHAT + WHERE = does COBS apply
WHO: firm with a UK establishment
+ third-country firm via UK branch
+ UK firm (for research/PAD — anywhere)

WHAT: designated investment business (in full)
+ long-term insurance (life) (in full)
+ accepting deposits (partial)

WHERE: in the UK, also with a UK client from overseas
- UK firm's EEA branch post-Brexit
  • COBS = Conduct of Business Sourcebook, sits in the Business Standards block of the FCA Handbook.
  • Approach: outcomes-based, not prescriptive. Implements MiFID, but also covers non-MiFID firms.

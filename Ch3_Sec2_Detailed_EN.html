CHAPTER 3
Chapter 3, Section 2 — Detailed Deep Dive
Insider Dealing · Syllabus 3.2 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Insider dealing is the most-tested type of market abuse: using inside information to trade, encouraging others, or unlawfully disclosing. The UK has two parallel regimes: (1) a criminal regime under the CJA 1993 (Part V) — criminal prosecution and prison; (2) a civil regime under UK MAR — FCA administrative enforcement. Key distinction: general defences (for everyone) vs special defences (for market makers and certain scenarios only). Also: insider lists — issuers must maintain lists of all persons with access to inside information. 2-3 exam questions here, often scenarios about defences.

2.1

Inside Information + the Insider — UK MAR definition

▸Inside Information definition

Inside information — 4 key elements
Information is inside information if it is:
  • Of a precise nature
  • Not been made public
  • Relating directly / indirectly to one or more issuers / financial instruments
  • If made public — likely to have significant effect on prices of those financial instruments / related derivatives

▸"Precise nature" — the test

Information is deemed precise if it indicates:

  • A set of circumstances that exist or may reasonably be expected to come into existence
  • An event that has occurred or may reasonably be expected to occur

And it is specific enough to enable a conclusion about the possible effect on prices of financial instruments / related derivative instruments / spot commodity contracts / emission allowance products.

Precise vs imprecise — example
Precise: "Company XYZ will announce an acquisition of Company ABC for £2bn next Tuesday" — specific event, specific time, specific price.
Imprecise: "Company XYZ might do some M&A sometime" — too vague; no conclusion about price impact is possible.

▸For persons executing client orders — extended definition

For persons charged with executing orders, inside information also includes:

  • Information conveyed by a client and relating to the client's pending orders in financial instruments
  • Of a precise nature, relating directly/indirectly to financial instruments
  • If made public — likely to significantly affect the prices of those financial instruments / related spot commodities / related derivatives / auctioned emission allowance products
Client order = inside information
A broker knows: a large hedge fund will buy a 5% stake in a small-cap company tomorrow. That is inside information — even though it is about a pending order, not about the issuer. The broker cannot front-run the client order.

▸Insider Dealing — 4 specific behaviours under UK MAR

1
Dealing — a person possesses inside information and uses it by acquiring / disposing on own account or third party's account, directly or indirectly, of financial instruments to which the information relates.
2
Cancelling / amending orders — using inside information by cancelling / amending an order placed BEFORE the person possessed the inside information. A rare scenario often overlooked, but also illegal.
3
Recommending / inducing another — a person possesses inside information and recommends, on the basis of that information, that another person acquire / dispose / cancel / amend orders. This is itself insider dealing, even if no direct sharing takes place.
4
Recommending cancellation/amendment — recommending or inducing the cancellation / amendment of an order concerning a financial instrument to which the information relates.
2.1

Who is an "Insider" — Inside Sources

An individual in possession of price-sensitive information is an insider if they:

  • Know it is inside information
  • The information was knowingly acquired from an inside source

▸3 categories of inside sources

1
Direct inside source — the person is themselves an inside source. For example: director, employee, shareholder of an issuer of securities. And need not necessarily be the company whose securities are the subject of the dealing. E.g., a bank employee learning something about a client.
2
Through employment/office/profession — a person has access to information by virtue of their employment, office, or profession. Again, need not relate to the company. Examples: auditor, legal adviser, corporate finance adviser to the company.
3
Secondary (indirect) — access directly or indirectly from a person who was either an inside source themselves or had access through employment. Example: a director's spouse sees confidential info at home about a takeover bid and then buys shares in the target company.
Important — knowingly acquired
The person must KNOW the information is inside information + knowingly acquired from an inside source. Accidentally overhearing on a train — but not realising it was inside info — may be a defence. But if overheard AND they understood it was precise/non-public/price-sensitive AND used it — that is insider dealing.
2.1.1-2.1.2

Public Disclosure + Insider Lists

▸(1) Public Disclosure — issuer obligations

Issuers must inform the public ASAP
Issuers have obligations to inform the public as soon as possible of inside information directly concerning the issuer. The information must be made public in a manner enabling fast access + complete/correct/timely assessment.

This ensures all investors can base their investment decision-making on information in the public domain — helping to keep clean markets.

Issuers should use a Regulatory Information Service (RIS) for disseminating information.

▸(2) Insider Lists

Issuers and persons acting on their behalf are required to maintain an insider list of all persons with access to inside information:

  • Working under a contract of employment
  • Or otherwise performing tasks through which they have access (advisers, accountants, credit rating agencies)

▸When to update the list

The list must be promptly updated where there is a change:

  • The reason for including a person already on the list has changed
  • A new person has access to inside information and needs adding
  • A person ceases to have access to inside information

▸Purpose of the insider list

Why an insider list is needed
The obligation on issuers + advisers is to maintain insider lists so that knowledge of price-sensitive information can be traced by the FCA if it undertakes an investigation into adverse movement in a share price BEFORE a formal announcement. If the share price moved strangely over 3 days before an announcement — the FCA can trace who had access during that window.

Firms are required to maintain separate insider lists:

  • For each transaction
  • One for permanent insiders (directors, CEO, CFO — those who always have access to inside info)
2.1.3

Unlawful Disclosure + Permitted Categories

▸The base rule

Unlawful Disclosure (UK MAR)
Under UK MAR, unlawful disclosure is the sharing of inside information with another person, unless the disclosure is part of the "normal exercise of employment, profession, or duties".

It is an offence to disclose non-public, price-sensitive, specific, likely-to-affect-price information.

▸Penalties

A breach of these requirements can result in:

  • Unlimited fines
  • Injunctions
  • Also considered a criminal offence

▸5 categories of permitted disclosure

1
Normal duties — disclosure made in the normal exercise of employment, profession, or duties. E.g., CFO sharing with CEO and board during planning sessions.
2
Market soundings — part of a market sounding, for example to raise capital. See Section 2.3.
3
Confidentiality — disclosure is reasonable + accompanied by confidentiality requirements to facilitate a transaction, seek advice, or fulfil a legal obligation.
4
Professional advice — shared with a professional adviser, such as a lawyer or auditor. Professional privilege applies.
5
Takeover bids — made to facilitate or seek commitment for a takeover bid under the Takeover Code. Important for M&A — the bidder sometimes must share info with the target board / target shareholders.
STOR reporting — permitted disclosure
An employee reporting inside information to the FCA as part of a STOR is a permitted disclosure. Falls within the normal duties exemption — part of regulatory obligations.
2.2

CJA 1993 — Criminal Regime

▸Criminal offence definition

Insider dealing — criminal definition
Insider dealing is the deliberate exploitation of information by dealing in financial instruments, having obtained the information by virtue of some privileged relationship or position.

The legislation is Part V of the CJA 1993 (Criminal Justice Act 1993). The UK's current criminal law for insider dealing.

▸Recent update — 2023

CJA 1993 updated 15 June 2023
The CJA 1993 was updated and amended by the Insider Dealing (Securities and Regulated Markets) Order 2023, which came into effect on 15 June 2023. It updated the list of Regulated Markets for the purposes of Part V of the CJA 1993.

▸Section 52 — 3 ways of committing the offence

Section 52 of the CJA outlines 3 ways of committing the offence of insider trading:

1
Dealing in the security
2
Encouraging another to do so
3
Disclosing inside information to another

▸Section 56 — Inside Information definition (CJA)

Section 56 of the CJA — information:

  • (a) Relates to securities traded / admitted to trade / request to admit to trading on a UK, EEA, or Gibraltar Regulated Market, MTF or OTF
  • (b) Relates to securities traded on Nasdaq, SIX Swiss Exchange, or NYSE
  • (c) Relates to securities not covered by (a) or (b), the price of which depends on / has an effect on the price of a security in (a)/(b)
  • (d) Is specific / precise
  • (e) Has not been made public
  • (f) Is price-sensitive — if made public, likely to have a significant effect on the price of a security
CJA vs UK MAR — scope
CJA 1993 — only specific Regulated Markets / MTFs / OTFs (UK, EEA, Gibraltar, Nasdaq, SIX Swiss, NYSE).
UK MAR — broader: includes OTC + bilateral trades.
If an OTC trade — only UK MAR civil regime, not CJA criminal.
2.2.1

Instruments covered by CJA 1993

The CJA uses the term "security", not "financial instrument". However, in 2023 this was updated — to be consistent with the financial instrument definition in UK MiFID. The CJA now covers:

1
Transferable securities — shares, bonds
2
Money-market instruments
3
Units in CIS
4
Options, futures, swaps, FRAs — derivatives related to securities, currencies, interest rates, emission allowances, financial indices
5
Derivative contracts on commodities, cash-settled
6
Commodity derivatives, physically settled, traded on a UK Regulated Market / MTF / OTF
7
Other derivative commodity contracts
8
Credit risk derivative instruments
9
CFDs
10
Emission allowances
Example — debt securities vs spot FX
Debt securities (bonds) are caught under insider dealing legislation.
Other common alternatives (e.g. spot FX, spot commodity purchases, physical gold) are NOT MiFID financial instruments — not in scope of CJA insider dealing.
2.3

Market Soundings

A market sounding is communication with potential investors to gauge interest in a transaction. It may include disclosure of non-public information that is sensitive and considered inside information.

▸Obligations of the disclosing firm

The firm must comply with the following obligations:

  • Obtain consent of the person receiving the market sounding to receive inside information
  • Inform the person that they are prohibited from using the information by:
    • Acquiring / disposing of financial instruments
    • Cancelling / amending an order already placed
    • Personal account dealing
    • Encouraging another person to invest / divest in the underlying
  • Inform the person that by agreeing to receive the information, they are obliged to keep it confidential
  • Maintain a record of all information given to the person receiving the sounding
  • Maintain a record of recipients who did NOT consent to receive inside information
  • Advise recipients when the information ceases to be considered inside information

▸Recipient obligations

Market sounding recipients — their own assessment
The provisions place an obligation on the recipient of a market sounding — they are required to assess for themselves whether the information provided is "inside information". The recipient may have additional information that would mean the info is NOT inside information (already public).
Market sounding example
An investment bank is preparing a secondary share placement for Listed Company X. The bank contacts 15 institutional investors to gauge their interest at various price levels. That is a market sounding. The bank must: (1) obtain consent; (2) inform about restrictions; (3) require confidentiality; (4) record everything. The investors receiving the information are prohibited from trading in X shares or related derivatives.
2.4

Defences — General + Special

▸General Defences (CJA 1993)

General defences — available to all defendants accused of insider dealing / encouraging others:

1
No profit expected — the defendant did not expect the dealing to result in profit (or avoid a loss) due to the information.
2
Information widely disclosed — the defendant believed on reasonable grounds that the information had been sufficiently widely disclosed so that none of those taking part in the dealing would be prejudiced by not being aware.
3
Would have acted anyway — the defendant would have acted in the same way regardless of being in possession of the information. E.g., institutional portfolio rebalancing.

▸Defences only for the disclosure offence

For the disclosing-only offence, defences:

  • The defendant did not expect any person to deal
  • The defendant may have expected a person to deal, but did not expect the dealing to result in a profit (or avoid a loss) due to the information
Important nuance — "no one would deal"
The defence "not expecting any person to deal" is ONLY against the disclosure offence (s.53(1)(c) CJA). It is not available for the dealing offence (s.52(1)) or the encouraging-to-deal offence (s.52(2)(a)).

▸Special Defences — in particular circumstances

Special defences — further defences in particular circumstances:

▸(1) Market Makers

Market maker special defence (s.53 CJA)
If a market maker can show they acted in good faith in the course of their business as a market maker — they are not guilty of insider dealing or encouraging another to deal. A market maker can hold unpublished price-sensitive information as an insider and continue making a market.

Why this defence exists: market makers must deal continuously — even when they may receive information. The law requires continuous quoting — if every bit of new info interrupted trading, markets would stop.

A further defence is available to a market maker if they can show they were acting in connection with an acquisition / disposal where the price was under negotiation and they acted to facilitate the deal. The defendant must show the information was market information arising directly out of the negotiations.

▸(2) Market Information

Market information includes, for example:

  • Information about a sale of a block of securities under consideration
  • The price at which the transaction is likely to be completed

An insider is not guilty of dealing / encouraging others if they can prove:

  • The information held was market information
  • It was reasonable for the defendant to act as they did despite having the information as an insider

Whether the action was reasonable depends on:

  • Content of the information
  • Circumstances in which the defendant first had the information, and in what capacity
  • Capacity in which the defendant now acts
Textbook example — market information defence
A client discussed with a broker the purchase of a block of 10,000 shares in XYZ plc. The client instructs the broker to buy. Clearly, the broker has unpublished information that the buy order exists before dealing.

This is market information — a specific defence against a charge of insider dealing.

The defence applies equally where it is market information relating to the client's planned disposal of securities.

Consideration: size of the order + whether the size is significant enough to move the underlying share price.

▸(3) Price Stabilisation

A defence is available if the dealing / encouragement was done in conformity with the FCA's price stabilisation rules (MAR 2). See Sec 1.

▸(4) Legitimate Behaviour

Additional specific categories of behaviour deemed legitimate — not constituting insider dealing. Covered in Section 3 (Market Manipulation).

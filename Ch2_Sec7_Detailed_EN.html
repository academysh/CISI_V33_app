Chapter 2, Section 7 — Detailed Deep Dive
Advising and Selling Standards · Syllabus 2.10-2.11 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 7 is the most technical and most exam-heavy part of Chapter 2. It governs how a firm serves clients across two fundamentally different regimes: when advice is given (advised → suitability) and when no advice is given (non-advised → appropriateness). Plus additional topics: churning, suitability reports, insistent clients, and the execution-only exemption. Expect 5-8 exam questions, with lots of detail on thresholds, timing, and exceptions. This is one of the sections with the most numbers and specific wordings.

INTRODUCTION

The two fundamental axes of this section: advised vs non-advised

The whole of Section 7 revolves around one central question: how much responsibility does the firm bear for the client's investment decision? The answer depends on what exactly the firm is doing.

ADVISED → SUITABILITY
The firm gives a personal recommendation to the client or manages their portfolio.

The firm must understand the client fully: their objectives, financial situation, knowledge, experience.

The firm bears full responsibility for ensuring the recommendation is suitable for the client.

3 information areas to collect
NON-ADVISED → APPROPRIATENESS
The firm does NOT give a recommendation; the client decides what to buy.

The firm must satisfy itself that the client has sufficient knowledge and experience to understand the risks.

The client bears responsibility for the decision; the firm is responsible for the appropriateness test.

Only 1 information area to collect
Three regimes, three levels of protection
1. Suitability — the strictest: for advised business. 3 information areas + full firm responsibility for suitability.
2. Appropriateness — intermediate: for non-advised business in complex instruments. 1 area (knowledge & experience) + warnings.
3. Execution-only — the lightest: NO assessments. Only for non-complex instruments if the 3 conditions are met.

Each of these regimes is covered in detail below. Plus special cases: churning (excessive trading), insistent clients (who insist against a recommendation), and suitability reports (written documents for the client).

7.1

Application of Suitability Rules — when suitability applies

The COBS suitability rules apply in three cases — critical to know for the exam:

  1. The firm makes personal recommendations (investment advice) in relation to designated investments — including buy, hold or sell recommendations for a particular financial instrument.
  2. The firm manages investments on behalf of a retail client.
  3. The firm manages assets of an occupational pension scheme, stakeholder pension scheme or personal pension scheme — other than in relation to MiFID, equivalent third-country or optional exemption business.
Exam — key nuances of suitability scope
• MiFID suitability covers BUY + HOLD + SELL — not only buy recommendations! Advising "hold your position" also triggers suitability.
• For non-MiFID business — suitability applies only to retail clients + managing pension scheme assets.
• Execution-only and general information do NOT trigger suitability.
• Personal recommendation = recommendation to a specific person about a specific instrument. General educational content is not a personal recommendation.

▸Portfolio management — when suitability assessment is needed

If the firm manages the client's portfolio, it must conduct a suitability assessment:

  • At the start of the mandate — required.
  • Before recommending changes to the mandate that require client consent — required.
  • Keep client information up to date on an ongoing basis.

▸Incentive schemes and FCA guidance

When assessing suitability, firms must have policies and procedures ensuring they understand the nature, features (including costs and risks) of instruments selected for clients. The FCA expects firms to:

  • Consider whether their incentive schemes increase the risk of misselling, and if so — review governance/systems/controls.
  • Take action to address inadequacies.

The FCA has identified common areas where firms may need to do more to manage incentive risks effectively:

  • Checking for spikes or trends in the sales patterns of individual advisers to identify areas of increased risk
  • More monitoring of poor sales practices in face-to-face sales conversations
  • Managing risks in discretionary incentive schemes and balanced scorecards, including the risk that discretion could be misused
  • Monitoring non-advised sales to ensure staff incentivised to sell do not give personal recommendations
  • Improving oversight of incentives used by appointed representatives
  • Recognising that remuneration that is effectively 100% variable pay based on sales increases the risk of misselling
7.2

Information Required for Suitability Assessment — 3 areas

For MiFID investment business, if a firm provides investment advice or portfolio management services, MiFID II requires information across three areas:

# Area What to gather specifically
1 Knowledge and Experience • Types of service, transaction and investment with which the client is familiar
• Nature, volume, frequency and period of past involvement in such transactions
• Level of education, profession, or relevant former profession
2 Financial Situation Including ability to bear losses
3 Investment Objectives And risk appetite / risk profile

▸Fact-finding — gathering information

To conduct a suitability assessment, the firm must gather enough information from the client to understand the essential facts about them ("fact-finding"). It must have a reasonable basis to believe that (bearing in mind its nature) the service or transaction:

  • Meets their investment objectives
  • Carries a level of investment risk the client can bear financially
  • Carries risks the client has the experience and knowledge to understand

▸What if the information is not obtained or is incomplete

CRITICAL rule
Firms must NOT discourage clients from providing this information. If the firm does not obtain the information needed to assess suitability, it must not make a personal recommendation or take a decision to trade for the client.

All 3 areas are necessary. Missing any of them — cannot advise or trade.

▸Relying on client-provided information

The firm is entitled to rely on information provided by the client — unless it is manifestly out of date, inaccurate or incomplete. In other words, the firm is not obliged to verify every client statement, but where something is obviously implausible or out of date, clarification should be sought.

Practical example of information gathering
Maria, 45, comes in for advice on investing a £100K inheritance. Fact-find must cover:
Knowledge & Experience: Previously invested in ISAs (UCITS funds) for 10 years; MBA in finance; worked as an accountant at an insurance company for 15 years. → Sufficient knowledge for complex products.
Financial Situation: Salary £70K, emergency fund £20K, mortgage £150K with 8 years left, no children. → Can bear moderate losses.
Objectives + Risk Appetite: Goal — buy a house in 15 years; comfortable with medium risk; does not want to lose more than 20% of capital. → Balanced portfolio with a growth tilt.
7.3

Assessing Suitability for Professional Clients

For professional clients the rules are modified — the firm is entitled to make certain assumptions that reduce the assessment burden.

Client type Assumption about knowledge & experience Assumption about ability to bear losses
Per se professional ✓ Can assume ✓ Can assume
Elective professional ✓ Can assume ✗ CANNOT assume — must assess
Retail ✗ Cannot — must assess ✗ Cannot — must assess
Exam key fact
Per se professional — can assume BOTH (knowledge/experience AND financial loss-bearing capacity).
Elective professional — can assume ONLY knowledge/experience. Ability to bear losses must be assessed.
Retail — assess everything fully.
The distinction between per se and elective professional is a favourite exam question.
Important: suitability does NOT disappear for professionals
Professional clients are still under the suitability rules for MiFID business. The assumptions only reduce the assessment burden — they do not eliminate it. The firm must still consider whether the investment matches the client's objectives.
7.4

Churning and Switching — excessive trading

▸Definitions

"Churning" is the activity of over-dealing or trading more frequently than is needed for the client, in order to generate additional fees or commissions for the firm. It is one of the grossest breaches of fiduciary duty.

Churning is most relevant in the context of discretionary portfolio management — because in that setup, the firm makes trading decisions without needing to seek the client's approval for each trade.

"Switching" is the activity of selling one investment and replacing it with another. Not wrong in itself — clients sometimes genuinely should switch. But frequent or unnecessary switching can become churning.

▸FCA guidance on switching and suitability

FCA guidance on switching sits in the COBS suitability rules. The key principle:

The "collectively unsuitable" rule
In the context of assessing suitability, a series of transactions that look suitable in isolation may not be suitable if the recommendations and/or trading decisions are so frequent as to be detrimental to the client.

That is — each trade in isolation may be reasonable, yet collectively they harm the client (through excessive fees, taxes, transaction costs).

▸Considering the client's investment strategy

Firms must bear in mind the client's investment strategy when determining how frequently to deal for them, including whether switching between packaged products is suitable.

Example of churning in a discretionary account
A discretionary portfolio manager makes 35 trades in one month for a conservative retail client with a long-term income strategy. This is significantly above average (say, typically 3-5 trades per month for this profile).

The problem:
• Excessive trading inconsistent with the client's investment strategy (conservative, long-term).
• Each trade might be justifiable in isolation, but collectively they are detrimental — transaction costs, taxes, market timing risk.
• The manager potentially generates unnecessary fees for the firm.
→ Classic churning concern. FCA investigation likely.
Example of a legitimate switch
A client holds Fund A (emerging markets equity) with an AMC of 1.8%. Fund B hits the market — same strategy, AMC 0.8%, no performance difference. The firm switches the client from A to B.

This is a legitimate switch: the client gets better value (lower fees), the investment strategy is unchanged. No pattern of frequent switches — one sensible move.
7.5

The Suitability Report — the written document for the client

A suitability report is a written document for the client explaining the recommendation. MiFID II extends the requirements: the suitability report is now required for retail clients for any investment advice (which now equates to a personal recommendation).

▸When a suitability report is required

The firm must provide a suitability report if it makes a personal recommendation. This applies to any investment advice to acquire or sell any investment, including:

  • Equities
  • Derivatives
  • Structured products
  • Unregulated collective investments

The requirement also applies to products covered under the previous regime (MiFID) if the recommendation is to:

  • Acquire a holding / sell all or part of a holding in:
    • A regulated CIS
    • An investment trust, where shares have been or are to be acquired through an investment trust savings scheme, or
    • An investment trust where shares are to be held in an ISA which has been promoted as the means for investing in one or more specific investment trusts
  • Buy, sell, surrender, convert or cancel rights under, or suspend contributions to, a personal pension scheme or stakeholder pension scheme
  • Elect to make income withdrawals or purchase a short-term annuity
  • Pension transfer or pension opt-out
  • Personal recommendation in connection with a life assurance policy

▸5 Exceptions from the suitability report requirement

There are 5 exceptions — a frequent exam topic:

  1. The firm, acting as investment manager for a retail client, makes a personal recommendation in connection with a regulated CIS.
  2. The client is habitually resident outside the UK or not present in the UK at the time of acknowledging consent.
  3. The personal recommendation is made by a friendly society in connection with a small life policy, premium ≤ £50 per year or (if payable weekly) ≤ £1 per week.
  4. The personal recommendation is to increase a regular premium to an existing contract.
  5. The personal recommendation is to invest additional single premiums or single contributions to an existing packaged product to which a single premium / contribution has previously been paid.
Exam — the friendly society numbers
£50/year or £1/week — thresholds for the friendly society small life policy exception. Typical distractor numbers: £100, £25 — all wrong.

▸Timing — when the suitability report must be provided

Situation Timing of the suitability report
Life policy Before the contract is concluded — unless information is provided orally OR cover is required immediately → report in durable medium immediately after contract conclusion
Personal / stakeholder pension (if cancellation rules apply) Within 14 days of concluding the contract
Other cases Before the transaction is concluded — unless distance marketing communication + client consents → ASAP after the transaction is effected / executed

▸3 mandatory content elements of the suitability report

The suitability report must, at least:

  1. Specify the client's demands and needs
  2. Explain any possible disadvantages of the transaction for the client
  3. Explain why the firm concluded the recommended transaction is suitable for the client, having due regard to the information provided by the client

▸Telephone-only life policy — a special case

If the transaction is the sale of a life policy by telephone, and the only contact between firm and client before conclusion is by telephone, the suitability report must:

  • Comply with Distance Marketing Directive (DMD) rules
  • Be provided immediately after conclusion of the contract
  • Be in a durable medium
Telephone life policy scenario
A client phones the broker: "I urgently need a term life insurance policy, I'm leaving tomorrow for a high-risk business trip — give me the recommendation and set it up right now." The broker gives a recommendation verbally, the client signs up on the phone, the policy becomes effective immediately.

What to do about the suitability report: DMD compliant + immediately after conclusion + durable medium. I.e. an email with the full suitability report within minutes of the call, covering demands/needs + disadvantages + why suitable.
7.6

Dealing with Insistent Clients — clients who insist against a recommendation

▸Definition of an insistent client

An insistent client is one where 3 conditions are met:

  1. The firm has given the client a personal recommendation.
  2. The client decides NOT to enter into the transaction, or enters into a transaction different from the one recommended in the personal recommendation.
  3. The client wishes the firm to facilitate the transaction (despite the disagreement).

▸4 elements that must be communicated

When the firm executes a transaction for an insistent client that is not in accordance with the personal recommendation, it must communicate to the client in a way that is fair, clear and not misleading, and having regard to the information needs of the insistent client so that the client can understand the information, the following 4 elements:

  1. The firm has NOT recommended the transaction, and the transaction is not in accordance with the personal recommendation.
  2. Reasons why the transaction is not in accordance with the recommendation.
  3. Risks of the transaction proposed by the insistent client.
  4. Reasons why the firm did not recommend this transaction.
Exam critical point
All 4 elements must be communicated before the firm facilitates the transaction. This is not just a warning, it is a full explanation with reasons. Do not confuse with a simple appropriateness warning (which is a single warning, not 4 reasoning elements).

▸Acknowledgement from the client

Additionally: the firm should obtain an acknowledgement from the insistent client that the transaction is not in accordance with the firm's personal recommendation and is being carried out at the client's request.

▸Must the firm facilitate?

Key nuance — NO automatic duty or prohibition
After communicating the 4 elements + obtaining acknowledgement, the firm MAY decide to facilitate — it's a matter of judgement. There is no automatic prohibition on facilitating the transaction. Equally, there is no obligation to facilitate if the firm considers it harmful to the client.
Real-life insistent client example
The firm recommends a 60-year-old client to hold his balanced retirement portfolio (70% equities, 30% bonds). The client speaks with a friend who made money in crypto and decides to sell everything and invest in a single crypto asset.

Firm's obligations:
(1) Explain: "We did not recommend this transaction. It is not in accordance with our recommendation."
(2) "Reasons: you are planning to retire in 5 years, you need liquidity and predictability, crypto is highly volatile."
(3) "Risks: crypto can lose 80-90% over a few months, putting your retirement at risk."
(4) "Why we did not recommend it: inconsistent with your age/objectives/risk profile."
Then: obtain acknowledgement. Then: decide whether to facilitate or refuse. The firm is also entitled to refuse if it considers this harmful to the client — that too is a legitimate outcome.
7.7

Appropriateness (Non-Advised Sales) — the appropriateness test

The rules on non-advised sales apply to a range of MiFID (and some non-MiFID) investment services that do not involve advice or discretionary portfolio management.

▸Scope — to whom appropriateness applies

Specifically, the rules apply to firms:

  1. Providing MiFID investment services, other than personal recommendations or managing investments (because those services are already covered by the suitability rules).
  2. Arranging deals or dealing in warrants and derivatives for retail clients, where the firm is, or should be, aware that the client's application or order is in response to a direct offer financial promotion.
  3. Which assess appropriateness on behalf of other UK MiFID firms.

The purpose of the appropriateness test is to provide a degree of protection for non-advised transactions.

▸Information — only ONE area (unlike suitability)

When the firm provides one of the above services, it must ask the client for information about knowledge and experience in the investment field specific to the type of product/service offered by the firm and/or requested by the client, so that the firm can assess whether the product/service is appropriate.

Suitability vs Appropriateness comparison
Suitability: 3 information areas (knowledge/experience + financial situation + objectives).
Appropriateness: ONLY 1 area — knowledge and experience.

The logic: for non-advised the client makes the decision; the firm only checks that they understand the risks. Financial situation and objectives are not relevant — the client knows their own position and makes their own choice.

▸Assumptions for professional clients

For appropriateness, the firm may assume that a professional client has the necessary knowledge and experience for the services/products for which they are classified as professional. This applies to both per se and elective professional clients.

▸Inferring knowledge from experience

Where it is reasonable to do so, the firm may infer knowledge from experience when assessing appropriateness. The firm may also satisfy itself that the client's knowledge alone is sufficient to understand the risks.

▸What to do if the product is NOT appropriate

If the firm concludes that the product/service is NOT appropriate for the client, it MUST WARN the client of that fact. The warning may be in a standardised format.

▸What to do if the client refuses to provide information

If the client declines to provide information, the firm must warn the client that it is unable to assess whether the product/service is appropriate. The warning may also be in a standardised format.

If the client then asks to proceed — the firm may decide whether to do so (judgement call). There is no automatic prohibition.

7.8

Execution-Only Exemption and non-complex instruments

There is a third regime, separate from suitability and appropriateness — execution-only. In this regime, the firm makes no assessment: it simply executes the client's order. But this regime is only available under strict conditions.

▸3 conditions for the execution-only exemption (ALL must be met)

  1. Service at the client's initiative (execution-only or reception/transmission of orders).
  2. The client is clearly informed that the firm does NOT assess appropriateness and that they will not benefit from that protection.
  3. The firm complies with conflicts of interest obligations.

▸But that's not enough — a non-complex instrument is also required

Execution-only is only available for non-complex instruments. For complex instruments (warrants, derivatives, structured products) appropriateness is required, even if the client is entirely on their own initiative.

▸4 non-complex instrument criteria (ALL must be met)

  1. NOT a derivative.
  2. Sufficient liquidity.
  3. Client liability does NOT exceed the cost of acquisition — the client cannot lose more than they paid.
  4. Publicly available comprehensive information.

▸Examples of non-complex vs complex

Non-complex (execution-only OK) Complex (appropriateness required)
• Listed shares on a UK/EEA regulated market
• Money market instruments
• Bonds WITHOUT embedded derivatives
• UCITS funds (explicitly listed in the rules) 		• Warrants
• Derivatives (futures, options, CFDs)
• Structured products with embedded derivatives
• Hedge funds not admitted to a regulated market
Key non-complex examples
UCITS funds — always non-complex (explicitly listed).
Listed shares on a regulated market — non-complex.
Bonds without embedded derivatives — non-complex.
A warrant — complex (it's a derivative).
A structured product with an embedded derivative — complex.

▸Series of similar transactions

Firms do not need to re-assess appropriateness each time for a series of similar transactions/services. However, they must reassess before beginning a new service.

Example — series vs new service
The client opened an execution-only account and made a trade in Apple shares — appropriateness was assessed once. They then do 50 more equity trades in similar non-complex instruments — no need to re-assess (series).

But when the client suddenly wants to trade CFDs (complex derivatives) — that is a new type of service. Appropriateness must be re-assessed separately for that class of instrument.

▸What to do if the client insists on a non-appropriate product

If the firm has assessed that the product is NOT appropriate, and the client still wants to proceed:

  • The firm must warn clearly about this finding.
  • The client may decide to proceed at their own risk.
  • The firm may execute the transaction — but the warning must be given.

The transaction is not automatically prohibited. The client is free to decide; the firm fulfils its duty through the warning.

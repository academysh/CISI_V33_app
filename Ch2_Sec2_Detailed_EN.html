Chapter 2, Section 2 — Detailed Deep Dive
Appointed Representatives (ARs) · Syllabus 2.2 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
An Appointed Representative (AR) is a kind of "satellite" of an authorised firm. The AR itself does not hold an FCA licence, but can carry on regulated business under the umbrella of another firm (called the principal). The principal is the one responsible for the AR's conduct, as if the AR were its own employee. In 2022, the FCA significantly tightened the AR regime following several scandals — this section explains how the system works today.

INTRODUCTION

What an Appointed Representative is and why it exists

Under the basic principle of FSMA 2000, to carry on regulated activity in the UK you must be either an authorised person or an exempt person. An Appointed Representative is precisely an exempt person under Section 39 FSMA and the FSMA (Appointed Representatives) Regulations 2001.

The idea is simple: there is a large authorised firm (say, an investment company), and it wants to distribute its services through smaller local advisers or agents. Instead of each of them going through the expensive and slow FCA authorisation process, they become ARs of that larger firm — and operate under its licence and responsibility.

Real-world example
Major insurance company Aviva (the principal) wants to sell its life policies across the country. Rather than setting up 500 of its own branches, it signs agreements with 500 small independent brokerage firms. Each such firm becomes an AR of Aviva — they sell its policies, receive commission, but technically they are not FCA-authorised. Responsibility for their conduct rests with Aviva.
Core idea of the AR regime
The AR's exempt status is not a free ride. The exemption from authorisation is given in exchange for the principal taking on full responsibility for the AR's conduct and its ongoing supervision. This is an important trade-off: "less red tape for the AR — but all the legal responsibility sits with the principal."
2.1

How COBS applies to Appointed Representatives

The COBS rules apply to firms in respect of the activities carried out for them by their ARs. In other words, COBS does not apply directly to the AR (the AR is an exempt person; FCA rules formally do not bite at its level). But any business conducted by the AR for which the principal has accepted responsibility is treated as having been conducted by the principal itself.

Important legal point
Formally, the AR is not authorised → FCA rules do not apply to it. But in practice, through the principal, they apply in full. If the AR breaches COBS, the regulator does not go to the AR (it has no formal regulatory relationship with the AR) — it goes to the principal.

▸What the principal is required to do

  • The principal must hold the relevant permissions for the activities that its AR will carry out. For example, if the AR is going to advise on investments — the principal must hold permission to advise on investments.
  • Inside the AR firm, certain individuals must be approved for controlled functions — namely the governors (directors, partners) and individuals performing customer functions (those dealing with clients).
  • The principal must conduct thorough reviews of the suitability and conduct of its ARs — both before appointment and on an ongoing basis.
Example — principal's permissions
Brokerage firm "City Brokers Ltd" holds an FCA permission only for arranging deals. It wants to appoint an AR that will advise clients on investments. This is impossible: for the AR to give advice, City Brokers must first obtain permission to advise on investments. Without the matching permission at the principal level, the AR cannot give any advice.
2.2

AR financial promotions — a very important topic

This is one of the most frequently tested exam topics. Let's work through the logic step by step.

Under Section 21 FSMA, a financial promotion can only be made by an authorised person or an exempt person. Unauthorised persons cannot issue promotions themselves — their materials must be approved by an authorised "gateway" firm.

Question: An AR is an exempt person. Therefore, technically, a promotion communicated by an AR is an exempt promotion, and formally it does not require approval.

However! The FCA specifically included in COBS: a firm (the principal) is required to ensure that its ARs comply with the COBS rules when communicating any financial promotions. In other words, the formal "exempt" status does not relieve the principal of responsibility for the content.

Why the FCA made a specific point of this
Without this reminder, a loophole could open up: the AR communicates a promotion, it is exempt, no approval is needed, so anything goes. The FCA closed that loophole: the principal is always responsible for the content of its ARs' promotions.
Scenario: AR sends a bad marketing email
"AR Solutions Ltd" is an appointed representative of "PrincipalCorp plc". AR Solutions sends a marketing email about a new investment product to retail clients. The email contains misleading claims about past performance. Who is responsible?

Answer: PrincipalCorp plc. Although the AR sent the email, the principal bears legal responsibility for the content of all financial promotions communicated by its ARs. Clients are not obliged to verify claims themselves — that is the firm's duty. The FCA will fine PrincipalCorp.
Exam — key fact
For which promotions is the principal responsible? All types. Not only MiFID instruments, not only retail promotions, not only high-risk ones — ALL OF THEM. This is a direct exam question (Q26 in the official sample paper).
2.3

Which regulated activities can an AR carry on

The AR Regulations limit the set of regulated activities an AR may carry on under its exempt basis. The list is closed — here it is:

Activity Status for AR
Dealing in investments as agent — only pure protection or general insurance contracts ✓ Allowed (restricted)
Arranging deals in investments and making arrangements with a view to transactions ✓ Allowed
Arranging deals in home finance transactions ✓ Allowed
Assisting in the administration and performance of a contract of insurance ✓ Allowed
Arranging the safeguarding and administration of assets ✓ Allowed
Advising on investments ✓ Allowed
Giving basic advice on stakeholder products ✓ Allowed
Advising on home finance transactions ✓ Allowed
Agreeing to carry on a regulated activity ✓ Allowed
Operating electronic platforms in relation to lending (P2P) ✓ Allowed
Certain consumer credit related activities ✓ Allowed
Dealing in investments AS PRINCIPAL ✗ PROHIBITED
Managing investments (portfolio management) ✗ PROHIBITED
Two prohibited activities — you must remember these
ARs CANNOT:
1. Deal as principal (trading on their own account)
2. Manage investments (discretionary portfolio management)

Why? Both activities are considered too high-risk for exempt status. They require direct FCA authorisation and their own prudential oversight (capital adequacy, etc.).
Example: AR wants to manage a portfolio
A client asks their AR adviser to take discretionary authority over a £500,000 portfolio. The AR adviser must refuse — this is a prohibited activity. The only way forward is for the client to sign the contract directly with the principal (which holds permission to manage investments); the AR can only provide organisational support in that process.

▸Nuance on dealing as agent

ARs can deal as agent, but only for pure protection contracts (protection-only insurance policies, e.g. term life cover) and general insurance contracts (motor, home insurance, etc.). ARs cannot act as agent for shares, bonds, or derivatives.

▸Consumer credit — a special rule

A firm can hold limited permission for certain regulated activities in consumer credit, and at the same time be an AR of another firm for other (non-consumer-credit) activities. This is an unusual "dual" position, but it is specifically permitted by the FCA.

2.4

The 2022 reforms — what changed and why

In 2021, the FCA consulted on reforming the AR regime. The reason — a string of scandals where ARs caused harm to consumers while principals failed to exercise proper oversight. The new rules came into force at the end of 2022.

The FCA identified two key areas of change:

  • Additional information on ARs and notification requirements for principals.
  • Clarifying and strengthening the responsibilities and expectations of principals.

▸Four target outcomes of the reform

  1. Principals fully understand their responsibilities in relation to ARs, and have stronger and better oversight of, and take more effective responsibility for, their ARs.
  2. The FCA can more effectively challenge firms that already have, or are planning to appoint, ARs.
  3. Principals address problems that cause, or have the potential to cause, harm to consumers or markets.
  4. Retail consumers can access better-quality information on principals and ARs and make good decisions when choosing products and services.

▸Specific new requirements — Information & Notification

Requirement Timing / detail
Notify FCA of a new AR appointment 30 days BEFORE the appointment takes effect
Provide information on existing ARs (one-off on introduction of rules) 60 days from when the rules came into force
Provide data on the business and on ARs, including the nature of their regulated activities Ongoing requirement
Data on AR complaints and revenue Provided to the FCA regularly
FCA updates the public Register with the regulated activities the principal permits its ARs to undertake Ongoing
Notify the FCA whether the principal currently provides, or will cease to provide, regulatory hosting services On change
Exam numbers — do not confuse them!
30 days = notification to FCA of a NEW AR appointment (BEFORE).
60 days = deadline for information on EXISTING ARs (one-off on rules coming into force in 2022).
This is a favourite trap in exams — the context matters.

▸Specific new requirements — Responsibilities

  • Enhanced oversight of ARs, including:
    • adequate systems and controls
    • sufficient resources
    • monitoring AR growth
  • More effective responsibility for ARs:
    • monitoring and assessing the risk of harm to consumers and market integrity
    • oversight to a comparable standard as if the ARs were direct employees
  • Clarity on the circumstances where the principal terminates an AR relationship and assists with an orderly wind down.
  • Annual review of the ARs' activities, business and senior management — the principal must prepare a self-assessment document.
Key oversight standard
The FCA specifically stated: the principal must oversee ARs to a standard comparable to its oversight of direct employees. This is a very high bar — "commercially reasonable" or "reasonable oversight" is not enough. The benchmark: "as if this person were your own full-time employee".
Scenario: AR breaches suitability
"PrincipalBank plc" discovers that its AR "AdviseNow Ltd" has been giving unsuitable investment advice to retail clients. What does the FCA expect PrincipalBank to do?

Correct behaviour: address the problem proactively — do not wait for the FCA to investigate. Consider whether to terminate the AR relationship. On termination — ensure an orderly wind down: so that AR clients are not further harmed, their documents are transferred correctly, and a proper handover is arranged. All of this — at the principal's cost and under its direction.

UK Regulatory Infrastructure · Syllabus 1.5 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 5 is a map of all the players around the FCA and PRA. The UK regulatory environment is more than just the FCA and PRA. Around them are dozens of organisations that either directly regulate (CMA, HMRC, Pensions Regulator), set policy framework (HM Treasury, BoE, FPC), or influence standards (FATF, IOSCO, BIS, trade associations, professional bodies). This section is about who does what. On the exam 1-2 questions, but these often test confusion between similar entities: FPC vs PRA, MPC vs FPC, HMT vs BoE. The section also covers the Pensions Regulator, overseas influence post-Brexit, and oversight bodies (internal/external audit, trustees, compliance, industry/professional bodies, trade associations).

5.1

Competition and Markets Authority (CMA)

The CMA promotes competition for the benefit of consumers, both within and outside the UK. Its aim is to make markets work well for consumers, business, and the economy.

CMA — NOT a financial services body
Although the CMA covers financial services and works closely with the FCA, the CMA is not a financial services body. It is a general-purpose competition regulator for the entire UK economy.

▸CMA's objective

The CMA's objective is to ensure that consumers get a good deal when buying goods and services, and that businesses operate within the law.

▸CMA's activities

The CMA undertakes this in a number of ways:

  • Investigating mergers that could restrict competition
  • Conducting market studies and investigations in markets where there may be competition and consumer problems
  • Investigating where there may be breaches of UK prohibitions against anti-competitive agreements and abuses of dominant positions
  • Bringing criminal proceedings against individuals who commit the cartel offence
  • Enforcing consumer protection legislation — tackling practices/market conditions that make it difficult for consumers to exercise choice
  • Cooperating with sector regulators — encouraging them to use competition powers, considering regulatory references and appeals
  • Providing information and advice to individuals and businesses about rights/obligations under competition and consumer law
  • Providing advice, reporting, and monitoring in relation to government subsidies

▸3 CMA outcomes

  1. People can be confident they are getting great choices and fair deals
  2. Competitive, fair-dealing businesses can innovate and thrive
  3. The UK economy can grow productively and sustainably
Example — CMA vs FCA on retail banking
The FCA assesses how banks sell products (conduct). The CMA assesses whether there is competition between banks — e.g. whether a client can easily switch between providers, whether there are barriers for newcomers. In 2017, after a retail banking market investigation, the CMA forced the big-9 banks to open APIs to fintechs — this became the Open Banking initiative. A prime example of FCA + CMA working together.
5.2

HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC)

HMRC is the UK's primary revenue-raising agency. It collects income tax, corporation tax, VAT, national insurance contributions, and customs duties.

HMRC — NOT a financial regulator
HMRC does not regulate financial services firms. HMRC's relevance to financial services is through information-sharing arrangements with the FCA and PRA.

▸HMRC + FCA + PRA — information sharing

The FCA, PRA, and HMRC have agreed to share information and expertise. The agreement aims to enhance their:

  • Understanding of banks and their behavioural responses to regulatory, tax, and other changes
  • Helping to identify significant issues undermining their objectives
  • Enabling necessary action to address issues more quickly and on a more coordinated basis
What the agreement does NOT mean
It does not mean that the FCA receives individual taxpayer records, or that HMRC regulates firms. It is about mutual understanding + coordinated action on systemic issues.
5.3

Financial Policy Committee (FPC)

▸FPC background

A stable financial system is a key ingredient of a healthy and successful economy. Since 2009, the Bank of England has had a statutory objective to "contribute to protecting and enhancing the stability of the financial systems of the UK".

From April 2013, the BoE took on further responsibilities — the creation of an independent FPC at the BoE.

▸FPC role

FPC's mandate — macroprudential
The FPC identifies, monitors, and takes action to remove or reduce systemic risks — with a view to protecting and enhancing the resilience of the UK financial system.

The FPC focuses on macroeconomic and financial issues that may threaten the long-term growth prospects of the UK economy. This is "macroprudential" regulation — looking at the system as a whole, not individual firms.

▸Meetings and FSR

The FPC meets at least 4 times a year and publishes a record of formal meetings. Also responsible for the BoE's biannual Financial Stability Report (FSR):

  • Covers the Committee's assessment of the outlook for stability and resilience of the financial sector
  • Policy actions it advises to reduce and mitigate risks to economic stability
FPC vs PRA — critical distinction
FPC = macroprudential (system-wide risks — asset bubbles, excessive leverage, interconnected exposures).
PRA = microprudential (individual firm soundness — does this bank have enough capital?).
Both are part of the BoE family, but at different levels: the FPC looks at the forest, the PRA looks at individual trees.
5.4

Bank of England (BoE) — central bank

The BoE performs all the functions of a central bank. Most important:

  • Maintaining price stability
  • Supporting the economic policies of the Government — promoting economic growth

Two main areas — Monetary Stability and Financial Stability.

▸(1) Monetary Stability — MPC

Stable prices + confidence in the currency — the 2 main criteria for monetary stability. Stable prices are maintained by making sure price increases meet the Government's inflation target.

Monetary Policy Committee (MPC)
The BoE aims to meet the target by adjusting the bank interest base rate, agreed/decided by the Bank's Monetary Policy Committee (MPC), which meets 8 times a year (roughly every six weeks).

▸(2) Financial Stability

Maintaining financial stability — protecting against threats to the whole financial system. The BoE's surveillance and market intelligence functions detect these threats. Threats are dealt with through:

  • Financial and other operations, both at home and abroad
  • In exceptional circumstances, the BoE may act as lender of last resort — extending credit to banks and other institutions when no other institutions will
"Lender of last resort" — not routine
This is exceptional circumstances only — providing emergency liquidity to prevent a wider system collapse. Not a routine facility, not available on request. The BoE uses its own judgment, based on assessment. Reserved for situations where a firm's failure could cause systemic damage.

▸BoE works with other institutions

  • HM Treasury — the Government department responsible for financial and economic policy
  • Other central banks and international organisations — aim of improving the international financial system
MPC vs FPC — a frequent confusion
Both are committees within the BoE, but with different mandates:
• MPC — sets interest rates (monetary stability), meets 8 times/year.
• FPC — identifies systemic risks (financial stability), publishes FSR biannually, meets at least 4 times/year.
5.5

HM Treasury (HMT)

HM Treasury (HMT), sometimes referred to as "the Treasury", is the department responsible for developing and executing the British Government's public finance and economic policy.

▸HMT's role in financial services

HMT — overall responsibility for financial services
HMT has overall responsibility for the financial services sector in the UK.
  • FCA reports directly to HMT
  • The Chancellor of the Exchequer appoints the FCA's CEO and Chair

▸Government vs regulator — important distinction

HMT develops policy (e.g. decides whether Consumer Duty is needed, whether to reform pensions). The FCA implements policy through rules and supervision. HMT cannot dictate individual supervisory decisions of the FCA — the FCA is operationally independent.

5.6

The Pensions Regulator (TPR) — Occupational Pensions

The TPR protects the UK's workplace pension and ensures scheme members' rights are safeguarded through regulation that monitors:

  • Employers
  • Trustees
  • Pension specialists
  • Business advisers
TPR — work-based pensions only
TPR regulates only work-based pensions. Personal pensions (SIPP, personal pension plan) are outside TPR's scope — they are regulated by the FCA.

▸2 types by who pays

TypeWho pays
ContributoryPart of the employee's salary is added to the employer's contribution — both pay
Non-contributoryOnly the employer makes payments — employer only

▸2 types by benefit structure

TypeStructureWho bears risk
Defined Contribution (DC) A set amount of payment is made to the scheme, no guarantee of the final value of the pot Member bears the risk
Defined Benefit (DB)
(e.g. final salary)		 Scheme members are guaranteed to receive x% of final salary. Details vary per scheme Employer bears the risk
DB vs DC — why it matters
DB schemes are very expensive for employers — if the pension fund underperforms, the employer must make up the shortfall. This is why most UK private sector employers closed DB schemes to new members in the 2000s-2010s.

▸Pensions Act 2008 — automatic enrolment

The main aim of the Pensions Act 2008 was to introduce "automatic enrolment" for workplace pensions, requiring employers to automatically enrol eligible employees into a pension scheme and contribute towards it.

The Act identifies eligible workers as:

  • Over 22 and under State Pension age
  • Earning over a certain threshold

The Pensions Act 2008 requires all UK employers to put certain staff into a workplace pension scheme and contribute towards it. Any employer with at least 1 employee has a legal duty to comply with automatic enrolment.

▸TPR enforcement

TPR can take enforcement actions:

  • Compliance notices
  • Penalty notices
  • Fines

▸Late compliance — backdating

If the employer complies late
The employer is expected to pay back missed contributions — put staff in the position they would have been in had they complied on time. This includes backdating contributions to the date when the staff member first met the age and earnings criteria.

The employer must pay all unpaid employer contributions. The staff member must pay theirs, unless the employer chooses to pay their share too (as part of enforcement action — TPR may require).
5.7

Overseas Legislation Impact + International Bodies

▸Post-Brexit framework

Following Brexit, the UK government introduced legislation that converted existing direct EU legislation (Regulations) into UK law, and preserved existing UK laws implementing EU obligations.

The European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018 gave the government powers to amend current legislation — ensuring that it functions when the UK is no longer an EU member state. This allowed the same rules and laws to apply on the day after exit as on the day before — as far as possible.

▸Why overseas legislation impacts the UK

Many firms operate globally, offering products and services with similar but different local rules, impacting how firms undertake operational activities.

Examples of extraterritorial reach
Dodd-Frank (US) and EMIR (EU) have extraterritorial requirements — based on who the client is and where they are domiciled. For example: a branch of a UK bank has a UK EMIR reporting obligation no matter where the derivative transaction was executed and no matter the domicile of the other client.

Another example — requirements based on where the issuer is listing shares or bonds: having to comply with local listing, disclosure, and reporting obligations.

▸International Intergovernmental Bodies

The following intergovernmental (supernational) bodies do not make rules that directly apply in the UK, but their recommendations shape the structure and future of UK financial services regulation.

FATFAML / CTF
Financial Action Task Force — global anti-money laundering and terrorist financing body. Sets international standards aimed at preventing illegal activities. A policy-making body.
• Developed 40 Recommendations — a coordinated global response to prevent organised crime, corruption, terrorism
• Helps authorities go after the money of criminals (drugs, human trafficking)
• Stops funding for weapons of mass destruction
• Over 200 countries have committed to implementing the Recommendations
• Reviews ML and TF techniques — strengthens standards (e.g. regulation of virtual assets / crypto)
• Monitors countries + holds them to account
BISCentral banks' bank
Bank for International Settlements — the "bank for central banks". Fosters international monetary and financial cooperation + promotes financial stability.
• Established 1930
• Hub for central bankers + financial regulators — share knowledge, economic research, support global financial standards
• Owned and governed by central banks
• Board of Directors — 18 members, including 6 "ex-officio" (Central Bank Governors of Belgium, France, Germany, Italy, UK, USA)
• 63 central banks are members
• UK represented by the Governor of the BoE
IOSCOSecurities standards
International Organisation of Securities Commissions — the global body setting international standards for securities regulation.
• Cooperative forum for securities regulatory agencies from 130+ jurisdictions
• Aim: protect investors, maintain fair/efficient/transparent markets, reduce systemic risk
• Develops IOSCO Principles
• IOSCO Board — 34 securities regulators
• UK represented by the CEO of the FCA
5.8

Oversight and Influencing Bodies

▸(1) Internal Audit

The internal audit function plays a crucial role in the ongoing maintenance and assessment of the firm's internal control, risk management, and governance systems. Both internal auditors and supervisors use risk-based approaches — to determine their respective work plans and actions.

Internal audit is expected to develop an independent and informed view of the risks faced by the firm — based on:

  • Access to all records and data
  • Enquiries
  • Professional competence
Reporting line — critical for independence
Internal audit must discuss views, findings, and conclusions directly with the audit committee and the board of directors. Helping the board oversee senior management.

Does not report to the CEO or Compliance — that would compromise independence.

▸(2) External Audit

A firm's external auditors play a crucial role in assessing the firm's risk and control infrastructure. The external auditor of a firm should:

  • Have relevant industry knowledge and competence — to respond appropriately to the risks of material misstatement + meet additional regulatory requirements
  • Be objective and independent in BOTH fact AND appearance with respect to the firm
  • Ensure reporting directly from auditor to supervisor, unless not permitted (then indirectly through the firm)
  • Exercise professional scepticism when planning and performing the audit
  • Comply with applicable standards of quality control
  • Identify and assess risks of material misstatement in financial statements
  • Respond appropriately to significant risks of material misstatement in the bank's financial statements

▸(3) Trustees

A trust is a type of legal arrangement that allows a suitable third party (trustee) to hold and manage an asset on behalf of an organisation/beneficiary. Various types:

  • Administrative trustees
  • Investment trustees
  • Pension scheme trustees
  • Charity trustees

Trustees can delegate management of investments to those with relevant professional experience (internal or external), and pass oversight of portfolios to a finance or investment committee. But trustees must be aware that they are ultimately responsible for any decisions.

▸(4) Compliance Function

The compliance function's responsibilities are to assist senior management in effectively managing the compliance risks faced by the firm.

Post-Brexit — ESMA guidance treatment
The FCA acknowledged it will continue to comply with and expect firms to comply with all Level 3 measures (Guidelines, Q&A, opinions), published and adopted by EU supervisory bodies (ESMA, EIOPA, EBA) when the UK was part of the EU. However, subsequent changes from ESMA will not apply in the UK. The FCA is gradually replacing them with its own guidance.

▸(5) Industry Bodies

Industry bodies are similar to trade associations (have members, work for their members), but not product specific — more generalist.

In financial services they "champion" and support the success of the UK-based financial and professional service regulatory architecture.

Example — TheCityUK
TheCityUK represents the broad spectrum of the financial + related professional services industry — sustaining London's reputation as a global capital of finance. Remit: "promotion of the UK as a world leading international financial centre at home and overseas".

Members can be at:

  • Individual level — senior individuals in regulated firms, acting in personal capacity
  • Firm level — individuals acting on behalf of the firm

Members are subject to a code of conduct, expected to be adhered to over and above any code of conduct from employers.

FX Global Code
An example — the FX Global Code — a set of principles applying to both firms and individuals, aimed at providing a common set of guidelines to promote integrity and effective running of the wholesale FX market.

▸(6) Professional Bodies

Membership of a professional body brings important benefits — individuals concerned + public interest.

Banking and finance — NO statutory powers
In UK banking and finance, with the exception of accountancy and actuarial sectors, no professional body has statutory regulatory powers, and membership is voluntary.

Members are bound by codes of conduct — upheld vigorously. Expected to comply with these codes, which take precedence over the firm's internal code — this can create an ethical dilemma.

▸CISI — a professional body

Professional bodies are responsible for the development and management of professional examinations useful and relevant for individuals working in the financial industry (CISI, Chartered Banker Institute). Membership is at the individual level, where they are required to attain a level of knowledge and skills.

The aim — raise levels of competence, knowledge, and understanding + promote ethical behaviours.

CISI website states
The CISI's objective is to "set standards of professional excellence and integrity for securities, investment, wealth and financial planning professionals, providing qualifications and promoting the highest level of competence".

CISI expects members to comply with its "code of conduct" — over and above any code applied to individuals by their employer.

▸(7) Trade Associations

Trade associations provide representative and other collective services to firms, generally in a specific sector with common interests.

They work with members on:

  • Development of markets and rules — advocating stable, competitive, sustainable markets
  • Undertake advocacy on behalf of members with:
    • Legislators (HM Treasury)
    • Regulators (PRA/FCA)
  • Assist the development of rules — ensure proportionate + guidance for firms
Industry bodies vs Trade associations
Trade associations — product-specific / sector-specific (e.g. AIC for investment companies, BBA for banks — now UK Finance).
Industry bodies — broader, more generalist, promoting the sector as a whole (TheCityUK).

CHAPTER 1
Chapter 1, Section 6 — Detailed Deep Dive
Regulated and Prohibited Activities · Syllabus 1.6 + 1.7 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 6 is about what exactly is regulated in UK financial services. FSMA sets the general principle — the general prohibition (Section 19) — but does not spell out precisely which activities are regulated and which investments. That is done in a separate statutory instrument — the Regulated Activities Order (RAO) 2001. A regulated activity is defined by a three-part test: (1) specified activity + (2) specified investment + (3) by way of business. The section also covers offences, defences, prohibition orders, and actions for damages. Key exam distinction: exemption (you CAN do the regulated activity without authorisation) vs exclusion (the activity is not treated as regulated at all). 2-3 exam questions.

6.1

General Prohibition — Section 19 FSMA

FSMA is subdivided into 30 parts. Part II (Regulated and Prohibited Activities) contains the general prohibition:

General Prohibition (Section 19 FSMA)
"No person may carry on a regulated activity in the UK, or purport to do so, unless they are either authorised (by the PRA and/or FCA) or exempt from the requirement to be authorised".

▸Sanctions for breach

Contravention is a criminal offence. Maximum sanctions:

  • Up to 2 years' imprisonment
  • Unlimited fine

▸Defence

It is a defence for a person to show that they took all reasonable precautions and exercised all due diligence to avoid committing the offence. This is a complete defence, not mitigation.

▸Unenforceability of Agreements

A further effect — agreements made are unenforceable by the offending person against the other party. This also applies to agreements made as a result of the offender's activities — even if the offender is not a party.

The other party is entitled to:

  • Recover any money or property transferred under the agreement
  • Be compensated for any loss suffered
Textbook example — X, ABC plc, UNA Ltd
X buys shares in ABC plc following a recommendation from brokers UNA Ltd. X discovers: UNA is not authorised under FSMA.

X has 2 choices:
• Keep the shares, take no action
• Sue UNA for recovery of her money and damages (handing back the shares)

Relevant UNA staff have also committed a criminal act — up to 2 years prison + unlimited fine.

▸Part 4A Permission

To be authorised, a person must first apply for permission to perform particular regulated activities. If the criteria are satisfied — the regulator grants Part 4A Permission.

6.1.1

Exempt Persons — 6 categories

Various FSMA provisions by which a person can be exempt from the general prohibition:

ProvisionExempt persons
Section 39 FSMA + Appointed Representatives Regs 2001Appointed Representatives (ARs)
Section 285 FSMARIEs (Recognised Investment Exchanges) + RCHs (Recognised Clearing Houses)
Section 312A FSMAOperators of multilateral trading systems exercising certain rights
Section 316 FSMAMembers of the Society of Lloyd's
FSMA Exemption Order 2001Central banks, supranational bodies, local authorities, charities, industrial/provident societies, government-overseen orgs
Part 20 FSMASpecial regime for professional firms (accountants, solicitors, actuaries) subject to conditions
Exemption vs Exclusion — critical distinction
Exemption → person CAN carry on regulated activity, but authorisation is not needed (ARs, RIEs).
Exclusion → activity falls OUTSIDE the definition of regulated activity entirely (intra-group, newspaper advice).

Fundamentally different. A frequent exam trap.
6.2

Prohibition Orders — Section 56 FSMA

Separately from the general prohibition, Section 56 FSMA gives regulators the power to make an order prohibiting an individual from performing a specified function.

▸Grounds

Either regulator may do this if it considers the individual not a fit and proper person. The order may relate to:

  • A particular regulated activity
  • Regulated activities generally (industry-wide ban)

▸Breach of prohibition order

Breach — criminal offence + fine
An individual who breaches the order → guilty of a criminal offence + liable to a fine. Defence — show they took all reasonable care and exercised due diligence.

▸Firm responsibility

An authorised person (firm) must take reasonable care to ensure that individuals for whom they are responsible do not breach prohibition orders.

Prohibition orders may also be made against exempt persons and exempt professional firms (Part 20 regime — professional firms undertake regulated activities under supervision of their professional body rather than direct FCA/PRA).

▸Variation / revocation

A person may apply to the FCA or PRA to have an order varied or revoked.

▸Section 59 — approval requirement

Section 59 FSMA
An authorised person must take reasonable care to ensure that no person performs a role requiring approval (SMF under SM&CR + controlled function under APER) unless they have received confirmation of approval.
6.2.1

Actions for Damages — Sections 71 + 138D

▸Section 71 FSMA — private person sues firm

Section 71 — a private person may sue a firm for damages if they suffer loss due to breach of statutory duty, when:

  • An individual in the firm carries out a function in breach of a Section 56 prohibition order, OR
  • An individual carries out a controlled function without prior approval (Section 59)

Section 71 also provides for non-private persons (businesses, trusts) — sue for damages as a consequence of negligence concerning prohibition orders, in prescribed circumstances.

▸Section 138D FS Act 2012 — breach of rule

Section 138D — a person suffering losses as a result of a breach of a rule by an authorised person has the right to claim damages.

The person needs to only show:

  • The authorised person breached a rule
  • They suffered a loss
Private right of action
Consumers can sue directly in the civil courts — no need to wait for FCA enforcement. A powerful enforcement mechanism for rules.
6.3

Regulated Activities — 3-Part Test

To know whether someone has breached the general prohibition, you need to know what regulated activities actually are. Defined in the Regulated Activities Order (RAO) 2001.

3 conditions for a regulated activity
An activity is a regulated activity if:
  1. It is an activity of a specified kind
  2. Carried on by way of business
  3. Relates to an investment of a specified kind
All 3 conditions must be met simultaneously.

The RAO defines regulated activities first by reference to specified investments, then to activities the firm may carry on in relation to those investments. An activity that is listed but carried out in relation to an asset that is not a specified investment is not a regulated activity.

6.3.1

Specified Investments — 26 types in RAO

1
Deposits — money paid from one person to another, repayable when a specified event occurs. Exclusions: e-money, prepayments for goods/services, security deposits.

Electronic money — monetary value stored on an electronic device, issued on receipt of funds, accepted as payment by third parties.
3
Rights under contracts of insurance — long-term (life assurance, endowment) + general (motor, buildings). FCA guidance in the PERG Sourcebook.
4
Shares — shares/stock in any company (wherever incorporated) or unincorporated body formed outside the UK. Exclusions: OEIC shares (separate CIS definition), some building society shares (captured as deposits).
5

6
Government and public securities — gilts, local authority bonds, similar issued by Government/public bodies.
7
Instruments giving entitlements to investments — warrants, rights to subscribe for shares/debentures.
8
Certificates representing certain securities — depository receipts (ADRs, GDRs).
9
Units in CIS — Collective Investment Schemes (OEICs, unit trusts).
10
Rights under stakeholder pension scheme
11
Rights under personal pension scheme
12
Options — calls and puts.
13
Futures — contracts for sale/purchase at a future date.
14
CFDs (Contracts for Differences) — including spread bets.
15
Lloyd's syndicate capacity and membership
16
Funeral plan contracts — pre-paid funeral arrangements.
17
Regulated mortgage contracts — residential mortgages.
18
Regulated home reversion, home purchase, home finance plans
19
Credit agreements — covered by CCA 1974 and FCA regulation.
20
Consumer hire agreements
21
Agreements for provision of credit information services
22
P2P agreements — peer-to-peer lending platforms.
23
Claims management services
24
Rights under pension benefits
25
Rights to, or interests in, other specified investments — rights in anything that is a specified investment (excluding home finance).
26
Emission allowances — EU ETS / UK ETS carbon credits.
NOT specified investments — common exam questions
  • Premium bonds (NS&I) — not an investment in the RAO sense
  • Government trade bills
  • Security deposits
  • Physical gold bullion (but gold derivatives — yes)
  • Residential property for personal use
  • Antiques / collectibles
  • Money paid in advance for goods/services

6.5

Main RAO Exclusions

A significant number of exclusions are provided in FSMA — those carrying on regulated activities falling entirely within an exclusion can conduct the activity without applying for authorisation.

▸(1) Absence of Holding Out

Dealing in investments as principal is a regulated activity. Persons dealing for themselves in the hope of making profits must be authorised/exempt. However, the activity is restricted to persons holding themselves out as market makers and regularly soliciting the public to induce dealing.

The exclusion applies if the counterparty is NOT:

  • An authorised person
  • An exempt person
  • An overseas person
  • An incoming electronic commerce provider

▸(2) Groups and Joint Enterprises

Firms undertaking intra-group dealing or joint enterprise relationships benefit from the exclusion of the following activities:

  • Dealing in investments as principal / agent
  • Arranging deals in investments
  • Managing investments
  • Safeguarding and administering investments
  • Sending dematerialised instructions
  • Advising on investments
Intra-group example
A parent holding company moves assets between subsidiary companies. There is no third party. This is not a regulated activity under the exclusion — even though technically it looks like "managing investments".

▸(3) Advice in Newspapers

A particular exclusion for advice in writing or other legible form, contained in:

  • A newspaper
  • A journal, magazine, periodical publication
  • A service of regularly updated news or information

The existence of money/city pages does not make the principal purpose of the paper anything other than the provision of news — authorisation is not required.

Exception — tip-sheets
If the principal purpose of the publication is the provision of investment advice, with a view to encouraging investors to undertake investment activity, authorisation IS required. This is the case for periodicals that tip certain investments and are often sold on a subscription basis.

▸(4) Trustees, Nominees, Personal Representatives

Exclusion if the person carrying on the regulated activity is:

  • Acting as representative of another party
  • Not generally holding themselves out as carrying on regulated activities
  • Not receiving additional remuneration for providing the services

Applies to:

  • Dealing in investments as principal
  • Arranging deals / making arrangements
  • Other ancillary activities

▸(5) Employee Share Schemes

An employer setting up an employee share scheme for its own employees (SAYE, SIP, etc.) does not carry on a regulated activity in respect of the scheme.

▸(6) Overseas Persons

An overseas person (no permanent establishment in the UK) dealing with or through UK-authorised persons does not carry on a regulated activity in the UK.

Overseas persons example
A US broker-dealer with no UK office accepts orders from a UK-authorised agent (e.g. Barclays UK). Under the overseas persons exclusion, the US broker does not need UK authorisation — the deal flows through the UK-authorised intermediary.

▸Part 20 Professional Firm restrictions

Part 20 FSMA enables professional firms (accountants, solicitors, actuaries) to undertake certain regulated activities under the supervision of their professional body rather than direct FCA authorisation. However:

  • Must return commissions from product providers to the client
  • Certain activities are prohibited:
    • Accepting deposits
    • Dealing in investments as principal (market maker)
    • Lending / administering home finance (except as trustee/personal rep)
    • Establishing / operating / winding up a CIS or stakeholder/personal pension scheme
    • Managing Lloyd's syndicate underwriting capacity
    • Effecting / carrying out contracts of insurance (including Lloyd's)
    • Providing funeral plan contracts
6.6

Threshold Conditions (TC) — Minimum Standards

Threshold Conditions (TCs) are the minimum standards expected of authorised persons — being authorised and remaining authorised. The FSMA (Threshold Conditions) Order 2013 changed and redistributed responsibility between the FCA and PRA. Some FCA TCs apply to all firms, including dual-regulated.

The latest TCs were issued in August 2023.

▸FCA assessments

  • Dual-regulated firms: FCA assesses only conduct issues; PRA — prudential
  • Solo-regulated firms: FCA assesses BOTH conduct and prudential

▸Business Model Threshold Condition

Business Model TC
The FCA expects firms to demonstrate an appropriate, viable, and sustainable business model, given the nature and scale of business activity they intend to carry out. It requires:
  • Adequate contingency planning
  • How the business model meets the needs of clients/customers — not placing them at undue risk
  • Not placing at risk the integrity of the wider financial system (financial crime)
  • Clear information — how the firm can evidence meeting the condition

The FCA has stated it will recommend refusal at an early stage if satisfied that a firm does not (or will not continue to) meet the minimum threshold standards. This acts as a catch-all provision + a good fall-back.

▸PRA Additional Conditions

Firms must meet BOTH PRA- and FCA-specific threshold conditions. PRA-specific TCs apply to banks, building societies, credit unions, designated investment firms that are regulated by the PRA for prudential purposes.

3-part test for regulated activity
(1) Specified activity +
(2) Specified investment +
(3) By way of business
= Regulated activity → need authorisation OR exemption
  • 6.1 General Prohibition (Section 19 FSMA): no person carries on a regulated activity in the UK unless authorised or exempt. Criminal offence — up to 2 years prison + unlimited fine.
  • Defence: reasonable precautions + due diligence (complete defence).
  • Unenforceability: agreements by an unauthorised firm are unenforceable by the offender. The other party recovers money/property + compensation.
  • Part 4A Permission — application to PRA/FCA for authorisation.
  • 6 Exempt Person categories:
    • Section 39 — ARs
    • Section 285 — RIEs + RCHs
    • Section 312A — MTFs
    • Section 316 — Lloyd's members
    • FSMA Exemption Order 2001 — central banks, supranational, local authorities, charities
    • Part 20 — professional firms (accountants, solicitors, actuaries)
  • Exemption vs Exclusion:
    • Exemption = activity IS regulated, but the person does not need authorisation
    • Exclusion = activity falls OUTSIDE the definition of regulated activity
  • 6.2 Prohibition Orders (Section 56): FCA/PRA may prohibit an individual (if not fit and proper) from performing a specified function. Breach = criminal offence + fine. Also applies to exempt persons and Part 20 firms. Section 59 — firms must ensure only approved persons perform SMFs.
  • Actions for Damages:
    • Section 71 FSMA — sue the firm if breach of statutory duty (prohibition order / Section 59)
    • Section 138D FS Act 2012 — right to claim damages for breach of rule (just show breach + loss)
  • 6.3 Regulated Activities: defined in RAO 2001. 3 conditions simultaneously: specified activity + specified investment + by way of business.
  • 26 Specified Investments: deposits, e-money, insurance contracts, shares, debentures, government securities, warrants, CIS units, pension rights, options, futures, CFDs, Lloyd's capacity, funeral plans, mortgages, P2P, claims management, emission allowances, etc.
  • NOT specified: premium bonds (NS&I), government trade bills, security deposits, physical gold, residential property (personal use), antiques.
  • 19+ Specified Activities: accepting deposits / e-money / insurance / dealing (principal/agent) / arranging / managing / safeguarding (custody) / CREST / CIS / UCITS/AIFs / pension schemes / advising / mortgages / insurance distribution / debt counselling+collecting+administration / P2P operation.
  • 6.5 Main RAO Exclusions (6):
    • (1) Absence of holding out — dealing own account without public solicitation
    • (2) Groups and joint enterprises — intra-group transactions
    • (3) Advice in newspapers — if the principal purpose = news (NOT tip-sheets)
    • (4) Trustees, nominees, personal representatives — representative capacity, no holding out, no additional remuneration
    • (5) Employee share schemes — employer for own employees
    • (6) Overseas persons — dealing through UK-authorised intermediaries
  • Part 20 Professional Firms — accountants/solicitors/actuaries under the supervision of their professional body. Must return commissions. Certain activities prohibited (deposits, market making, CIS, insurance, pensions, funeral plans).
  • 6.6 Threshold Conditions (TC): minimum standards — latest August 2023. Dual-regulated: FCA=conduct, PRA=prudential. Solo: FCA=both. Business Model TC — appropriate, viable, sustainable model + contingency planning.

Chapter 1, Section 1 — Detailed Deep Dive
The Regulatory Environment · Syllabus 1.1 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 1 is the foundation of the entire syllabus. It describes how the UK financial regulation system is set up: who the main players are (FCA and PRA), what law underpins their work (FSMA 2000 as amended by FSA 2012 and FSMA 2023), what statutory objectives each of them has, how they divide firms between themselves on the "twin peaks" principle, where they get their money, and to whom they report. These are "where the regulator sits on the map" questions — ~3-4 exam questions — but without grasping them, the later chapters do not fit into a coherent picture.

1.1

FSMA 2000 and FS Act 2012 — the legal framework

▸What FSMA is

The Financial Services and Markets Act (FSMA) 2000 is the primary legislation regulating financial services in the UK. It came into effect in 2001 and has been amended many times since. Its main purpose is to provide stronger protection for consumers than existed under the previous framework (where the industry was largely self-regulated).

What "person" means in FSMA
FSMA applies to all "persons" in the UK. In the FSMA context, "person" means:
  • Natural persons (individuals)
  • All types of legal person: firms, incorporated bodies, partnerships, trusts, and other unincorporated associations
The term is as wide as possible — it is not just companies.

▸General prohibition — Section 19 FSMA

It is a criminal offence to carry on regulated activity by way of business in the UK unless a person is either authorised or an exempt person. This is called the "general prohibition" and is set out in Section 19 FSMA.

A criminal offence — this is serious
A breach of the general prohibition is not an administrative infringement but a criminal offence. In addition, contracts entered into by an unauthorised firm with a client are unenforceable against the client — the client can walk away; the firm cannot compel performance.

▸FS Act 2012 — transition to twin peaks

In 2012, FSMA was significantly amended by the Financial Services Act 2012, which came into effect in April 2013. Before that, there was a single regulator — the Financial Services Authority (FSA). The 2007-2008 crisis showed that a monolithic regulator struggles: conduct issues (mis-selling of products) and prudential issues (banks with insufficient capital) require different expertise.

FS Act 2012 split the FSA into three parts:

  • Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) — conduct regulator + prudential for non-PRA firms
  • Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA) — prudential for the largest firms (part of the Bank of England from 2016)
  • Financial Policy Committee (FPC) — macro-prudential / systemic risks, part of the Bank of England

▸FS Act 2023 — post-Brexit reform

In June 2023, the Financial Services and Markets Act 2023 received Royal Assent. Certain provisions came into effect in August 2023, the rest — on 1 January 2024. This Act:

  • Starts the process of revoking retained EU law in financial services
  • Gives powers to HM Treasury, PRA, and FCA to replace EU legislation with UK-specific rules designed for UK markets
  • Enhances the UK regulatory regime — updates the objectives of the PRA and FCA, adding greater focus on long-term growth and international competitiveness (a new secondary objective)
  • Implements the "Smarter Regulatory Framework" — a general reform

Revoking EU laws is a lengthy process, which the PRA and FCA are undertaking via a phased implementation approach.

1.1.1

Twin Peaks — the split into FCA and PRA

The UK regulatory model is known as the "twin peaks" approach. The logic: for deposit takers, insurers, and investment banks, two different supervisors — one focuses on prudential issues (PRA), the other on conduct and market-related issues (FCA).

But the FCA is also responsible for prudential regulation of all firms not regulated by the PRA (the so-called solo-regulated firms — asset managers, stockbrokers).

▸FCA — role and scope

The FCA is responsible for:

  • Protecting consumers of financial services
  • Keeping the industry stable
  • Promoting healthy competition between financial services providers
  • Solely responsible for authorisation and supervision of all financial institutions not regulated by the PRA — including intermediaries and investment exchanges
  • Powers to investigate and prosecute insider dealing and market abuse
  • Acts as the Listing Authority for the UK (UKLA)
  • Oversees the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS)
  • Jointly with the PRA, oversees the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS)

▸PRA — role and scope

The PRA's focus is prudential regulation of banks, building societies, credit unions, insurers, and major investment firms. The goal: to promote the safety and soundness of these firms, which in turn reduces harm to the UK financial system.

After the Financial Services Act 2016, the PRA became part of the Bank of England — micro-prudential regulation of institutions was brought within the BoE's scope via the establishment of the Prudential Regulation Committee (PRC).

▸FCA — financial and organisational independence

Both authorities — FCA and PRA — are funded entirely from fees paid by the firms they regulate. They set the fees payable by regulated firms and make rules in respect of the activities under their remit. This secures operational independence from government funding.

FCA — a private company
The FCA is a private company with a special privilege that allows it not to use the word "limited" in its name. The FCA is accountable to the UK Government for how it performs its functions — via HM Treasury. The PRA, by contrast, is not directly accountable to any UK Government body, but is answerable to Parliament via the House of Commons Treasury Committee through the Bank of England.
Examples of how firms split
Barclays Bank: deposit-taker + investment bank → dual-regulated. The PRA checks capital adequacy; the FCA supervises how Barclays sells products to retail clients.
Schroders (asset manager): not a deposit-taker, not an insurer → solo-regulated by the FCA (both conduct and prudential).
AXA UK (insurer): an insurance company → dual-regulated. The PRA checks solvency ratios; the FCA monitors how AXA sells policies.
A small IFA (10 staff): solo-regulated by the FCA.
1.2

Statutory Objectives — FCA and PRA

Statutory objectives are goals set by law (FSMA, as amended) that the regulator must advance through its work. They are not abstract mottos — they are legally binding objectives against which the regulator reports and can be challenged in the courts.

1.2.1

FCA — 1 Strategic + 3 Operational Objectives

The FCA has a single strategic objective and three operational objectives, set out in the Financial Services Act 2012.

SECONDARY (from FSMA 2023)

▸Strategic objective

FCA strategic objective
"Ensuring that the relevant markets function well" — ensure relevant markets operate well.

▸Operational objective 1: Consumer Protection

The consumer protection objective — "securing an appropriate degree of protection for consumers" (ensuring an appropriate degree of protection for consumers).

The key word is "appropriate". This does not mean preventing all risk of loss to consumers. When considering what degree of protection is "appropriate", the FCA must have regard to:

  • Different degrees of risk involved in different kinds of investment
  • Differing levels of experience and expertise of consumers
  • The needs of consumers for advice and accurate information
  • The general principle that consumers should take some responsibility for their decisions
A common exam mistake
The answer "FCA must prevent all risk of loss to consumers" is wrong. Consumer protection is an appropriate degree, not absolute protection. Consumers share responsibility for their decisions.

▸Operational objective 2: Integrity

The integrity objective — "protecting and enhancing the integrity of the UK financial system". The integrity of the financial system includes 6 elements:

  1. Soundness, stability, and resilience
  2. Not being used for a purpose connected with financial crime
  3. Not being affected by behaviour amounting to market abuse
  4. Orderly operation of financial markets
  5. Reliability and transparency of the price-formation process in those markets
  6. Suitability of the listing rules

▸Operational objective 3: Competition

The competition objective — "promoting effective competition in the interests of consumers" in the markets for regulated financial services or services provided by a RIE when carrying out regulated activities in respect of which it is exempt from the general prohibition.

The FCA may have regard to the following matters:

  • The needs of different consumers who use or may use these services — including their need for information enabling informed choices
  • The ease with which consumers can change the person from whom they obtain services (switching)
  • The ease with which new entrants can enter the market
  • How far competition encourages innovation
Competition objective in action
The FCA launches the Open Banking initiative — requiring the big-9 banks to share account data with fintech start-ups via APIs. This promotes competition: eases market entry for new entrants (fintechs) + eases consumer switching to better products + encourages innovation. A direct advance of the competition objective.
1.2.2

PRA — Safety and Soundness + Insurance Objective

▸General objective

PRA General Objective
"Promoting the safety and soundness of PRA-authorised persons" — promoting the safety and soundness of PRA-authorised persons.

This general objective is advanced primarily through:

  1. Seeking to ensure that the business of PRA-authorised persons is carried on in a way that avoids adverse effects on the stability of the UK financial system
  2. Seeking to minimise the adverse effect the failure of a PRA-authorised person could have on the stability of the UK financial system

▸Insurance objective

The PRA also has an insurance objective, relating only to effecting or carrying out contracts of insurance as principal — to the extent that is a PRA-regulated activity. The objective: "contributing to securing an appropriate degree of protection for those who are or may become policyholders".

Why insurance is separate
Insurers differ from banks. Bank failure is an immediate stress for depositors. Insurer failure is slower — but with much longer tails (life insurance may run 40-50 years after policy inception). Hence the PRA has a separate insurance objective — not "safety and soundness" but protection of policyholders.
1.2.3

Secondary Objective (FSMA 2023) — International Competitiveness

Following the coming into force of FSMA 2023, the PRA and FCA have been given a new secondary objective:

Secondary Objective
"Facilitate the international competitiveness of the UK economy and its medium-to-long-term growth (subject to international standards)" — to facilitate the international competitiveness of the UK economy and its medium-to-long-term growth (subject to international standards).

▸What "secondary" means

The FCA has specifically stated that the new objective will apply only when the FCA is advancing its existing operational objectives (consumer protection, market integrity, competition). In other words, the secondary objective does not override the primary ones. If an action improves international competitiveness but harms consumer protection — it is not taken.

▸The "international standards" safeguard

The phrase "subject to international standards" means the FCA will not take action or develop rules that harm the UK's reputation or standing in the international financial community. I.e. "competitiveness" cannot mean a race to the bottom — lowering regulatory standards for competitiveness's sake.

Secondary objective in application
The FCA considers a rule that might slightly improve consumer protection, but creates gold-plating over EU standards and makes the UK less attractive for international firms. Under the secondary objective, the FCA may not adopt the rule if it maintains adequate protection without unnecessary burden.
1.2.4

Accountability — 4 Mechanisms

The FCA's objectives also provide a degree of accountability. Four mechanisms:

  1. Annual report — the FCA's annual report to the Treasury contains an assessment of how well the FCA has met its statutory objectives. The report is scrutinised by various Parliamentary committees.
  2. Rules — the FCA must show how the rules it makes relate to its statutory objectives. You cannot just issue a rule without justification.
  3. Judicial review — if the FCA fails to consider the objectives or incorrectly interprets them, it can be challenged in the courts.
  4. Regulatory failure — the FCA and PRA will be held accountable if breaches of the objectives occur, or are made worse, because of a serious failure on the part of the regulator.
Despite 4 mechanisms, the FCA remains operationally independent
The FCA reports but is operationally independent of government. The Treasury cannot dictate policy or intervene in individual decisions. This independence is underpinned by independent funding (fees from firms, not taxpayers).
1.2.5

Supervision — how the regulator watches

Under FSMA, the relevant regulator (FCA and/or PRA) is required to maintain arrangements to supervise compliance with requirements imposed on authorised persons. The supervisory approach must ensure that the statutory objectives are met.

The FCA's supervisory efforts focus on:

  • The conduct of BOTH FCA-authorised and PRA-authorised firms
  • Plus the prudential regulation of authorised firms not directly authorised by the PRA

The PRA supervises the prudential aspects of firms it directly authorises.

▸FCA Mission: Approach to Supervision (2019)

In 2019 the FCA published "FCA Mission: Approach to Supervision", outlining eight supervisory principles and a supervisory model based on 3 types of work:

TypeWhat it does
ProactivePre-emptive identification of harm through review and assessment of firms and portfolios. Includes business-model analysis and review of culture drivers
ReactiveDealing with issues emerging or already arisen — to prevent harm growing
ThematicWider diagnostic or remedy work where actual or potential harm cuts across multiple firms

▸Conduct Risk and FTOC

The FCA does not provide a precise definition of "conduct risk". Broadly speaking, conduct risk is the risks posed to customers and the wider integrity of financial markets by the way authorised firms and their staff conduct themselves, and the risks that firms' behaviours result in poor outcomes for the consumer.

The concept of Treating Customers Fairly (TCF) and its 6 principles was introduced as an initiative in 2006. TCF has since been renamed "Fair Treatment of Customers" (FTOC) — setting out consumer outcomes the FCA considers core to what it expects of firms.

1.3

Rule-Making Powers — Part 9A FSMA

▸Source of powers

Part 9A of FSMA empowers the FCA to make rules that are legally binding on authorised firms concerning regulated activity — and also activity that is not regulated. Such rules are necessary or expedient for the FCA for the purpose of advancing one or more of its operational objectives.

The PRA has similar rule-making powers; however, its powers extend only to PRA-authorised persons.

▸Other powers under FSMA

Besides rule-making, FSMA empowers either regulator to:

  • Grant authorisation to persons applying for Part 4A permission, vary a firm's permission, and cancel authorisation
  • Supervise authorised persons on an ongoing basis to ensure they continue to meet authorisation requirements and comply with Handbook rules and other regulatory obligations
  • Employ a range of disciplinary measures and sanctions — to punish or limit activities of firms and senior management that fail to comply
  • Enforce the regulatory framework — the general approach is "credible deterrence", using enforcement strategy as a tool to change behaviour in the industry
1.3.1

FCA Handbook — Rules, Guidance, and Evidential Provisions

The FCA Handbook is the document containing the rules, guidance, and other provisions with which authorised firms must comply. The Handbook is divided into a number of separate Sourcebooks and manuals covering different topics (COBS, SYSC, CASS, MAR, PRIN, ENF, DEC, etc.).

▸Three types of provision — an important distinction

Provision typeBinding?What it is
Rules Binding Legally binding — the firm must comply. Breach → disciplinary action
Guidance Not binding Indicates what the FCA considers good practice. Not mandatory — but useful as evidence of compliance
Evidential provisions Not binding in its own right "Safe harbour" — compliance with it tends to establish compliance with the linked rule (and vice versa for contravention)
Evidential provision — how it works
Rule: "The firm must take reasonable steps to prevent financial crime". Evidential provision: "Having an annual AML training programme". This is evidential — you are not required to have exactly that training. But if the firm does have it — this tends to establish that the firm took reasonable steps. If the firm has no training programme at all — this tends to establish contravention of the rule.

▸Other material types in the Handbook

  • Directions and requirements — specific directions to a firm
  • UK legislative material — related statutes (FSMA sections, Regulations, etc.)
  • Statutory notices — forms of formal notices for enforcement

▸Regulatory Decisions Committee (RDC)

The RDC is an independent FCA committee that takes contested statutory decisions, including:

  • Refusing Part 4A permission applications
  • Cancelling existing permissions
  • Enforcement decisions

The RDC is operationally separate from FCA supervisory and enforcement staff — this ensures decision-making independence.

Super-complaints — another FCA feature
Under the Financial Services Act 2012, certain designated consumer bodies (e.g. Citizens Advice, Consumers' Association) can alert the FCA via a super-complaint — when they believe a market feature harms consumer interests. The FCA must respond publicly within 90 days. This is a mechanism for consumer voice in the regulatory process.

CHAPTER 1
Chapter 1, Section 4 — Detailed Deep Dive
Prudential Standards · Syllabus 1.4 · CISI V33
What is this section about?
Section 4 is about the financial soundness of firms. If conduct rules (COBS, Principles) are "how firms behave", prudential rules are "how much capital and liquidity a firm must hold to meet its obligations". The logic since 2008: a firm without an adequate buffer collapses and drags its clients down with it. Prudential is split into three sourcebooks depending on what the firm does: GENPRU (banks), MIFIDPRU (MiFID investment firms), and IPRU-INV (everyone else). The central new development is IFPR (Investment Firms Prudential Regime), effective from January 2022: a radical shift from "risks TO the firm" → "harm the firm MAY CAUSE to consumers and markets". On the exam 1-2 questions.

4.1

Capital Adequacy — the foundation of prudential

Principle 4 of the Principles for Businesses states:

Principle 4 — Financial Prudence
"A firm must maintain adequate financial resources".

▸Financial resources — what they are

Financial resources include both:

  • Capital resources — the firm's permanent funding base (shares, reserves, subordinated debt)
  • Liquidity resources — the firm's ability to meet short-term obligations as they fall due (cash, readily realisable assets)

The goal: the firm can meet its liabilities as they fall due.

▸Why standards differ

Standards differ according to:

  • The activities undertaken by the firm
  • Whether the firm's activities fall within the scope of MiFID
Why not one rule for all
A bank and an IFA have radically different risk profiles. A bank takes retail deposits — failure leads to immediate mass-consumer harm. An IFA gives advice — its failure will not wipe out the sector. Applying identical capital requirements would be: (1) insufficient for banks, (2) overkill for advisors. Hence 3 different sourcebooks.
4.1.1

Three Sourcebooks — who falls under which

SourcebookWhoRegulator
GENPRU
(General Prudential Sourcebook)		 Firms subject to UK CRD — banks / credit institutions / deposit-taking institutions PRA
MIFIDPRU
(Prudential Sourcebook for UK MiFID Investment Firms)		 UK-regulated firms subject to MiFID — not banks. In force from 1 January 2022 FCA
IPRU-INV
(Interim Prudential Sourcebook for Investment Businesses)		 Investment firms not subject to CRD/CRR or MIFIDPRU FCA
Decision-tree logic
If firm = bank → GENPRU (PRA).
If firm = MiFID investment firm (not a bank) → MIFIDPRU (FCA, since 2022).
If firm = neither of the above (MiFID-exempt, personal investment firm, etc.) → IPRU-INV (FCA).
It is the catch-all: "not CRD, not MIFIDPRU → IPRU-INV".

▸IPRU-INV — for which firms

IPRU-INV applies to:

  • Authorised professional firms
  • Certain types of securities and futures firms not directly subject to MiFID
  • Lloyd's underwriting agents and members' advisers
  • Fund managers — including operators of personal pension schemes and SIPP operators
  • Exempt CAD firms or local firms with limited MiFID business or passporting rights
  • Service companies
  • Personal investment firms operating under a MiFID exemption — e.g. financial advisory firms
  • Credit unions providing Junior ISAs
Practical classification examples
Barclays Bank — deposit-taker → GENPRU (PRA).
Schroders Investment Management — MiFID investment firm → MIFIDPRU (FCA).
Small IFA (MiFID exemption) — personal investment firm → IPRU-INV (FCA).
Lloyd's underwriting agent → IPRU-INV (FCA).

▸Activity-dependent IPRU-INV

IPRU-INV — rules depend on activity
The way IPRU-INV applies depends on the activity conducted by the firm. Consequently, if the firm's activity changes, this may mean the firm must comply with different aspects of IPRU-INV. It is not uniform — each type of firm has prescribed capital adequacy requirements tailored to its activities.
4.1.2

CRD + CRR — the framework for banks

▸What CRD/CRR is

The Capital Requirements Directive (CRD) and the Capital Requirements Regulations (CRR) apply to credit institutions (banks / deposit-taking institutions), managed by the PRA. The body of text previously developed while the UK was in the EU was retained following Brexit.

CRD and CRR created a common framework for financial resources and risk management — minimising the risk of a firm's financial failure.

Origin — Basel III
CRD/CRR implement Basel III international standards (Basel Committee on Banking Supervision). After the 2008 crisis these standards were significantly tightened. The UK retained them post-Brexit, but now has the power to diverge from EU interpretation.
4.1.3

3 Pillars of the CRD Framework

The CRD framework is based on the concept of three pillars:

PillarWhat it is
Pillar 1 Minimum capital requirements for credit, market, and operational risks. Quantitative — a specific minimum the firm must hold.
Pillar 2 Firms and supervisors decide whether additional capital should be held to cover risks not covered in Pillar 1. Qualitative — firm-specific assessment.
Pillar 3 Disclosure of information about risks, capital, and risk management — to improve market discipline.

▸Reporting requirements

Firms are required to provide detailed reports to the relevant regulator on capital adequacy provisions. The frequency of reporting depends on:

  • The size of the firm
  • Whether it is subject to UK CRD requirements or not
Immediate notification on breach
Additionally — if at any time a firm knows that its capital resources have fallen below the prescribed minimum, it must notify the regulator (PRA) IMMEDIATELY. No grace period, no waiting, no alternative — notification must be immediate.
4.1.4

Tiers of Capital — quality matters

Firms subject to UK CRD requirements must also comply with provisions relating to the quality of capital held. A firm must consider the loss absorbency of instruments it wishes to use to meet capital resource requirements.

Capital is broadly split into three tiers:

TierExamplesLoss absorbency
Core Tier 1 Permanent share capital, reserves, externally verified interim profits Highest (absorbs losses first)
Tier 2 Long-term subordinated debt, revaluation reserves Medium
Tier 3 Short-term subordinated debt, interim trading book P&L Lowest

▸The logic of tiers

Permanent instruments (share capital) — first to absorb losses and cannot be withdrawn. Long-term debt — medium: maturity far enough away but not permanent. Short-term debt — can be paid off quickly, less reliable in stress scenarios.

Exam example — tiers
Permanent share capital → Core T1 (highest loss absorbency).
Long-term subordinated debt (10 years) → T2 (medium).
Short-term subordinated debt (2 years) → T3 (lowest).

Maturity matters: same instrument type, different maturity → different tier.

▸Goodwill — deducted, not capital

Goodwill is not counted as capital
Intangibles (goodwill, deferred tax assets) are not counted AS capital — they are deducted FROM capital. The logic: goodwill does not generate cash, does not absorb losses in stress.
4.2

IFPR — Investment Firms Prudential Regime

▸IFPR background

The Investment Firms Prudential Regime (IFPR) is a new regime for FCA solo-regulated MiFID investment firms. Contained in the FCA's MIFIDPRU Sourcebook.

▸Timeline

DateEvent
2021The EU's Investment Firms Directive (IFD) and Investment Firms Regulation (IFR) came into effect in the EU
1 January 2022UK IFPR came into effect in the MIFIDPRU Sourcebook — with UK-specific adjustments

▸The philosophical shift — fundamental

Key paradigm shift
Old approach (CRD): focused on "risks firms face" — what could happen to the firm?
IFPR (new): focuses on "harm firms may cause others" — what damage can the firm inflict on consumers and markets?

This is a radical shift. Before IFPR, a firm with £100M AUM had the same requirements as a firm with £10bn — both applied similar calculations. Under IFPR requirements scale with the potential harm from activities.

▸Aim of IFPR

The aim of IFPR is to simplify requirements for FCA solo-regulated MiFID investment firms, refocusing prudential requirements and expectations away from the risks firms face — towards mitigating the potential for harm the firm can pose to consumers and markets.

▸IFPR approach

The approach under IFPR (as contained in MIFIDPRU):

  • Consider the harm a MiFID investment firm can cause to others, based on the activities carried out
  • The amount of capital and liquid assets held is sufficient for an orderly wind down
Orderly wind down — the concept
IFPR is not about preventing firm failure absolutely — it is about ensuring that if a firm fails, it can wind down in an orderly manner. This means: enough capital/liquidity so the firm can return client assets, close positions, pay employees and exit the market without disruption to consumers or markets. A "controlled death" rather than chaotic collapse.
4.2.1

2 Overarching Requirements — key obligations

Each type of firm has prescribed capital adequacy requirements, and all firms must comply with 2 overarching requirements:

  1. Firms must AT ALL TIMES have available the amount and type of financial resources required by the regulator's rules.
  2. Firms must notify the relevant regulator IMMEDIATELY if the firm becomes aware it is in breach or expects to be in breach of the relevant financial resources rule.
Exam points
"At all times" — not quarterly, not yearly, not "after the first year" — continuously.
"Immediately" — not "within 5 days", not "next quarterly report" — right away.
"Expects to be in breach" — notification is required even before actual breach, if anticipated.
4.2.2

4 FCA Outcomes of IFPR

The FCA identified the following outcomes of IFPR:

  1. A prudential regime aligned to the way investment firms run their business:
    • Taking into account the different business models of FCA investment firms
    • Better protect consumers and markets from the harm those firms may pose
  2. FCA investment firms subject to MEANINGFUL and CONSISTENT prudential requirements
  3. FCA investment firms spend LESS TIME on complex capital requirement calculations that do little to help manage risk:
    • Allowing management more time to focus on running the business, while managing and mitigating harm and risk
  4. Prudential rules understandable and accessible — with most rules brought into a new single prudential sourcebook (MIFIDPRU)
Before IFPR — fragmentation
Before IFPR, prudential rules for MiFID investment firms were scattered across many sourcebooks — GENPRU, BIPRU, IPRU-INV, parts of SYSC. Firms spent huge resources just navigating the rules. IFPR consolidated most rules in one place (MIFIDPRU) — outcome 4.

▸IFPR vs old regime — an example

Small boutique advisory firm (£50M AUM)
Old (pre-2022): applied complex CRD calculations designed for banks. Spending £50K/year on compliance. The calculations gave little insight into actual harm the firm could cause.
IFPR (post-2022): simplified calculations scaled to activities. Focus on the orderly wind down scenario — how much capital is needed so the firm can return client assets and close shop cleanly. Compliance cost dropped; focus shifted from paperwork to actual risk management.
